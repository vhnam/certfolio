## Organizational Awareness: Understanding the Design Environment

### Design Does Not Operate in Isolation

Designers must first understand the **organizational context** in which they work.

- Org charts: Reveal hierarchy, decision power, and potential blockers.
- Cross-department relationships: Identify allies such as Sales, Marketing, and Customer Support.
- Internal dependencies: Show how design decisions ripple across departments.

This perspective prevents designers from assuming design is the center of the organization.

### The Satellite Model of Design

Design is **one satellite within a larger business ecosystem**.

Other key departments often include:

- Sales
- Marketing
- Finance
- Engineering
- Customer Support

Each department is measured through **clear KPIs**, which means design must also demonstrate measurable impact.

### The “Big Five” Business Priorities

Most businesses prioritize five core outcomes:

- Increasing revenue
- Decreasing costs
- Increasing new business (acquisition)
- Increasing existing business (retention and upsells)
- Increasing shareholder value

Design contributes most directly through:

- Improving efficiency
- Reducing operational costs
- Increasing user success and satisfaction

## Navigating the Reality of Product Development

### The Reverse Double Diamond

The ideal design process:

1. Discover the problem
2. Define the problem
3. Design solutions
4. Deliver solutions

In reality, organizations often start with a **predefined solution**.

Example:

- Stakeholders request a feature before research is done.

### A Practical Approach

Instead of resisting this reality, designers can:

1. Accept the requested feature.
2. Define success metrics immediately.
3. Launch a minimal version (MVP).
4. Collect usage insights.
5. Refine the problem using real data.

This approach turns forced solutions into **learning opportunities**.

## Connecting Business Goals to Design Work

### The Problem with Business OKRs

Organizations often define goals using **OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)**.

Example objective:

- Reduce the time from free user → premium subscriber.

Designers often struggle to translate this into **specific design actions**.

### The KPI Tree Framework

A **Design KPI Tree** connects strategy with implementation.

Structure:

```
Business Objectives
       ↓
Design Outcomes
       ↓
Design Initiatives
       ↓
Metrics
```

This framework creates a direct connection between **design decisions and business impact**.

### Components of a KPI Tree

#### Business OKRs (Root)

High-level company goals.

Example:

- Reduce free-to-premium conversion time from 42 days to 21 days.

#### Design Outcomes (Branches)

Behavioral or experience changes that design can influence.

Examples:

- Reduce time to first success moment
- Increase feature discovery
- Improve filter usage

#### Design Initiatives (Leaves)

Concrete design actions.

Examples:

- Enable bulk portfolio import
- Improve search filters
- Add preview access to premium insights

#### Metrics (Measurement Layer)

Every initiative must include:

- A measurable number
- A timeframe

Example:

- 75% success rate for portfolio imports by Q3.

### Collaborative Creation

KPI trees should be built with:

- Product managers
- Developers
- Designers
- Sales teams

Benefits:

- Shared ownership
- Early feasibility validation
- Stronger alignment across departments

## Redefining Design Metrics

### The Problem with Vanity Metrics

Traditional web analytics can be misleading:

- Bounce rate
- Session duration
- Page views

These metrics lack context and may not reflect real user success.

Example:

A long session duration could mean:

- High engagement
- Confusing navigation

### The Core UX Performance Metrics

#### Task Success Rate

The most important design metric.

Measures:

- Whether users achieve their intended goal.

Examples:

- Successfully completing a purchase
- Finding a specific report
- Submitting a form correctly

#### Time on Task

Measures efficiency.

Example:

- Complex date picker → 53 seconds
- Simple numeric input → 5 seconds

The faster path indicates a better design.

#### Error Rate

Tracks:

- Frequency of user mistakes
- Ease of error recovery

This is particularly important in:

- Forms
- Filters
- Complex workflows

#### Search Quality Score

Measures the overlap between:

- Results returned by the system
- Results experts believe users should see

Method:

1. Analyze top 100 search queries.
2. Ask domain experts what results should appear.
3. Compare expected results vs actual results.

Higher overlap = better search experience.

#### Frustration Score

Measures signals of user frustration:

- Rage clicks
- Repeated failed attempts
- Dead-end pages
- Confusing navigation loops

These often represent **low-effort improvements with high impact**.

## Prioritizing Design Work with the Kano Model

### Why Feature Prioritization Matters

Not all features create equal value.

The Kano Model categorizes features by **user perception**.

### Kano Categories

#### Must-Be Features

Basic expectations.

Examples:

- Login works
- Search returns results

Users rarely notice when they exist but are extremely frustrated when missing.

#### Performance Features

Features where improvement directly increases satisfaction.

Examples:

- Faster loading
- More accurate search

The better these features perform, the happier users become.

#### Attractive Features (Delighters)

Unexpected features that create loyalty.

Example:

Exceptional customer service experiences like Nordstrom’s well-known service stories.

#### Indifferent Features

Features users do not care about.

These should often be **removed or deprioritized** to reduce development effort.

## Measuring Long-Term Design Impact

### The Task Performance Indicator (TPI)

TPI is a framework for **long-term UX measurement**.

Core metrics:

- Task success rate
- Time on task

### Establishing a Baseline

Steps:

1. Recruit 15–18 participants.
2. Define representative tasks.
3. Record task success and completion time.
4. Calculate baseline performance.

This baseline becomes the reference point for future improvements.

### The No “Think Aloud” Rule

Unlike traditional usability testing:

- Participants should not explain their thinking during tasks.

Reason:

- Speaking slows them down.
- It distorts the **Time on Task metric**.

### Designing Representative Tasks

Tasks must be:

- Specific
- Measurable
- Realistic

Example:

“Find a hybrid car under £300/month with 10k annual mileage.”

Each task should have **one clear correct answer**.

### Continuous Testing

Repeat the same test:

- Every 6–12 months
- With the same user segments

Expected improvements:

- Higher success rates
- Shorter completion times

This provides **clear evidence of UX impact over time**.

## Demonstrating ROI of Design

### Translating Design Work into Business Value

Design impact becomes clear when framed through business outcomes.

Examples:

Instead of saying:

- “The interface is cleaner.”

Say:

- “Task completion improved by 30%.”
- “User errors decreased by 40%.”
- “Conversion time decreased by 21 days.”

### The Evidence Loop

Design ROI can be demonstrated through a repeatable cycle:

1. Define business objective
2. Build KPI tree
3. Implement design initiatives
4. Measure performance metrics
5. Compare against baseline

This creates **objective evidence of design value**.

## Practical Strategies When Research Is Limited

If direct access to users is unavailable, designers can still gather insights.

Possible sources:

- Sales conversations
- Customer support tickets
- Public product reviews
- Internal team members unfamiliar with the product
- Remote usability testing platforms

Even limited data can establish **an initial baseline**.

## Core Principles for Design Teams

### Speak the Language of Business

Design must connect to:

- Revenue
- Cost reduction
- Customer retention
- Operational efficiency

### Collaborate Across Teams

Design should be aligned with:

- Product strategy
- Engineering feasibility
- Sales insights

### Measure Before and After

Always track:

- Baseline performance
- Post-design improvements

### Focus on Real Outcomes

Design success is not:

- “The UI looks better.”

Design success is:

- Faster task completion
- Higher success rates
- Reduced user frustration
- Improved business performance
