## Foundations of Behavioral Design

### Definition

- Behavioral design: Applying insights from psychology, neuroscience, and behavioral economics to influence user behavior.
- Goal: Create products that engage, encourage, and persuade users effectively.

### Key Disciplines

- Behavioral economics: Studies decision-making using economic + psychological models.
- Psychology: Observes human behavior and mental processes.
- Neuroscience: Explains how the brain processes information and triggers actions.

## Attention & Perception

### Pre-attentive Processing

- Pre-attentive visual system: Detects patterns instantly before conscious attention.
- Designers can guide attention using:
  - Color contrast
  - Shape differences
  - Positioning

### Example Insight

- Searching visually is slow without cues.
- Highlighting (e.g., color) makes targets instantly noticeable.

## Mental Models & Learning

### Definitions

- Mental model: Internal representation of how something works.
- Default behavior: Users apply existing mental models to new experiences.

### Design Implications

- Familiar design → faster understanding.
- Novel design → forces users to rethink and learn from scratch.

### Example

- iPhone design: Broke existing phone conventions to avoid old mental models and create a new interaction paradigm.

## Decision-Making & Confidence

### Definitions

- Confidence: The key trigger that enables users to make decisions.
- Decision paralysis: Inability to choose due to uncertainty or overload.

### Key Insights

- Decisions are not purely rational—they depend on perceived confidence.
- Confidence can come from:
  - Research
  - Social proof (e.g., trusted recommendations)
  - Simplicity of options

### Time Factor

- Long decision time → reduced likelihood of action.
- The brain monitors time and discourages prolonged decisions.

## Choice Architecture

### The Paradox of Choice

- More options:
  - Attract users
  - Increase exploration
- Too many options:
  - Reduce confidence
  - Cause decision paralysis

### Key Principle

- “If you want action, reduce choices.”

### Supporting Research

- “Tyranny of choice”: People enjoy many options but struggle to choose among them.

### Practical Pattern

- Provide many options → satisfy exploration
- Highlight one recommendation → enable decision

#### Example (E-commerce)

- Show full catalog
- Emphasize:
  - “Best choice”
  - Reviews
  - Recommendations
    → Helps users confidently select one option

## Ethics in Behavioral Design

### Risks

- Manipulation through:
  - Dark patterns
  - Addictive mechanics
  - Exploiting cognitive biases

### Ethical Considerations

- Designers must:
  - Avoid exploiting users
  - Promote user well-being
  - Balance business goals with responsibility

### Example Idea

- Systems could detect harmful behavior (e.g., overuse) and intervene to protect users.

## Key Takeaways

### Core Principles

- Attention: Guide what users see first.
- Mental models: Align with or intentionally break expectations.
- Confidence: The true driver of decisions.
- Choice: Balance abundance with clarity.
- Ethics: Design responsibly, not manipulatively.

### Practical Heuristics

- Reduce friction → increase action
- Highlight one clear path → boost confidence
- Use visual cues → control attention
- Limit overwhelming choices → prevent paralysis
