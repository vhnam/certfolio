---

## Humanity-Centered Design

### Definition

- Humanity-centered design: An approach that expands design focus from individuals to entire socio-technical systems, including all people, living beings, and the environment.
- Socio-technical system: A complex system that includes humans, technology, organizations, and environmental factors interacting together.

### Evolution of Design Focus

- Design as marketing tool: Originally used to increase sales and support industry.
- User-centered design: Focused on usability for people interacting with products.
- Human-centered design: Shifted language to emphasize people instead of “users.”
- Humanity-centered design: Expands scope to global systems, society, and the planet.

### Core Principles of Human-Centered Design

- Focus on people: Ensure designs are understandable and usable by real users.
- Systems thinking: Recognize that no product exists in isolation.
- Solve the right problem: Address root causes instead of symptoms.
- Iterative design: Prototype, test, and refine continuously.

### Limitations of Human-Centered Design Today

- Overemphasis on aesthetics: Modern products often prioritize visuals over usability.
- Reduced usability: Interfaces (e.g., touchscreens in cars) can create unsafe or inefficient experiences.
- Narrow scope: Focus on individuals ignores larger system-level consequences.

## Designing for Systems and Society

### Why System-Level Thinking Matters

- Global problems: Challenges today (e.g., pandemics, supply chains) are interconnected.
- Interdependence: People, organizations, and countries rely on each other.
- No isolation: Actions in one part of the world affect others.

### Example: Supply Chain Disruption

- Semiconductor chips: Essential components in electronics (phones, computers, appliances).
- Global dependency: Manufacturing concentrated in specific regions (e.g., China).
- Impact of disruption:
  - Lockdowns halted production
  - Caused global shortages
  - Affected multiple industries

## Expanding Scope to Humanity and the Planet

### What “Humanity” Includes

- All people: Across cultures, regions, and societies.
- All living creatures: Non-human life affected by design decisions.
- Earth’s ecosystem: Environmental systems that sustain life.

### Design Responsibilities

- Avoid harm:
  - Do not damage the environment
  - Do not negatively impact other species
- Promote sustainability:
  - Support long-term ecological balance
- Improve quality of life:
  - Enable positive, sustainable living conditions

## Key Takeaways for Designers

### Mindset Shift

- From individual → system → humanity
- From product thinking → ecosystem thinking
- From isolated solutions → interconnected impact

### Designer Capabilities

- Problem finders: Identify root causes, not just symptoms.
- Iterative thinkers: Continuously test and refine solutions.
- System thinkers: Understand complex interdependencies.

### Practical Implications

- Design decisions must consider:
  - Global impact
  - Cross-industry effects
  - Environmental consequences
- Success criteria expands from:
  - Usability → Sustainability + societal impact

### Final Perspective

- We are all connected:
  - People
  - Organizations
  - Countries
  - Ecosystems
- Humanity-centered design ensures solutions work not just for users—but for the entire world system.
