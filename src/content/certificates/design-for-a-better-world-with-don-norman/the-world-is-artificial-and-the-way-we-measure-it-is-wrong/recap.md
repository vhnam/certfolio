## Artificiality and the Designed World

### The World Is Artificial

- Artificial world: Most aspects of our environment are **human-made systems**.
- Designed systems: Cities, infrastructure, institutions, and technologies exist because humans designed them.
- Design responsibility: Since design helped create the current global problems, **design can also help solve them**.

## Limitations of Traditional Measurements

### Origins of Numerical Metrics

- Traditional metrics: Measurement systems originally developed in the **physical sciences**.
- Quantitative focus: These metrics emphasize **numerical data and measurable variables**.

### Missing Qualitative Factors

Traditional measurements often ignore important aspects of life, such as:

- Human well-being
- Social conditions
- Environmental sustainability
- Ecosystem health

Because of these limitations, alternative measurements are necessary to understand **complex human and ecological systems**.

## Measuring What Truly Matters

### The Problem with Easy Measurements

- Measurement bias: Many things are measured simply because **they are easy to measure**.
- Meaningful metrics: Important measurements should reflect **real societal values and impacts**.

### Combining Numbers and Stories

To understand complex systems:

- Numerical metrics provide **quantitative insight**.
- Stories and context explain **human experiences and outcomes**.

Using both approaches helps create a **more complete understanding of reality**.

## Human Behavior and Global Challenges

### Behavior as the Core Issue

- Many global problems are driven by **human behavior**.
- Technology alone cannot solve these issues.

### Designers’ Role

Designers focus on:

- Understanding people
- Studying behavior
- Creating systems that support human needs

Because of this expertise, designers can contribute to solving **major societal and environmental challenges**.
