## The Problem with Measuring Everything

### Misinterpretation of Measurement in Science

- Lord Kelvin quote: Often paraphrased as _“If you can’t measure it, you don’t understand it.”_
- Original context: Kelvin referred specifically to **physical sciences**, where measurement is reliable and repeatable.
- Misapplication: Social sciences and economics adopted the idea and began trying to **measure everything**, even when measurement is inappropriate.

### Path Independence vs Path Dependence

- Path independence: Systems where outcomes do not depend on how you reached them.
  - Example: Physics experiments produce the same results repeatedly.

- Path dependence: Systems where outcomes depend on history, context, and experiences.
  - Example: Human behavior depends on culture, upbringing, and past experiences.

Human systems are **path-dependent**, which makes them far harder to measure with simple numeric metrics.

## The Problem with GDP

### GDP as a Misleading Metric

- Gross Domestic Product (GDP): Measures a country's economic activity by calculating **total spending**.
- Core issue: GDP measures **money flow**, not societal well-being.

Problems with GDP:

- Positive and negative activities are counted the same.
- Environmental damage increases GDP because industries spend money.
- Cleaning pollution also increases GDP because it requires spending.
- Harmful actions may therefore be counted **twice as economic growth**.

GDP does not measure:

- Quality of life
- Health care quality
- Education quality
- Environmental sustainability
- Social well-being

## Measuring What Actually Matters

### Quality of Life as a Better Measurement Goal

Instead of focusing on economic output, societies should measure:

- Quality of life
- Health and well-being
- Education systems
- Family stability
- Environmental sustainability

Rather than precise numeric measurements, social systems often require **comparative judgments**, such as:

- "This situation is better than that."
- "This policy improves quality of life."

## Alternative Measurement Systems

### Global Efforts to Create Better Metrics

Several initiatives attempt to measure societal success more meaningfully:

- OECD Better Life Index
- World Happiness Report
- United Nations quality-of-life metrics

These approaches evaluate **multiple aspects of life instead of one number**.

## The Doughnut Model of Economics

![The Doughnut of Social and Planetary Boundaries](../../../../assets/images/design-for-a-better-world-with-don-norman/the-world-is-artificial-and-the-way-we-measure-it-is-wrong/the-doughnut-of-social-and-planetary-boundaries.avif)

### Overview

- Doughnut Economics: Developed by economist **Kate Raworth** at the University of Oxford.
- Purpose: Measure societal success by balancing **human needs and planetary limits**.

The model uses a **doughnut-shaped framework** with two boundaries.

### Inner Ring: Social Foundation

Represents the **minimum conditions required for a decent life**.

Key factors include:

- Food
- Shelter
- Healthcare
- Education
- Equity
- Economic opportunity

If a society falls inside this ring, people lack essential resources.

### Outer Ring: Ecological Ceiling

Represents the **planetary boundaries that must not be exceeded**.

Key issues include:

- Climate change
- Air and water pollution
- Deforestation
- Biodiversity loss
- Resource depletion

Crossing this boundary damages the Earth’s life-support systems.

### The Safe and Just Space

The goal is to operate **between the two rings**, where:

- Human needs are met
- Planetary systems remain stable

This space represents a world that is both **socially just and environmentally sustainable**.

## Why the Doughnut Model Is Valuable

### Multi-Dimensional Measurement

Unlike GDP, the doughnut model:

- Evaluates **multiple indicators simultaneously**
- Shows ecological and social performance together
- Allows comparison between countries across many dimensions

Examples of insights:

- A country may perform well in **climate protection but poorly in healthcare**.
- Another may excel in **education but struggle with equity**.

This provides a **much clearer picture of societal performance**.

## Why This Is a Design Problem

### Making Complex Systems Understandable

The doughnut model is also a **design solution**.

- It visualizes complex societal data in an understandable format.
- It helps people quickly understand how their country performs.
- It communicates relationships between ecological and social issues.

Even though economists created it, the approach demonstrates **design thinking applied to societal challenges**.

## Implications for Designers

Designers can contribute by:

- Developing **better ways to visualize complex data**
- Creating **new metrics that reflect real human needs**
- Designing systems that measure **what truly matters**

Meaningful metrics help designers:

- Understand the **real problems**
- Evaluate whether their **solutions improve people's lives**

## Key Takeaways

- Many current measurement systems are **misleading or incomplete**.
- GDP measures economic activity but **ignores well-being and sustainability**.
- Human systems are **path-dependent**, making them difficult to measure with simple metrics.
- New measurement frameworks focus on **quality of life and environmental limits**.
- The **Doughnut Economics model** shows how multiple indicators can represent societal health.
- Designers can help create **better ways to measure, visualize, and understand complex systems**.
