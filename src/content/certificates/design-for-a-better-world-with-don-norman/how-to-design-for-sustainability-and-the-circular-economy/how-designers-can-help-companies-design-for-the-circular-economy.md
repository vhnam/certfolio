title: How Designers Can Help Companies Design for the Circular Economy
---

## The Challenge of Implementing a Circular Economy

The circular economy aims to eliminate waste by keeping materials and products in use for as long as possible. Ideally, products should be designed so that their components can be reused, repaired, or eventually returned safely to nature.

However, implementing this system is difficult. It requires companies to change how they manufacture products, select materials, and design items so they can be repaired or reused. These changes may increase costs and disrupt existing production methods.

Another challenge is that many products today prioritize aesthetics over durability and repairability. For example, making devices thinner and sleeker often makes them harder to repair or upgrade. Moving toward circular design may require rethinking these priorities.

## Why Business Models Must Change

A major obstacle to the circular economy is the current business model used by many companies. Most manufacturing businesses depend on continuously selling new products to remain profitable.

If products are designed to last much longer—five or ten years instead of two or three—companies may sell fewer items. This threatens traditional revenue models that rely on frequent replacement cycles.

Because of this, transitioning to a circular economy often requires companies to rethink their entire business strategy rather than simply redesigning their products.

## Designers as Agents of Change

Designers have a critical role in helping companies move toward circular systems. However, simply arguing that products harm the environment is not enough. Designers must propose solutions that also help businesses remain competitive and profitable.

To do this effectively, designers must understand how companies operate. They need to speak the language of business, communicate with executives, and show how circular practices can benefit both the organization and society.

This means designers must expand their knowledge beyond traditional design skills to include business strategy, organizational politics, and the broader social and technological systems in which products exist.

## From Products to Services

One promising strategy for circular design is shifting from selling products to providing services. Instead of focusing on the physical object, companies focus on the benefit the product provides.

For example, a camera’s real value is not the device itself but the ability to capture and preserve memories. A company could provide this service through a subscription model, supplying and maintaining the device while ensuring the service continues over time.

This approach creates a continuous income stream for companies while reducing the need for frequent product replacement.

## Case Study: Transportation as a Service

The automotive industry provides a clear example of how service-based models can support circular thinking. Most privately owned cars remain unused for 90–95% of their lifetime, yet manufacturers produce millions of new vehicles each year.

Transportation as a Service (TaaS) offers an alternative model. Instead of owning a car, people access vehicles through shared services such as carsharing platforms. The company manages a fleet of vehicles while users simply pay for transportation when needed.

This system reduces the number of cars required while still providing mobility. It also allows companies to maintain vehicles more efficiently and adopt cleaner technologies.

## The Future Role of Designers

Moving from a linear to a circular economy requires a fundamental redesign of products, business models, and systems. Designers are uniquely positioned to guide this transformation.

To succeed, designers must understand the complex socio-technical systems that connect businesses, users, and the environment. By combining sustainable design with business thinking, designers can help create solutions that benefit companies, people, and the planet.
