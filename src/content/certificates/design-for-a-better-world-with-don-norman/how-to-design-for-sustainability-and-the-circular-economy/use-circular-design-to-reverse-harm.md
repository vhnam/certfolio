## Linear vs. Circular Systems

Modern manufacturing largely follows a **linear economy**: raw materials are extracted, turned into products, used for a period of time, and then discarded as waste. Each stage—mining, manufacturing, transportation, usage, and disposal—produces pollution and waste.

Nature works differently. Natural systems are **circular**, where materials continuously return to the ecosystem. For example, a banana peel decomposes and becomes nutrients for the soil. Nothing is wasted because materials are reused or regenerated.

## Why Recycling Alone Is Not Enough

Recycling helps reduce waste but is **not a complete solution**. It is often expensive and energy-intensive, and in many cases it costs more to recycle materials than to produce new ones.

The real solution is to **design products differently** so that they can be repaired, upgraded, reused, and eventually disassembled into reusable materials.

## Designing for Circularity

In a circular economy, products should be designed to remain useful for as long as possible.

Key approaches include:

- **Repairability:** Products should be easy to fix when they break.
- **Upgradability:** Components such as cameras or wireless chips should be replaceable instead of forcing users to buy new devices.
- **Disassembly:** Products should be easy to take apart so materials and components can be reused.

Some companies already follow this model. For example, printer manufacturers collect used cartridges and **reuse their components** to create new products.

## Principles of Circular Design

![The butterfly diagram of the circular economy](../../../../assets/images/design-for-a-better-world-with-don-norman/how-to-design-for-sustainability-and-the-circular-economy/the-circular-economy-butterfly-diagram.avif)

Circular design focuses on three main principles:

1. **Eliminate waste and pollution**
   Reduce harmful materials and redesign products to prevent waste.
2. **Circulate products and materials**
   Keep products in use for as long as possible through repair, reuse, and recycling.
3. **Regenerate natural systems**
   Return natural materials to the environment so they can support future growth.

## Challenges for Designers

One major challenge is the **conflict between aesthetics and circularity**. Modern products are often designed to be extremely thin, lightweight, and visually appealing by combining many materials together. While this improves appearance and usability, it makes products **very difficult to separate and reuse**.

Designers therefore play a critical role in creating products that balance **function, aesthetics, and sustainability**.

## Key Takeaway

Today’s economy is mostly linear—extract, produce, use, and discard. A **circular economy** aims to eliminate waste by designing products so their materials can be repaired, reused, recycled, or naturally decomposed. Achieving this requires new design approaches, collaboration across industries, and careful consideration of materials from the very beginning of the design process.
