title: Sustainability is Not Enough
---

## What Is Sustainability

### Definition

- Sustainability: Maintaining the current state of a system over time.
- Environmental sustainability: Managing resources and systems so they continue to support life without causing long-term damage.

Key idea:

- Maintaining the **current state of the world is not enough**, because the current state already includes climate damage, pollution, and large-scale waste.

### Why Sustainability Should Not Be the Ultimate Goal

Key problems with the current state:

- Increasing **carbon emissions**
- Rising **global temperatures**
- Growing **waste from manufacturing and consumption**
- Environmental disasters such as **fires, droughts, floods, and hunger**

Important insight:

- Sustainability prevents things from getting worse.
- But we must also **repair the damage already done to the planet**.

### Communicating Climate Change

- Global temperature rise: Scientists often refer to a **2°C increase since the Industrial Revolution**.
- Misinterpretation: A 2°C change seems small in daily weather terms.

Better communication:

- Focus on the **real consequences** of climate change.

Examples:

- Floods
- Hurricanes
- Droughts
- Wildfires
- Food shortages

## Rethinking Design and Production

### Waste in Manufacturing and Product Design

Environmental harm occurs at multiple stages:

- Mining raw materials
- Manufacturing products
- Using energy during production
- Discarding products after short lifespans

Design problems:

- Products are difficult to repair.
- Devices are hard to open and disassemble.
- Components cannot be easily reused or recycled.

Required design shift:

- Create products that are **repairable, upgradeable, and long-lasting**.

### Changing Business Models

Current business model:

- Companies rely on **frequent product replacement**.
- New product versions encourage continuous consumption.

Problems:

- Leads to excessive waste
- Short product lifespans
- Increased environmental damage

Alternative approach:

- Sell **systems instead of disposable products**.
- Extend product life cycles through maintenance and upgrades.

## Planned Obsolescence

### Definition

- Planned obsolescence: Designing products with intentionally limited lifespans so consumers must replace them.

### Techniques Used in Planned Obsolescence

1. Breakdown: Products are difficult or expensive to repair due to restricted parts or specialized servicing.
2. Progress: New technologies are incompatible with older devices, forcing replacement.
3. Fashion: Marketing makes older products appear outdated even when they still function.

Example:

- Smartphones and laptops often become obsolete within one or two years.

## Case Study: Smartphones

### Traditional Smartphone Design

Example issues with many smartphones:

- Non-removable batteries
- Restricted repair services
- Limited upgrade options

Example:

- Many devices require **authorized service centers**.
- Repairs outside official channels may **void warranties**.

Result:

- Users replace the device instead of repairing it.

### Modular Design Alternative

Some products follow a more sustainable model.

Example:

- Desktop PCs allow users to replace or upgrade individual components.

Example company:

- Fairphone: A smartphone designed to be modular and repairable.

Key characteristics:

- Parts can be replaced with a standard screwdriver.
- Components such as batteries or screens can be swapped easily.
- Devices are designed for **repair, refurbishment, and upgrades**.

## Circular Design Inspired by Nature

### Nature’s Model

Nature operates in a **closed-loop system**.

Principles:

- Waste becomes a resource.
- Materials return to the ecosystem.

Design implication:

- Products should be **easy to disassemble and reuse**.

Goal:

- Move toward a **circular economy** where materials remain in continuous use.

## The Take Away

Maintaining the current environmental state is not enough because the current system already produces excessive waste and carbon emissions.

Key principles for designers and businesses:

- Design products that are **repairable, modular, and long-lasting**.
- Replace planned obsolescence with **circular design**.
- Shift business models from selling products to **providing systems and services**.
- Recover and reuse materials through **disassembly and remanufacturing**.

Systems thinking benefits:

- Reduced environmental impact
- Lower production costs
- New revenue streams such as spare parts and repairs.
