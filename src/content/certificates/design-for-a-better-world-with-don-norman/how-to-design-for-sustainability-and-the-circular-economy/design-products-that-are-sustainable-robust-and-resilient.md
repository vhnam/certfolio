title: Design Products That Are Sustainable, Robust and Resilient

## Understanding Complex Socio-Technical Systems

### Definition

- Complex socio-technical system: A system where **technology, human behavior, organizations, and natural systems interact and influence each other**.
- Interconnected systems: Actions in one part of the system affect other parts in unpredictable ways.

Key idea:

- Environmental problems are not caused by a **single country or organization**, but by **global interconnected activities**.

## System Dynamics

### Feedback Loops

- Feedback loop: A system mechanism that **reacts to current conditions and adjusts behavior accordingly**.

Example:

- Thermostat system:
  - Temperature rises above the set level → air conditioner turns on.
  - Temperature falls below the set level → heater turns on.

Purpose:

- Maintain a desired state by continuously responding to changes.

### Feedforward Loops

- Feedforward loop: A system mechanism that **predicts future conditions and adjusts actions in advance**.

Example:

- Predicting environmental changes and adjusting behavior before problems occur.

Important characteristic:

- Effects of some actions may **take decades to appear**, making system behavior difficult to understand.

## Example of System Interconnection

### Air Conditioner Example

Air conditioners illustrate how everyday technologies affect larger systems.

Direct effects:

- Cool indoor environments.

Indirect effects:

- Heat expelled into the outdoor environment.
- Additional heat produced due to energy inefficiency.

Energy dependency:

- Air conditioners require electricity generated from various sources.

Possible energy sources:

- Coal
- Natural gas
- Hydroelectric power
- Wind energy
- Solar energy

System consequences:

- Electricity production generates additional heat and emissions.
- Manufacturing and transportation of devices also consume energy.

Result:

- Even simple technologies become part of **large interconnected environmental systems**.

### Electric Vehicles Example

Electric vehicles appear environmentally friendly, but system-level effects must be considered.

Questions designers must ask:

- What materials are required to manufacture batteries?
- How much waste is produced during manufacturing?
- How are batteries disposed of or recycled?
- What energy source powers the electricity used for charging?

Key insight:

- If electricity comes from **coal power plants**, electric vehicles may produce **greater environmental impact** than expected.

## Design Goals for Complex Systems

Design solutions must address multiple system qualities simultaneously.

### Sustainability

- Sustainability: Maintain the current state and prevent further environmental harm.

Goal:

- Stop adding waste, emissions, and damage to ecosystems.

### Robustness

- Robustness: The ability of a system to **withstand stress or repeated use without failing**.

Example:

- Structures designed to remain stable under harsh environmental conditions.

### Resilience

- Resilience: The ability of a system to **recover or repair itself after damage**.

Example:

- Systems capable of restoring functionality after disruptions.

### Regeneration

- Regeneration: Designing systems that **restore environmental resources and return materials back to nature**.

Goal:

- Mimic natural ecosystems where waste becomes a resource.

## Applying These Principles

### Rethinking the Air Conditioning Problem

Instead of focusing only on improving air conditioners, designers can **remove the need for them** through better building design.

Possible solutions:

- Use insulating building materials that reduce heat absorption.
- Plant shade trees to block direct sunlight.
- Apply traditional architectural techniques for passive cooling.

Examples:

- Wind catchers (Bâdgir) in Iran that create natural ventilation.
- Hawa Mahal in India designed to promote airflow and cooling.

Benefits:

- Reduced energy consumption
- No electricity required for cooling
- Lower environmental impact

## The Take Away

Designers must recognize that products and services exist within **complex global systems**.

Key design principles:

- Sustainability: Avoid creating further environmental harm.
- Robustness: Ensure systems can withstand stress and long-term use.
- Resilience: Enable systems to recover from damage.
- Regeneration: Restore ecosystems and return materials to natural cycles.

Outcome:

- Designing systems that are **both robust and resilient** allows society to gradually **repair environmental damage and regenerate ecosystems**.
