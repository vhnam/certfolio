## Lesson Overview

### Context: The Designed World and Planet Health

- Designed world: Most objects, systems, and environments around us are artificial creations made by humans.
- Progress measurement: Traditional ways of measuring progress often ignore environmental impact and planetary health.
- Planet health: The condition of Earth’s ecosystems, which directly affects human survival and quality of life.

This lesson highlights how environmental sustainability is often overlooked when evaluating technological and economic progress.

### Don Norman’s Perspective on Earth and Human Survival

Quoted verbatim:

> “The earth is extremely resilient. Even though the continents shift, landmasses fall into the sea, and others emerge from the middle of the ocean (the Hawaiian Islands, for example); even though some species of animals die, and others emerge; even though severe storms, fires, earthquakes, and volcanoes occur, these changes do not challenge the existence of the planet. Climate change? A meteorite strike that wipes out an entire species? It has happened before. The earth will survive. Human life is not as resilient, however.”
> — Don Norman, _Design for a Better World_

Key idea:

- Earth resilience: The planet itself can recover from extreme natural changes.
- Human vulnerability: Human societies are far less resilient and are at risk from environmental disruption.

## Sustainability and Climate Challenges

### Sustainability

- Sustainability: The practice of maintaining systems and resources so they continue to support life and future generations.
- Climate change: A major global challenge threatening ecosystems and human life.
- Public discourse: The term “sustainability” appears widely in marketing, conferences, and academic discussions.

However, the lesson questions whether companies that claim sustainability truly create meaningful environmental impact.

## Modern Production and Waste

### Waste from Modern Production

Modern production systems have contributed to massive environmental problems.

Key issue:

- Industrial production: Large-scale manufacturing has created enormous amounts of waste and resource consumption.

This raises the need to rethink how products are designed, produced, and disposed of.

## Economic Models

### Linear Economy

- Linear economy: A system where resources are extracted, products are made, used, and then discarded as waste.
- Result: Continuous growth in waste and resource depletion.

### Circular Economy

- Circular economy: A system where materials are reused, repaired, recycled, or regenerated to minimize waste.
- Goal: Keep resources in use for as long as possible.

#### Opportunities

- Reduced waste
- More efficient resource use
- New business models and innovation

#### Challenges

- Changing existing manufacturing systems
- Aligning economic incentives
- Redesigning products and services

## The Role of Designers

### Designers in the Circular Economy

Designers play a crucial role in enabling sustainable systems.

Key responsibilities:

- Designing products for reuse and repair
- Reducing material waste
- Supporting circular production models
- Creating systems that extend product life cycles

### Adapting Design Practices

Designers must adapt their methods to support sustainability.

Required shifts:

- Thinking beyond individual products
- Considering full product life cycles
- Designing systems rather than isolated objects

## Learning Goals

### Key Concepts Covered

- How modern production creates large amounts of waste
- Why sustainability alone is not the final goal
- The difference between linear and circular economies
- Opportunities and challenges in building a circular economy
- The critical role designers play in sustainable systems
- How designers must adapt their practices
