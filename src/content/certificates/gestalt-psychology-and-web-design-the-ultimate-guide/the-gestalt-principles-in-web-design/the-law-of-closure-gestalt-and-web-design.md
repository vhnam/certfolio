## Applying the Laws

### Law of Closure

- Law of Closure: Humans perceive incomplete or partially hidden elements as complete, whole objects.
- Perceptual bias:
  - The brain fills in missing visual information
  - Prefers complete, unified forms over fragmented ones
- Effect:
  - Enables recognition even with limited detail

## Examples of Closure

### Visual Recognition

- Incomplete figures:
  - Still perceived as whole objects

- Example:
  - WWF logo:
    - Minimal shapes → recognized as a panda
- Insight:
  - Small visual cues can trigger full recognition

### Minimal Structures

- Graphs:
  - Axes without full boundaries still perceived as complete
- Key idea:
  - Closure allows users to infer structure from partial elements

## Design Applications

### Minimalist Design

- Use fewer visual elements:
  - Reduce unnecessary details
  - Maintain clarity and meaning
- Benefit:
  - Cleaner, more efficient interfaces

### Grouping and Structure

- Use implied boundaries:
  - Suggest grouping without explicit borders
- Improves:
  - Data-ink ratio
  - Visual simplicity

### Efficient Communication

- Provide:
  - Just enough information for recognition
- Result:
  - Faster understanding
  - Reduced cognitive load

## In Summary

### Key Principles

- Closure:
  - Completes missing visual information automatically
- Recognition:
  - Works even with incomplete or occluded elements
- Efficiency:
  - Supports minimalist, high-impact design

### Risks of Misuse

- Too little information:
  - May prevent recognition
- Over-simplification:
  - Can reduce clarity if users lack enough visual cues
