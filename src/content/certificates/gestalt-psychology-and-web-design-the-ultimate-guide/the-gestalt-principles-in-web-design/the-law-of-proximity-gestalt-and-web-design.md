## Applying the Laws

### Law of Proximity

- Law of Proximity: Elements placed close together are perceived as belonging to the same group.
- Gestalt Principle: Humans perceive “whole” objects rather than assembling parts individually.
- Kurt Koffka:
  - Idea: “The whole is other than the sum of the parts”
- Gestalt Effect:
  - Ability to recognize complete forms instead of isolated elements
- Perceptual bias:
  - Spatial closeness → perceived relationship

## Example of Law of Proximity

### Pizza Hut Example

- Correct use:
  - Text placed near corresponding images → clear association
- Incorrect alternative:
  - If text is far from image:
    - Confusion between neighboring items
    - Requires back-and-forth eye movement

### Limitation in Example

- Similar styling across boxes:
  - Signals grouping of all deals
- Problem:
  - Hard to distinguish individual items
  - Relies on short-term memory to locate a specific option
- Impact:
  - Slower identification
  - Increased cognitive effort

## The Design Solution

### Structuring Information

- Organize layout with:
  - Clear grouping
  - Logical spacing
- Benefits:
  - Reduces cognitive load
  - Enables faster decision-making
  - Supports scanning by categories

### Managing Visual Distance

- Keep related elements:
  - Close together
- Avoid:
  - Large gaps between related content
- Reason:
  - Reduces eye movement (saccades)
  - Prevents unnecessary effort

### Preventing Errors

- Large distance between related elements:
  - Increases chance of selection errors
- Poor proximity:
  - Requires more attention than users typically give
  - Slows interaction speed

## In Summary

### Key Principles

- Proximity:
  - Group related items by placing them close together
- Separation:
  - Place unrelated items far enough apart to avoid confusion
- Structure:
  - Use spacing to guide visual hierarchy and grouping

### Risks of Misuse

- Poor proximity:
  - Causes misinterpretation of relationships
  - Increases cognitive load
- Over-grouping:
  - Makes it difficult to distinguish individual items
  - Slows down visual search and decision-making
