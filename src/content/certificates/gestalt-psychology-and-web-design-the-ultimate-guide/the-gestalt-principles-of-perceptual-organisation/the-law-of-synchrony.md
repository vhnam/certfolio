## New Grouping Laws

### Definition

- Law of Synchrony: A Gestalt principle stating that elements occurring at the same time are perceived as a group.
- Perceptual bias: Humans group visual events based on temporal alignment, even without motion.

> **Key Idea**
>
> _The law of synchrony… identifies the grouping bias when visual elements appear at the same time yet they are not in motion._

### How It Works

- Elements that:
  - Appear simultaneously
  - Change state at the same time
    → are perceived as related
- Applies to both:
  - Dynamic events (e.g., flashing)
  - Static states (e.g., lights on/off together)

### Perceptual Mechanism

#### Time-Based Grouping

- The brain uses timing to:
  - Link elements together
  - Infer shared meaning or function
- Synchrony acts as:
  - A grouping signal independent of space or form

#### Relation to Common Fate

- Similarity:
  - Both rely on temporal patterns
- Difference:
  - Common Fate → motion-based
  - Synchrony → timing-based (with or without motion)

### Examples

#### Real-World Events

- People standing up together:
  - Perceived as one group
- Lights turning on simultaneously:
  - Grouped visually

#### Interface Signals

- UI elements updating at the same time:
  - Perceived as related
- Notifications or highlights:
  - Use synchrony to attract attention

### Importance

- Enables quick identification of related events
- Helps users:
  - Focus on relevant changes
  - Ignore unrelated elements
- Increasingly important in:
  - Digital interfaces
  - Attention-driven systems

### Design Implications

- Use synchronized changes to signal grouping:
  - Animations
  - State updates
- Highlight important elements by:
  - Triggering simultaneous visual changes
- Avoid unintended grouping:
  - Unsynchronized elements may confuse users

### Key Takeaways

- Timing is a powerful grouping cue
- Synchrony works even without motion
- It helps users detect relationships and prioritize attention quickly
