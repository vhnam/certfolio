title: The Law of Continuity
---

## Grouping Laws

### Definition

- Law of Continuity: A Gestalt principle stating that elements arranged along a line or curve are perceived as part of a continuous form.
- Object constancy: The ability to perceive complete objects even when parts are hidden or interrupted.
- Perceptual bias: Humans prefer smooth, continuous paths over abrupt changes.

> **Key Idea**
>
> _The law of continuity… identifies the human ability to determine object constancy when the whole object is not completely visible… and perceive interlinking elements as continuous forms in a particular direction._

### How It Works

- The brain follows:
  - The smoothest path
  - The most natural direction
- Intersecting or overlapping elements are perceived as:
  - Separate continuous lines
- Continuity is prioritized over:
  - Fragmentation
  - Abrupt directional changes

### Perceptual Mechanism

#### Path Following

- Visual system assumes:
  - Elements continue in a consistent direction
- Even when interrupted:
  - The mind “fills in” missing parts

#### Experience-Based Expectation

- Based on real-world learning:
  - Objects move through continuous states
- Leads to:
  - Predictable interpretation of partially hidden forms

### Example Insight

#### Line Interpretation

- Intersecting lines:
  - Perceived as two continuous paths (e.g., A → D and C → B)
- Alternative interpretations:
  - Ignored due to lower continuity

#### Occlusion Scenario

- Person behind object:
  - Arm and leg perceived as belonging to same body
- Even when incorrect:
  - Brain enforces continuity

#### Optical Illusion

- Two separate elements:
  - Misinterpreted as one continuous object
- Shows:
  - Perception is influenced by bias, not just raw input

### Design Implications

- Use alignment and flow to guide attention:
  - Lines
  - Curves
  - Directional layouts
- Maintain visual continuity to:
  - Improve readability
  - Create smooth user flows
- Avoid breaking expected paths:
  - Prevent confusion or misinterpretation

### Key Takeaways

- Humans prefer continuous over fragmented visuals
- The brain completes missing information automatically
- Continuity guides perception, attention, and interpretation
