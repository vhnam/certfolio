## Research

### Repetition Discrimination Task (RDT)

- Repetition discrimination task: An experiment to measure how grouping affects perception speed.
- Objective: Identify how quickly users detect adjacent identical shapes (circles or squares).

#### Task Setup

- Participants:
  - View sequences of shapes
  - Identify the only pair of identical neighboring shapes
- Actions:
  - Press one key for squares
  - Press another key for circles
- Measurement:
  - Response time (speed of detection)

#### Experimental Conditions

- Same group condition:
  - Identical shapes belong to the same visual group
- Different group condition:
  - Identical shapes belong to separate groups
- Control condition:
  - No grouping bias (uniform spacing)

### Key Hypothesis

- Law of Proximity:
  - Closer elements → faster detection
- Tested whether proximity is the dominant grouping factor

## Research Results

### Findings

- Faster detection:
  - When identical shapes are in the same group
- Slower detection:
  - When identical shapes are in different groups
- Slowest detection:
  - When shapes are close but visually grouped with non-identical elements

### Insight

- Grouping overrides proximity:
  - Visual grouping can be stronger than physical distance
- Perception depends on:
  - Context and grouping cues
  - Not just spatial closeness

## Three New Gestalt Laws

### Principle of Common Region

- Common region: Elements within the same bounded area are perceived as grouped.
- Example:
  - Items inside the same container (e.g., ellipse) are grouped
  - Even if closer to items outside the container

### Principle of Element Connectedness

- Element connectedness: Physically connected elements are perceived as a group.
- Example:
  - Two dots connected by a line → seen as one unit

- Stronger than:
  - Proximity or similarity

### Principle of Synchrony

- Synchrony: Elements changing or occurring at the same time are perceived as grouped.
- Example:
  - Flashing lights in unison → grouped together
- Related to:
  - Law of Common Fate (motion-based grouping)
- Difference:
  - Applies even without movement (static timing states)

## Key Takeaways

- Grouping directly affects perception speed and accuracy
- Proximity is not always dominant
- Stronger grouping cues include:
  - Common region
  - Connectedness
  - Synchrony
- Visual context can override spatial distance
