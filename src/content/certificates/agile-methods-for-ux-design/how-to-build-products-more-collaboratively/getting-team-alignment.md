---
title: Getting Team Alignment
---

Agile teams move quickly and often work in parallel. For collaboration to succeed, everyone must share a clear understanding of **what they’re building, why it matters, and what success looks like**. Without alignment, teams can fall into common traps—like communicating only through tickets, building features without understanding their purpose, or arguing about priorities without clear goals.

To address this problem, product management expert **Adam Thomas** introduced the **Initiative Document**—a structured way for teams to align around outcomes, strategy, and execution. This document helps connect everyday work to company goals and ensures everyone understands the reasoning behind product decisions.

The Initiative Document typically contains **five sections** that guide teams from strategy to execution.

## Abstract

The abstract provides a **concise overview of the initiative** and explains the problem the team is trying to solve.

A good abstract summarizes:

- What the team aims to achieve
- Who the work is for
- Why it matters
- The broader context within the company and market
- What success might look like

Rather than presenting a fixed solution, the abstract should frame the work as a set of **bets or hypotheses**. Product teams rarely know exactly what users want, so the goal is to explore possibilities and gradually build confidence.

When done well, the abstract gives readers immediate context and encourages discussion about the problem space instead of prematurely focusing on solutions.

## Objectives

The objectives section connects the initiative directly to the **company’s strategy and goals**.

It should clearly show how the team’s work supports larger organizational priorities. For example, an initiative might connect:

- A **product experiment or bet**
- To a **team-level objective or OKR**
- Which supports a **long-term company vision**

This creates a clear line from **day-to-day work to long-term strategy**.

If a team cannot explain how their work contributes to a meaningful objective, it may indicate that the initiative is not worth pursuing. The objective section therefore helps teams prioritize work that truly matters.

## Resources

This section outlines **what is required to execute the initiative**.

Typical considerations include:

- Which disciplines or roles are needed
- Who is responsible for specific tasks
- Communication expectations between teams
- How confident the team is based on available resources

Resource planning helps avoid common disruptions, such as team members being reassigned mid-project or underestimated staffing needs.

It also reminds designers and researchers that resource constraints affect design decisions. A solution that looks great on paper may not be feasible with the current team or timeline.

## Success and Survival

This section defines **what success looks like—and when the team should stop**.

It includes:

- The outcomes that indicate success
- Signals that show the initiative is working
- Conditions that would justify stopping the project

Clear success criteria help teams evaluate whether their bets are paying off. Equally important, defining failure conditions allows teams to **stop investing in initiatives that are not delivering value**.

This supports genuine agility: rather than blindly finishing projects, teams adapt based on evidence and learning.

## Time Horizon

Although agile teams embrace flexibility, stakeholders still need **time expectations**.

The time horizon section outlines:

- When progress should be reviewed
- When teams expect to learn whether a bet is successful
- How frequently stakeholders should check in

This does not require rigid deadlines, but it sets **reasonable expectations for progress and accountability**. Clear timelines also help build trust with leadership and stakeholders.

## Why the Initiative Document Matters

The Initiative Document helps teams maintain alignment in fast-moving agile environments. By clarifying goals, assumptions, resources, and evaluation criteria, it ensures everyone understands the **purpose behind the work**.

More importantly, it encourages teams to focus on **outcomes rather than outputs**, allowing them to learn quickly, adapt when necessary, and stop initiatives that no longer make sense.

In short, the Initiative Document helps agile teams stay focused on solving real problems—not just shipping features.
