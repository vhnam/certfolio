## Customer Collaboration in Agile

One of the core principles of the **Agile Manifesto** is **customer collaboration over contract negotiation**. Instead of defining every requirement upfront, agile teams work closely with customers during development to adapt and refine the product.

In early agile contexts—such as contract engineering—teams could collaborate directly with a single customer or a small group who helped guide decisions during development. However, modern products often serve **large and diverse audiences**, making it impossible to rely on a single customer’s input.

Because of this, teams must use **research and collaborative design approaches** to represent the broader user base.

## What Participatory Design Is

**Participatory design (or co-design)** is an approach where **users actively participate in the design process**, not just in research.

Instead of only observing users or interviewing them about their needs, designers involve them in **generating solutions** to problems.

This method:

- Expands the idea of customer collaboration
- Includes **real users or representative participants**
- Helps teams design solutions **with users rather than for them**

Participatory design originated in **Scandinavia**, where it was called **cooperative design**, and later influenced practices used in agile development.

## How Participatory Design Works

Participatory design shifts users from **research subjects** to **design collaborators**.

Typical participatory activities include:

- Co-design workshops
- Card sorting
- Storyboarding sessions
- Affinity mapping exercises

These activities allow participants to:

- Share how they **organize information**
- Suggest **ideas and solutions**
- Reveal **mental models and expectations**

Designers then use this input to inform and refine the product design.

## Example: Card Sorting

A common participatory design technique is **card sorting**, which helps teams understand how users organize information.

### How It Works

1. Prepare cards representing items in a system (e.g., job listings).
2. Ask participants to **group the cards in ways that make sense to them**.
3. Encourage them to **think aloud or discuss their reasoning**.
4. Conduct the activity with **multiple participants** to identify patterns.

### What It Reveals

Card sorting helps designers learn:

- How users naturally **categorize information**
- Which groupings feel **intuitive**
- Where users may **interpret content differently**

Instead of guessing how to structure a system, designers rely on **real user perspectives**.

## Benefits of Participatory Design

Participatory design offers several advantages for agile teams.

### Faster Learning

Involving users directly helps teams quickly identify:

- misunderstandings
- usability problems
- unrealistic assumptions

### More Inclusive Design

By gathering input from diverse users, teams avoid designing only from their own perspective.

### Better Problem Solving

Users can reveal **practical insights and ideas** that designers may overlook.

### Strong Alignment with Agile

Participatory design supports the agile principle of **continuous collaboration with customers and users**.

## Key Takeaway

Customer collaboration is a fundamental principle of agile development, but collaboration should extend beyond a single stakeholder.

**Participatory design techniques allow teams to involve real users in the design process**, helping generate better solutions and avoid costly mistakes.

Methods such as **card sorting, co-design workshops, and collaborative exercises** enable teams to learn quickly, design more inclusively, and ensure that products reflect the needs and mental models of the people who will actually use them.
