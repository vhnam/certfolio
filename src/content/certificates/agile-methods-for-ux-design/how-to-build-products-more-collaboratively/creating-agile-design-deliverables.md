## Role of Design Deliverables in Agile Teams

### Agile Myth: No Deliverables

- Agile myth: Agile design does not require deliverables.
- Reality: Agile teams still create **design artifacts and documentation**.

### Purpose of Deliverables

Deliverables help teams:

- Validate designs with **users** (e.g., prototypes)
- Think through **complex problems** (e.g., task flows, storyboards)
- Share ideas and gather **team feedback**
- Document **decisions and product direction**

### Key Principle

- Deliverables are **not the final product**.
- They exist to **support collaboration, communication, and decision-making**.

Spending excessive time creating **pixel-perfect artifacts for features that may never ship** wastes valuable design time.

## Understanding the Purpose of a Deliverable

Before creating a deliverable, designers should answer three questions:

- Who is the **audience**?
- What are you trying to **communicate**?
- What **action** should the audience take?

### Audience of Design Deliverables

The users of design deliverables are often **team members**, such as:

- Engineers
- Product managers
- Marketers
- Sales teams
- Customer support teams
- Future team members or your **future self**

Deliverables should help these audiences **understand design decisions and act on them**.

### Designing Deliverables for Action

Deliverables should provide enough information for the intended response.

Examples:

- Engineers: Need enough detail to **implement a feature**
- Team members: Need context to **give feedback**
- Stakeholders: Need information to **approve or prioritize work**

Mismatch examples:

- Asking for usability feedback from a **high-level sketch**
- Expecting engineers to build from a **vague concept**

## Choosing the Right Deliverable

### Framework for Selecting Deliverables

To decide which deliverable to create, consider four dimensions:

1. Visuality
2. Fidelity
3. Interactivity
4. Maintainability

## Visuality: How Visual Should It Be?

Deliverables vary from **text-based to highly visual**.

Examples along the visual spectrum:

- Story or written description
- Diagram
- Storyboard
- Sketch
- Mockup

### When Visual Communication Helps

Visual formats are useful when communicating:

- Layouts
- User flows
- Context of use
- Interface concepts

### When Text Is Better

Text-based formats may be better for:

- Business rules
- Pricing structures
- System behavior descriptions
- Feature explanations

Designers should ask:

- Could this be communicated **better with images or words**?

## Fidelity: How Detailed Should It Be?

Deliverables can range from **low fidelity to high fidelity**.

Fidelity spectrum:

- Rough sketch
- Wireframe
- Mockup
- Pixel-perfect design

### Benefits of Low Fidelity

Low-fidelity artifacts:

- Encourage **conceptual feedback**
- Keep discussions focused on **usability and functionality**
- Reduce wasted effort during early exploration

### Benefits of High Fidelity

High-fidelity artifacts are useful when evaluating:

- Visual design
- Branding
- Typography
- Layout and aesthetics

## Interactivity: How Interactive Should It Be?

Deliverables also vary in how interactive they are.

Interactivity spectrum:

- Static image
- Clickable wireframe
- Interactive prototype

### Choosing the Right Interactivity Level

- Static artifacts: Useful for **concept discussions**
- Clickable wireframes: Helpful for **structure and flow feedback**
- Interactive prototypes: Best for **usability testing**

More interactive artifacts provide **better behavioral feedback**, but require **more time to build**.

## Maintainability: How Easy Is It to Update?

Maintainability determines how easily a deliverable can be **updated over time**.

Examples:

- Printed persona posters: Hard to update
- Shared digital documents: Easy to maintain

### Why Maintainability Matters

Design artifacts often need to evolve as the product changes.

Examples:

- Personas may need updates
- Prototypes may need frequent revisions
- Documentation may need adjustments as features evolve

Choosing **maintainable formats** prevents future rework.

## Key Takeaway

### Deliverables Should Fit the Context

- There is **no fixed list of agile deliverables**.
- The right artifact depends on **audience, purpose, and stage of the project**.

### Agile Principle for Deliverables

- Deliverables should help teams **communicate, collaborate, and build better products**.
- Teams should regularly **evaluate whether their artifacts still serve their needs**.

### Avoid Waste

Overproducing deliverables can lead to wasted effort.

Examples of waste:

- Detailed prototypes for features that never ship
- Pixel-perfect mockups that require major redesign later

Using **lower fidelity or simpler deliverables when appropriate** allows teams to focus on solving real user problems.
