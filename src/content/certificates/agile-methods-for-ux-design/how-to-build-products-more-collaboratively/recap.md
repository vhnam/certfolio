## Collaboration in Agile Teams

### Understanding Team Input

Collaboration is central to agile teams. Designers receive suggestions from teammates with different expertise, including:

- Data insights
- Identified problems
- Proposed solutions
- Personal opinions

To evaluate suggestions effectively, designers should **investigate the motivation behind them** and determine whether they address the underlying problem.

## Sharing Work in Progress

Sharing **unfinished work early and often** helps teams:

- Receive feedback sooner
- Identify issues earlier
- Enable parallel work across disciplines

Early sharing accelerates development and improves collaboration between designers, engineers, and other stakeholders.

## Aligning Teams with an Initiative Document

An **Initiative Document** helps teams stay aligned on goals and expectations.

It typically includes:

- Company or product **objectives**
- How the initiative supports those objectives
- Available **resources**
- **Success criteria**
- The **time horizon** for completing the work

This shared understanding prevents confusion and keeps teams focused on the same outcomes.

## Agile Design Deliverables

Design deliverables in agile teams are similar to those in traditional processes but are often:

- **Lower fidelity**
- **Shared earlier**
- **Iterated frequently**

### Choosing the Right Deliverable

When deciding what deliverable to create, designers should consider:

- **Audience** – who will use the deliverable
- **Purpose** – what message it should communicate
- **Action** – what response or decision is needed from others

Deliverables should support collaboration rather than serve as final artifacts.

## Participatory Design

Agile teams benefit from involving **customers and users directly in the design process**.

Participatory design techniques include:

- **Card sorting**
- **Co-design sessions**
- Collaborative brainstorming

These methods help teams generate ideas with users and reduce the risk of building solutions that do not meet real needs.

## Product Roadmaps

### Purpose of a Roadmap

A **product roadmap** communicates how a team plans to achieve the **product vision and business objectives**.

It provides a high-level view of:

- Strategic direction
- Key initiatives
- Priorities over time

### Limitations of Gantt Charts

Many people associate roadmaps with **Gantt charts**, which show tasks on fixed timelines.

However, this format is poorly suited for agile teams because:

- Requirements frequently change
- Priorities evolve
- Teams need flexibility to adapt

## Roadmaps as Strategy Prototypes

Product roadmaps should be treated as **prototypes for product strategy**.

Teams should:

- Share roadmaps with stakeholders
- Gather feedback
- Continuously iterate

This approach ensures the team is moving in the right strategic direction.

## Lean Thinking in Roadmapping

Lean philosophy encourages teams to focus on **discovering the right problems to solve**.

One common framework is **Now / Next / Later**:

- **Now** – current priorities
- **Next** – upcoming opportunities
- **Later** – longer-term possibilities

This structure keeps teams flexible while maintaining strategic clarity.

## Managing Stakeholder Timelines

Sometimes stakeholders require timelines to coordinate activities such as marketing or operations.

A useful approach is to divide releases into:

- **Soft launch** – early release for testing and preparation
- **Hard launch** – official release with confirmed timelines

This method reduces dependency on development schedules while still supporting planning needs.
