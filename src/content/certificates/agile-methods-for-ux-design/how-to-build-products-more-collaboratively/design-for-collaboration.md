---
title: Designing for Collaboration in Agile Teams
---

## What Collaboration Means in Agile

### Definition

- Collaboration: Working with teammates from different roles to gather knowledge, requirements, and feedback throughout the design process.
- Agile collaboration: Continuous involvement of stakeholders while designing, rather than collecting all input upfront.

### Why Collaboration Matters

Different people in an organization have different knowledge that affects product design.

- Customer support: Understand common user problems and complaints.
- Engineers: Know technical constraints and implementation complexity.
- Product managers: Track product performance through metrics and data.
- Marketing and sales: Understand market positioning and customer acquisition.
- Legal teams: Ensure compliance with laws and regulations.

Ignoring these perspectives can lead to several problems:

- Designs that never get built
- Features changed drastically during development
- Legal or compliance issues
- Customer support overload due to poor user experience

## What Collaboration Is Not

### Not “Design by Committee”

- Design by committee: A process where every stakeholder has equal control over design decisions.
- Problem: Leads to unfocused products and compromises that damage the overall user experience.

Collaboration should not mean:

- Everyone decides everything
- All opinions are treated equally
- Designers simply implement stakeholder requests

### Not Being an Order Taker

- Designers and researchers still make design decisions.
- Feedback should inform decisions, not dictate them.

## Evaluating Feedback in Collaborative Design

### Types of Input

Not all feedback carries the same importance.

Examples:

- Preference-based feedback:
  - “I don’t like this image.”
- Requirement-based feedback:
  - “Checkout must support these payment options.”
- Product suggestions:
  - “We should add a feature for employers to rate job seekers.”

Treating all feedback equally can create **“FrankenProducts”**—products made of conflicting ideas that damage the user experience.

### Designer’s Responsibility

When receiving feedback, designers must evaluate:

- Whether the feedback improves the overall user experience
- Whether it introduces trade-offs
- Whether the request aligns with product goals

## Understanding the “Why” Behind Requirements

### Investigating Stakeholder Requests

When someone gives a requirement, designers should investigate its purpose.

Examples:

- Payment requirement:
  Understand whether all payment methods must launch immediately or can be added later.

- Visual request (e.g., a large red button):
  Determine whether the real concern is visibility of the call-to-action.

### Key Principle

- Requirements should be understood in terms of **goals**, not just instructions.

Designers should ask:

- Why is this requirement important?
- What problem is it trying to solve?
- Is there a better design solution that achieves the same goal?

## Continuous Collaboration in Agile

### Agile vs. Waterfall Input Gathering

- Waterfall: Requirements are gathered upfront before design begins.
- Agile: Input and feedback are collected continuously while designing.

This approach allows teams to:

- Identify problems earlier
- Adapt designs quickly
- Prevent major redesign later

## The Role of Designers in Collaborative Teams

### Designer Responsibilities

Even in collaborative environments, designers remain responsible for:

- Synthesizing feedback
- Balancing stakeholder needs
- Protecting the overall user experience
- Designing usable and useful products

### Core Mindset

- Seek input actively.
- Evaluate feedback critically.
- Focus on delivering a product that works for users and the business.

## Key Takeaway

### Collaboration Benefits

- Access to broader expertise within the organization
- Better understanding of technical, legal, and business constraints
- Fewer surprises during development
- Products that are more likely to ship successfully

Working collaboratively helps teams design solutions that meet both **user needs** and **business requirements**.
