title: Planning for the Future and the Problem With Roadmaps
---

## The Problem with Traditional Roadmaps

### Feature-Based Planning

Traditional product roadmaps often resemble **Gantt charts**, where teams commit to building specific features on a fixed timeline months or even years in advance.

This approach creates several problems:

- Estimates are often **inaccurate**
- Priorities **change over time**
- Teams may build **outdated or unnecessary features**

Instead of solving the most important problems, teams may end up **delivering what was planned long ago**.

### Parkinson’s Law and Development Pressure

Another issue with traditional timelines is **Parkinson’s Law**—work expands to fill the time allocated to it.

Consequences include:

- Slow progress due to **overly long timelines**
- **Developer stress** caused by rigid deadlines
- Accumulation of **technical debt** from rushed solutions

These problems make traditional planning poorly suited for agile environments.

## Agile Approach to Roadmaps

### Focus on Problems, Not Features

Agile teams should avoid committing to detailed feature lists far in advance.

Instead, roadmaps should focus on:

- **Problems to solve**
- **Outcomes to achieve**
- **opportunities to explore**

This allows teams to remain flexible while learning from research, experimentation, and user feedback.

### Roadmaps as Strategic Direction

In agile teams, a roadmap should communicate:

- The **direction of the product**
- The **initiatives being explored**
- The **priorities for solving user problems**

It should **guide discovery**, not act as a rigid commitment.

## The Now–Next–Later Roadmap

A common agile-friendly roadmap format is the **Now–Next–Later framework**.

### Now

Work that the team is **actively exploring or building**.

Characteristics:

- High confidence
- Clear understanding of the problem
- Detailed implementation work underway

### Next

Initiatives that are **likely to be addressed soon**.

Characteristics:

- Problems are identified
- Solutions are still being explored
- Priorities may shift depending on learning

### Later

Longer-term opportunities that are **recognized but not yet defined**.

Characteristics:

- Early-stage ideas
- Limited research or validation
- Flexible and open to change

This structure keeps teams aligned while maintaining **agility and adaptability**.

## Roadmaps as Strategy Prototypes

Agile roadmaps can function like **prototypes for product strategy**.

Just as designers test interface ideas through prototypes, teams can use roadmaps to:

- Explore **strategic assumptions**
- Validate **which problems matter most**
- Adjust priorities based on **new insights**

As teams learn from experiments, research, and user feedback, the roadmap should **evolve continuously**.

## The Takeaway

Agile planning focuses on **learning and adaptability rather than rigid commitments**.

Effective agile roadmaps:

- Prioritize **problems and outcomes instead of features**
- Use flexible structures such as **Now–Next–Later**
- Support **continuous discovery and iteration**

By treating roadmaps as **guides rather than promises**, teams can focus their efforts on solving the **most valuable user problems**.
