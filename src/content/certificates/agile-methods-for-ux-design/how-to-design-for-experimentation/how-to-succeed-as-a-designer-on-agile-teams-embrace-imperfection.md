title: "How to Succeed as a Designer on Agile Teams: Embrace Imperfection"
---

## The Core Challenge: Perfection vs. Speed

Agile teams prioritize shipping working software quickly to gather feedback. With short cycles (often 1–2 weeks), designers can feel intense pressure to deliver polished, pixel-perfect work at unrealistic speeds.

The problem:

- Perfection takes time.
- Agile demands learning through release.
- What designers think is “perfect” is rarely what users define as valuable.

In agile environments, striving for perfection too early often leads to burnout and wasted effort.

## The Mindset Shift: From Perfect to Good Enough

Instead of chasing an abstract idea of “perfect,” designers should aim for:

- **Good enough to solve a real problem**
- **Good enough to release safely**
- **Good enough to learn from**

Good enough does **not** mean:

- Broken
- Careless
- Poor quality

It means sufficient to gather meaningful feedback and improve through iteration.

## The Potato Analogy: Satisficing in Practice

The cooking analogy explains this clearly:

- Cutting potatoes unevenly ruins the meal (too low quality).
- Carving each potato into geometric perfection wastes time (over-optimization).
- Cutting them consistently and reasonably well gets dinner on the table.

The goal isn’t perfect potatoes — it’s a good dinner served on time.

In design, the goal isn’t flawless mockups — it’s a usable solution that delivers value and reveals what to improve next.

This approach is called **satisficing**: aiming for an adequate, workable solution instead of an idealized one.

## Why Iteration Matters

Agile teams improve products through repeated cycles:

1. Ship something usable.
2. Observe how people use it.
3. Gather feedback.
4. Improve it.

You only truly understand what “better” means **after** something is in users’ hands.

Without iteration:

- “Good enough” becomes dangerous.
- Teams ship once and never refine.
- Designers lose trust in the process.

With iteration:

- Quality increases over time.
- Stress decreases.
- Learning compounds.

## How Designers Can Thrive in Agile Teams

To succeed:

- Let go of early-stage perfectionism.
- Focus on solving the core user problem first.
- Define “good enough” collaboratively with the team.
- Commit to revisiting and improving features later.
- Avoid overbuilding details users may never notice or value.

The key is not lowering standards — it’s shifting when and how refinement happens.

## The Takeaway

Perfection is unknowable before release.

Aiming for “good enough” allows teams to:

- Ship faster
- Learn sooner
- Iterate smarter
- Reduce wasted effort

Design isn’t about making the perfect potato.
It’s about serving dinner — and making it better next time.
