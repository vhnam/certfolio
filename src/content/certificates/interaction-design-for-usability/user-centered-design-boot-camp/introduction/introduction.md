- Early experience: Built software on time-sharing systems and observed users in person, seeing their reactions and problems directly
- Interest in HCI: Began in late 1980s, took HCI course around 1990–1991
- Key influence: Don Norman's book _The Design of Everyday Things_ (originally _The Psychology of Everyday Things_)

## Humans and Technology

### Key Challenges in Human-Technology Interaction

- People do not behave the way designers often assume
- Many interactions are subconscious with no direct awareness
- Perception is not always accurate – brains take shortcuts and can be fooled
- Growing importance of designing for aging populations (Baby Boomers shifting demographics)

### Visual Perception Limitations

- **Visual silencing**: Brain limits processing when overloaded (example: focusing on central dot while surrounding dots change color and rotate; color changes appear to slow dramatically)
  - Relevant to complex interfaces and potentially virtual reality
- **Banner blindness** (Benway & Lane, late 1990s):
  - Users often ignore or overlook information in banner-like areas
  - Success rate dropped from ~90% (control) to 60% when target info was in a banner
  - Caused by mental labeling of interface components (banners, ads, headings) as ignorable

### Age-Related Changes in Perception

- Vision declines noticeably from mid-40s (onset of presbyopia)
- Key changes by age 80 vs. age 20:
  - Reduced ability to focus on near objects
  - Loss of contrast sensitivity
  - Changes in spatial frequency perception
- By 2050: Number of people aged 65+ expected to roughly equal those aged 0–14 (major demographic shift)
- Design implications: Must accommodate older users with reduced vision, mobility, and contrast perception

### Device Proliferation and Design Challenges

- High penetration of computing devices (especially mobile, laptops, tablets)
- Additional devices: e-readers, fitness bands, smart watches, VR headsets
- Common issues with mobile websites (especially on 7–10 inch tablets):
  - Poor usability, pop-ups that cannot be dismissed, content cut off
  - Trend toward apps rather than responsive websites

### Empathy Tools in Other Industries

- **Third Age Suit** (Loughborough University / Ford): Stiffeners on joints + yellowed/scratched goggles to simulate older adult movement and vision
- **Age Explorer Suit**: Similar simulation used in retail, product design, and domestic settings (includes sound muffling)
- Purpose: Help (often young) designers experience limitations of aging users

## The Action Cycle (Adapted from Don Norman)

Process describing how users interact with technology (Action → Response).

### Stages

1. **User Goal**: Desired change in the world or system (e.g., turn on lights, find stamp price)
2. **Action**: User decides and performs actions based on perceived affordances
3. **System Response**: Technology provides feedback (visible or otherwise)
4. **Perception & Evaluation**: User observes result and decides if goal is met or further action needed

### Goal Mapping Questions (for analyzing usability issues)

1. How do users know their goal is attainable?
2. How do users know what to do? (maps to action)
3. How will users know they've done the right thing? (perception)
4. How will users know they've attained their goal? (evaluation)

### Examples

- **Light switch / room lights**: Simple cycle with clear affordances and immediate feedback
- **Finding postage stamp price** on postal website: Users often stuck early because no clear path from goal to action (no obvious links or information on homepage)
