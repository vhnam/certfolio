## Visualization Types in UI Design

### Sketches

- Sketches: Quick, rough visualizations used to explore design ideas.
- Purpose: Generate ideas and experiment without committing to details.
- Characteristics:
  - Few or no features
  - No functionality
  - Low similarity to the final product
  - Minimal aesthetic refinement

Sketches are exploratory and noncommittal. They help designers think through problems and compare multiple ideas quickly.

### Wireframes

- Wireframes: Structured visual guides that show layout and interface components.
- Purpose: Refine design structure and communicate how elements should be arranged.

Wireframes typically include:

- User terminology and labels
- Placement of components
- Interface structure
- Clear screen layouts

Characteristics:

- More features than sketches
- Greater visual similarity to the final interface
- Minimal aesthetics
- Usually static (little or no functionality)

Wireframes often evolve directly from sketches and serve as a bridge to prototypes.

### Prototypes

- Prototypes: Interactive representations of a design used to simulate the final product.
- Purpose: Test interaction behavior and validate design decisions.

#### Low-Fidelity Prototypes

- Low-fidelity prototypes: Early interactive models used for exploration and testing.
- Characteristics:
  - Limited functionality
  - Simple visuals
  - Rough appearance

These prototypes encourage feedback because they appear unfinished.

#### High-Fidelity Prototypes

- High-fidelity prototypes: Detailed, interactive models closely resembling the final product.

Characteristics:

- High visual similarity to the final design
- More realistic interaction behavior
- Greater functional depth

High-fidelity prototypes help validate user expectations and refine details before development.

## Four Dimensions of Prototype Fidelity

![Chart showing the four dimensions of prototyping for sketches, wireframes, lo-fidelity, and hi-fidelity prototypes. Explained below.](../../../../assets/images/object-oriented-ui-design-build-interfaces-users-love/from-sketch-to-build-bring-your-designs-to-life-with-your-team/object-oriented-ui-design-illustrations-four-dimentions-of-prototyping.avif)

Bob Virzi and colleagues defined four dimensions used to evaluate prototypes.

- Breadth of features: Number of features included in the prototype.
- Degree of functionality: How much of the interaction actually works.
- Similarity of interaction: How closely interactions match the final product.
- Aesthetic refinement: How polished the visual appearance is.

These dimensions help designers determine the appropriate level of fidelity for a given stage of design.

## Visualization Selection Strategy

![Visualization Selection Guide (text version below).](../../../../assets/images/object-oriented-ui-design-build-interfaces-users-love/from-sketch-to-build-bring-your-designs-to-life-with-your-team/object-oriented-ui-design-illustrations-visualization-selection-matrix.avif)

Choosing the right visualization depends on the complexity and uncertainty of the design problem.

### Simple Interactions

- Well-understood: Skip directly to prototypes.
- Some uncertainty: Start with wireframes.
- High uncertainty: Sketches → Wireframes.

### Complex Interactions

- Well-understood: Wireframes → Prototype.
- Some uncertainty: Sketches → Wireframes → Prototype.
- High uncertainty: Multiple sketch iterations → Wireframes → Prototype.

### Critical User Flows

- Well-understood: Wireframes → Prototype.
- Some uncertainty: Sketches → Wireframes → Prototype.
- High uncertainty: Extensive sketching → Multiple wireframe iterations → Prototype.

Progressive refinement helps reduce risk while improving design quality.

## Design Approach

Effective UI design follows a gradual refinement process.

1. Start low fidelity: Use sketches and simple wireframes when uncertainty is high.
2. Iterate quickly: Explore ideas and gather feedback early.
3. Increase fidelity: Move toward detailed wireframes and prototypes as clarity improves.
4. Test interactions: Use prototypes to validate usability and expectations.

## Key Takeaways

- Visualization methods: Sketches, wireframes, and prototypes each serve different design stages.
- Sketches: Best for idea generation and early exploration.
- Wireframes: Define structure, terminology, and layout.
- Prototypes: Enable interaction testing and realistic evaluation.
- Fidelity decisions: Should be guided by uncertainty, complexity, and testing needs.
- Design principle: Focus on progress and learning rather than premature visual polish.
