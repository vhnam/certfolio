## Design Maps

### Definition

- Design maps: A collaborative visualization tool that connects user research, stories of use, and design artifacts into a single structured view.
- Purpose: Align user needs, system constraints, and implementation decisions.
- Origin: Introduced by John Pruitt and Tamara Adlin in _The Persona Lifecycle_.

Design maps bring together the **who, what, and how** of design so teams share the same understanding before development begins.

### Core Elements of Design Maps

#### Who: Personas

- Personas: Represent the key users the system is designed for.
- Persona-weighted feature matrix: Helps prioritize features based on the needs of important personas.

Personas define **who the design is for**.

#### What: Stories of Use

- Persona stories: Descriptions of the activities users perform in the system.
- Scenarios: Rich, contextual narratives explaining how users interact with the system.
- Constraints: Limitations affecting implementation, such as platforms, guidelines, or regulations.

Stories of use explain **what users want to achieve**.

#### How: Design Artifacts

Design artifacts show how the interface will support the user journey.

Examples include:

- Sketches
- Wireframes
- Prototypes (low-fidelity or high-fidelity)

These artifacts visualize **how the system works in practice**.

## Structure of a Design Map

### User Steps

The top row of a design map represents the **sequence of user actions**.

- Steps describe the activities the persona performs.
- They form the backbone of the design map.

### Supporting Notes

Additional notes provide collaborative insights and improvements.

Common note types:

- Steps: Actions taken by the persona.
- Questions: Issues or uncertainties that require clarification.
- Comments: Observations from team members.
- Ideas: Suggestions or alternative approaches.

These notes allow the team to discuss and refine the design before implementation.

### Visual Organization

Design maps often use **color-coded sticky notes**.

Example color system:

- Blue: Steps
- Yellow: Questions
- Green: Comments or assumptions
- Purple/Pink: Ideas

Important guideline:

- Do not rely only on color; use **labels or shapes** to ensure accessibility and clarity.

## Creating Design Maps

### Basic Process

1. Define the story of use: Identify the persona and the scenario.
2. List user steps: Break the story into sequential actions.
3. Add notes: Include questions, comments, and ideas.
4. Attach design artifacts: Link sketches, wireframes, or prototypes.
5. Resolve issues: Address questions and constraints before implementation.

Design maps evolve through collaboration and discussion.

### Collaborative Workshops

Design maps are often created in team workshops.

Typical workflow:

- Use sticky notes on a large sheet of paper.
- Team members contribute ideas simultaneously.
- Questions and comments are discussed and refined.

This process improves communication and reduces misunderstandings.

## Tools for Design Maps

### Paper-Based Maps

Paper design maps are often built using:

- Flip chart paper
- Butcher paper
- Craft paper rolls
- Sticky notes in multiple colors

These are ideal for **workshops and in-person collaboration**.

### Digital Tools

Teams can also build design maps using collaborative software.

Common tools:

- Figma (FigJam boards)
- Miro

Typical workflow in digital tools:

1. Import steps from a spreadsheet.
2. Convert text into sticky notes.
3. Color-code notes by type.
4. Arrange notes to represent the user journey.

Digital tools support remote collaboration and asynchronous updates.

## Enhancing Design Maps with Visualizations

Design maps can include **visual cues** to clarify the user experience.

Examples:

- Simple sketches
- Screen thumbnails
- Simplified UI views

Benefits:

- Connect user actions to interface behavior
- Help developers understand implementation details
- Maintain a shared understanding of the design

The level of detail depends on the team’s needs.

## Design Map Collaboration Principles

Effective design maps support team communication.

Key practices:

- Encourage team members to add comments and questions.
- Resolve uncertainties before implementation.
- Keep the map readable and accessible.
- Maintain a shared understanding across designers and developers.

Design maps reduce confusion during handoffs and improve development efficiency.

## Key Takeaways

- Design maps: Integrate **personas, stories of use, and design artifacts**.
- They represent the **who (personas), what (stories), and how (design solutions)**.
- Maps organize user steps along with **questions, comments, and ideas**.
- They improve collaboration and prevent misunderstandings between designers and developers.
- Tools like **Figma, Miro, or paper workshops** support collaborative creation.
- Adding visualizations helps connect user actions with interface behavior.
- Design maps keep teams aligned and ensure designs remain grounded in real user needs.
