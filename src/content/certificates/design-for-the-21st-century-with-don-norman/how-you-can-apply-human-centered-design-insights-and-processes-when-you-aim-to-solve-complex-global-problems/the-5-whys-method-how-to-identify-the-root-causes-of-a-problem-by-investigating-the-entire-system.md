## What Is the 5 Whys Method

### Definition

- 5 Whys Method: A root cause analysis technique that repeatedly asks “Why?” to uncover deeper causes behind a problem.
- Root Cause: The underlying condition that generates recurring symptoms.
- Symptom: A visible issue that results from deeper systemic problems.

### Core Principle

- Keep Asking Why: Do not stop at the first explanation.
- Go Beyond Symptoms: Fixing surface issues alone allows the problem to return.
- Think Systemically: Recognize that causes exist within interconnected systems.

## How the 5 Whys Works

### Step-by-Step Process

1. Identify the Problem: Clearly state the observed issue.
2. Ask Why: Determine the immediate cause.
3. Ask Why Again: Investigate the cause behind the previous answer.
4. Continue Iteratively: Repeat until reaching a level where meaningful intervention is possible.
5. Act on the Lowest Actionable Level: Address causes that can realistically be changed.

### Important Clarifications

- Not Literally Five: The number five emphasizes persistence, not a strict rule.
- No Absolute Bottom: You may never reach a final, ultimate cause.
- Action-Oriented Depth: Stop when you reach a level where effective change can occur.

## Example: Toyota Assembly Line

### Symptom

- Water Pump Not Fastened Properly: Discovered later in production.

### Symptom Fix

- Refasten the Pump: Solves the immediate issue only.

### Root Cause Exploration

- Why was it not fastened properly?
- Why did the worker make a mistake?
- Why was there confusion between tools or parts?
- Why were pumps and engines mismatched?

### Possible Systemic Solutions

- Color-Code Components: Match pumps and engines visually.
- Improve Tool Design: Reduce confusion between tools.
- Adjust Upstream Processes: Modify earlier stages to prevent recurrence.

## Limits of the 5 Whys in Complex Systems

### Common Criticism

- Single Cause Assumption: The method may imply one root cause.
- Complex Reality: Many problems have multiple interacting causes.

### Systems Perspective

- Multiple Root Causes: Complex issues often result from several contributing factors.
- Interconnected Failures: Removing any one contributing factor may prevent the outcome.
- Holistic Analysis: Must examine the entire system, not isolated elements.

## Example: Aviation Safety

### Public Expectation

- Simple Explanation: “Pilot error” or “mechanical failure.”
- Desire for Single Cause: Humans prefer clear cause-and-effect stories.

### Systemic Investigation

- Detailed Analysis: National safety boards examine incidents for years.
- Multiple Contributing Factors: Equipment, procedures, environment, human actions.
- No Single Cause: Several conditions combine to create failure.

### Outcome

- System-Level Improvements: Addressing multiple root causes increases safety.
- Long-Term Effectiveness: Commercial aviation has achieved extremely low fatality rates.
- Evidence of Systems Thinking: Systematic investigation prevents recurring accidents.

## How to Use the 5 Whys in Practice

### Example: Plane Crash Scenario

- Q1: Why did the plane crash? → It was in an uncontrolled dive.
- Q2: Why didn’t the pilot recover? → Failed to initiate recovery.
- Q3: Why? → Possibly unconscious or oxygen deprived.
- Q4: Why? → Further investigation required.

### Key Insight

- Partial Analysis: Initial answers are only part of the investigation.
- Continuous Inquiry: Additional layers must be examined.
- System Awareness: Causes exist within broader technical and social systems.

## Implications for Designers

### Root Cause Mindset

- Solve Both Symptom and Cause: Immediate relief is necessary but insufficient.
- Expect Multiple Causes: Rarely is there one single explanation.
- Apply Systems Thinking: View problems within broader socio-technical systems.
- Commit to Long-Term Solutions: Sustainable change requires addressing interconnected causes.
