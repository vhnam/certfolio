## The Human Mind and System Complexity

### Evolutionary Bias Toward Simple Causality

- Simple Cause and Effect: Humans are wired to understand immediate, visible results (e.g., throw a rock → see it hit).
- Immediate Feedback: We learn effectively when results follow actions quickly.
- Visible Impact: Direct consequences help us adjust behavior.

Quoted verbatim:

> “The human mind is not designed to understand the complexities of all these systems.”
> — Don Norman

## Complex Socio-Technical Systems

### Definition

- Socio-Technical Systems: Interconnected systems involving both societal and technological components.
- Interdependence: Actions affect multiple elements that influence each other.
- Systemic Origin of Problems: Issues arise from interactions within the whole system, not isolated parts.

### Why They Are Hard to Understand

- Time Delays: Large gaps between action and consequence (e.g., climate change, smoking).
- Scale Mismatch: Individual actions seem insignificant compared to global systems.
- Invisible Impact: Pollution or environmental damage may not produce immediate visible effects.
- Non-Linear Effects: Small changes may have no visible effect until a threshold triggers major change.
- Multiple Feedback Loops: Actions influence chains of events that eventually circle back in complex ways.

## Feedback Loops

### Definition

- Feedback Loop: A process where the results of an action return to influence future behavior.

### Immediate vs. Delayed Feedback

- Immediate Feedback: Enables learning and improvement (e.g., missing a basketball shot and adjusting).
- Delayed Feedback: Makes it difficult to link cause and effect (e.g., climate change, lung cancer from smoking).
- Feed-Forward and Non-Linearity: Effects may accumulate and suddenly create dramatic change.

## Everything Is a System

### Core Principle

- Everything Is a System: Problems must be understood as part of larger interconnected systems.
- Root Cause Awareness: Without systemic thinking, designers risk solving symptoms rather than underlying causes.
- Larger Picture Thinking: Effective solutions require understanding interactions, delays, and systemic structures.

### Examples of Complex Systems

- Climate Change: Effects unfold over decades.
- Public Health: Smoking’s impact appears years later.
- Education: Benefits of teaching children may only appear decades later.
- Global Challenges: Hunger, clean water, and sustainability require systemic understanding.

## Implications for Designers

### Systems Thinking

- Think Beyond Immediate Results: Look for delayed and indirect consequences.
- Identify Feedback Structures: Map how actions influence other parts of the system.
- Accept Complexity: Recognize that socio-technical systems are inherently difficult to grasp.
- Avoid Oversimplification: Simple fixes may fail if systemic interactions are ignored.
