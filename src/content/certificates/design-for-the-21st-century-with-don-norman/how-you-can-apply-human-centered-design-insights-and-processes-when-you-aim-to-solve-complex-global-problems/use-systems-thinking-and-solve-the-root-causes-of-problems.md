## Symptoms vs. Root Causes

### Core Insight

- Root Cause: The fundamental underlying issue within a system.
- Symptom: A visible surface problem that results from deeper systemic causes.
- Systems Thinking: Viewing problems as parts of interconnected socio-technical systems.

Quoted verbatim:

> “You have to look at everything as a system and you have to make sure you're getting at the underlying root causes.”
> — Don Norman

### Why First Solutions Fail

- Surface Fixes: Initial ideas often address symptoms, not root causes.
- Problem Replacement: Solving one visible issue may create another elsewhere.
- Oversimplification: Simple solutions rarely match complex systemic problems.

## Example 1: Electric Vehicles and Pollution

### Surface View

- Electric Vehicles: No tailpipe emissions.
- Assumption: Switching to EVs eliminates pollution.

### Systemic View

- Energy Source: Electricity may be generated from fossil fuels.
- Transmission Loss: Significant energy is lost during long-distance transmission.
- Pollution Shift: Emissions may move from cities to power plants rather than disappear.
- Net Impact: Without systemic change, pollution may remain or even increase.

## Example 2: Water Consumption

### Symptom-Focused Approach

- Household Efficiency: Shorter showers and water-saving devices.
- Immediate Benefit: Some reduction in domestic water use.

### Systemic Analysis

- Major Usage: Most water is used in agriculture and industry, not households.
- Industrial Waste: Water often not reclaimed or reused.
- Agricultural Loss:
  - Open Ditches: High evaporation rates.
  - Flood Irrigation: Significant waste.
  - Drip Irrigation: More efficient but expensive.
- Trade-Offs: Efficient systems may introduce high financial costs.

## Example 3: Cholera and Sanitation

### Surface Solution

- Education on Sanitation: Encourage better hygiene practices.

### Root Cause Perspective

- Infrastructure Gap: Lack of running water and sewage systems.
- Social Factors: Homelessness may prevent sanitation compliance.
- Systemic Interdependence: Solving health issues may require addressing housing and infrastructure.

## The 4 Principles of Human-Centered Design: Solve the Right Problem

### Solve the Right Problem

- Reframe the Problem: The initial problem statement is often incomplete.
- Look Beyond the Surface: Identify deeper systemic causes.
- Integrate Systems Thinking: Recognize interconnected parts and trade-offs.

Quoted verbatim:

> “One of my rules in consulting is simple: never solve the problem I am asked to solve. Why such a counterintuitive rule? Because, invariably, the problem I am asked to solve is not the real, fundamental, root problem. It is usually a symptom.”
> — Don Norman, _The Design of Everyday Things_

## Design Thinking and Root Cause Analysis

### Definition

- Design Thinking: An iterative and expansive approach that reframes problems and explores multiple solutions before converging.

### Key Practices

1. Reframe the Problem: Treat the original brief as a suggestion, not a final definition.
2. Use the Five Whys: Investigate deeper layers of causation.
3. Delay Convergence: Resist jumping to immediate solutions.
4. Explore Broadly: Consider multiple alternative solutions.
5. Iterate: Continuously refine understanding of both problem and solution.

### Mindset

- Challenges as Opportunities: Complex problems require patience and curiosity.
- Avoid Obvious Answers: If a solution seems immediate and simple, it likely ignores systemic complexity.
- Think Systemically: Address interacting causes rather than isolated symptoms.
