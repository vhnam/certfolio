## Applying the People-Centered Principle

### Core Idea

- People-Centered Design: A principle of Human-Centered Design that places people—their capabilities, context, and knowledge—at the core of decision-making.
- Design With, Not For: Designers should collaborate with communities rather than impose external solutions.
- Local Capabilities: Solutions must align with what local people can maintain, adapt, and sustain.

Quoted verbatim:

> “When you design, you have to understand what the capabilities are of the people you're designing for.”
> — Don Norman

## Pitfalls of Outsider-Driven Design

### Short-Term Engagement

- Limited Exposure: Visiting a community for weeks or even months is insufficient to understand cultural and systemic realities.
- Surface Understanding: Early impressions are often incomplete or misleading.
- Cultural Blind Spots: Real issues often emerge only after years of immersion.

### Failure of External Solutions

- Award-Winning but Ineffective: Solutions may win design contests but fail in real-world implementation.
- Maintenance Issues: When products break, local communities may lack the skills or resources to fix them.
- Dependency Creation: Outside experts solving problems can undermine local self-sufficiency.

## Power and Insights of the Local People

### Why Local Communities Must Lead

- Local Knowledge: Communities deeply understand their own problems, social systems, and cultural norms.
- Lived Experience: True understanding requires years of immersion, not brief visits.
- Practical Capabilities: Local people know what they can realistically build, maintain, and repair.
- Existing Innovation: Many communities are already creatively addressing their own challenges.
- Contextual Awareness: They understand hidden barriers and unspoken realities outsiders often miss.
- Sustainable Ownership: Solutions driven by locals are more likely to be accepted and adopted.
- Self-Sufficiency: Empowerment builds resilience rather than dependency.

## Community-Based Design

### Teach, Don’t Replace

- “Teach to Fish” Principle: Empower communities to solve their own problems sustainably.
- Self-Sustaining Systems: Long-term impact requires local ownership and knowledge transfer.

### Leveraging Local Creativity

- Observe and Learn: Designers should watch what communities are already doing.
- Help When Asked: Provide expertise only when invited.
- Knowledge Sharing: Introduce techniques or examples from other contexts when useful.
- Amplification: Share successful local solutions with broader audiences.

## Co-Design as a Better Approach

### Co-Design

- Co-Design: A collaborative process where the community drives the project and designers support.
- Designer as Facilitator: Offer tools, techniques, and cross-context insights.
- Community-Driven Projects: Local people define priorities and direction.

### Outcomes of Co-Design

- Higher Adoption: Solutions are more likely to be accepted and used.
- Cultural Fit: Designs align with local practices and values.
- Sustainable Impact: Communities retain control and capability.

## Rethinking Design Education

### Training Implications

- Current Model: Schools often train designers to independently “solve” global problems.
- Needed Shift: Education must emphasize long-term engagement and collaboration.
- Systems Awareness: Designers must recognize complexity in global challenges.
