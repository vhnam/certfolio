## The Problem with the Word “Failure”

### Cultural Attitudes Toward Failure

- Negative stigma: In some cultures, failure is life-defining and unrecoverable.
- Psychological burden: Individuals may internalize product failure as personal failure.
- Misinterpretation: A product not selling is not necessarily a designer’s failure.

### A Better Framing

- “Fail fast, fail frequently” (IDEO philosophy): Encourages experimentation.
- Don’s refinement: Replace it with **“Learn fast, learn frequently.”**
- Key shift: Focus on learning rather than labeling outcomes as failure.

Quoted verbatim:

> “If you want to be innovative, you have to be failing if you will, or maybe learning all the time.”
> — Don Norman

## Innovation Requires Risk

### If You’re Not Failing, You’re Not Trying

- Innovation requires pushing boundaries.
- Safe ideas rarely lead to breakthroughs.
- Extreme exploration increases chances of both failure and success.

### Scientists’ Mindset

- Scientists do not say “I failed.”
- They say: “That didn’t work.”
- Learning from experiments is progress, even without reaching the goal.

## Failure as Experience

### Startups and Entrepreneurship

- High failure rate: Most startups do not succeed.
- Investor perspective:
  - First failure: Expected.
  - Second attempt: More promising due to experience gained.
- Failure signals learning and resilience.

### Skill Development Analogy

- Athletes (gymnasts, skiers, skateboarders):
  - Spend thousands of hours practicing.
  - Repeatedly fail before mastery.
- Every successful performance is built on many prior attempts.

## Smart Failure: Break Big Risks into Small Experiments

### Avoid Catastrophic Failure

- Don’t risk a billion-dollar project all at once.
- Divide large projects into smaller, manageable experiments.

### Iterative Learning Process

1. Break down the big challenge.
2. Test small components.
3. Learn from each attempt.
4. Build toward larger success.

Smaller failures:

- Cost less.
- Provide insight.
- Increase likelihood of long-term success.

## The Design Mindset for Innovation

### Core Principles

- Embrace learning over perfection.
- View setbacks as data.
- Think in systems.
- Focus on real issues affecting people.
- Continuously refine ideas through iteration.

## The Bigger Vision

- Innovation depends on continuous experimentation.
- Repeated learning leads to greater long-term success.
- A culture that values learning over failure unlocks transformative possibilities.
