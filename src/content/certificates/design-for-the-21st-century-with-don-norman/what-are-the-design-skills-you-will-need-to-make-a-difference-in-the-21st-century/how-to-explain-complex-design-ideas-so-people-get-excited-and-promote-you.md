title: "How to Explain Complex Design Ideas So People Get Excited and Promote You"

## The Paradox of Expertise

Don Norman highlights a significant hurdle for professionals: **The more you know about a subject, the harder it becomes to explain it to others.** \* **The Abstraction Trap:** Experts and professors often speak in abstract principles because they apply to many things. However, for a non-expert audience, these abstractions are often impossible to grasp without context.

- **Over-explaining:** The harder you try to explain a complex topic, the more likely you are to confuse your audience. This "Curse of Knowledge" makes it difficult to remember what it was like _not_ to know the subject.

## The "Examples First" Rule

To bridge the gap between your expertise and your audience’s understanding, you must flip the traditional way of teaching.

- **Start with the Concrete:** Before diving into history or theory, provide practical examples. If you are talking about art history, show paintings first. If you are talking about usability, show a live demo of a great app or a massive "usability fail."
- **Build Toward Abstraction:** Once the audience understands a few concrete examples, show them the **abstraction** that ties those examples together. This creates a mental framework they can actually use.

## The "In Other Words" Technique

Don suggests an iterative approach to communication, similar to how we refine designs.

- **Iterative Clarification:** Start by writing out your long, detailed explanation. Then, force yourself to simplify it.
- **The Secret Phrase:** Look for where you’ve written "In other words..." and explained the concept simply. Don’s advice: **Keep the "in other words" part and throw away everything else.**
- **The Physical Pivot:** If you get stuck while writing, stand up and walk around. Point at a wall or a mirror and say out loud, _"Look, what I'm trying to say is..."_ Usually, what you blurt out in frustration is the clearest version of your idea.

## Communication as Motivation

The primary goal of a presentation or a pitch isn't always to transfer every detail—it’s to transfer **excitement.**

- **The Role of Lectures:** While not the best for learning deep details, lectures are excellent for motivating an audience.
- **Convey Your Passion:** If you are genuinely motivated by your topic, your audience will be too. Your goal is to get them excited enough to go out and study the topic on their own.

## Why This Matters for Designers

Clear communication is the bridge that allows a designer’s vision to become reality. You need these skills to:

- **Translate** design requirements for engineers.
- **Persuade** management to support a controversial or bold change.
- **Pitch** a design to a client or at a conference.
- **Share** user research in a way that makes the product team care.
