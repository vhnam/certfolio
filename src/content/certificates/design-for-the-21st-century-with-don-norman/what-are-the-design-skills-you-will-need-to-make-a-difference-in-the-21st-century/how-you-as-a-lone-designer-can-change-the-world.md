---
title: How You as a Lone Designer Can Change the World
---

## The Core Problem: You Can’t Do It Alone

Don Norman emphasizes that 21st-century societal challenges—such as environmental damage, inequality, hunger, and education—are too complex for any single designer to solve. These are systemic problems that require collaboration, organizations, and coordinated effort.

The real question is not _“Should designers work on societal issues?”_ but rather:

> **“As one designer, what can you realistically do?”**

The answer depends on who you are and where you are in your career.

## If You’re a New Designer

If you’ve just graduated and want to make a difference:

- Look for **organizations already working on societal problems**.
- Join NGOs, community groups, or mission-driven initiatives.
- Volunteer if necessary to gain experience and prove your value.

You may earn less money than in commercial roles, but you gain:

- Real-world impact
- Deep experience
- Personal satisfaction

This is a practical entry point into meaningful work.

## If You’re an Experienced Designer

You have two main options:

### Join Larger Mission-Driven Organizations

Find established institutions already addressing global challenges. Working within them allows you to:

- Learn how complex systems operate
- Build credibility
- Contribute at scale

You might stay long-term or later launch your own initiative.

### Change Your Current Company From Within

This path is harder.

If you want to shift your company toward socially responsible work, you must:

- Gain authority
- Understand the full business system
- Learn finance, marketing, sales, operations
- Understand profitability constraints

Companies must remain profitable to survive. If you want them to address societal issues, you must show **how they can do good while still making money**.

This requires:

- Systems thinking
- Business literacy
- Strategic influence

You cannot change direction without power—and you cannot gain power without understanding how the company truly works.

## Consider a Different Kind of Company

Don highlights the idea of **B Corp** in the United States.

Unlike traditional corporations (which prioritize shareholder profit), a B Corp legally commits to serving:

- The environment
- Customers
- Employees
- The broader community

Many conventional companies declare loyalty primarily to shareholders. Don openly criticizes this philosophy as morally flawed.

If you want to work on societal challenges, consider organizations that are structurally designed to support that mission.

## The Hard Truth

There is no easy path.

- Societal challenges are complex.
- Organizations resist change.
- Profit pressures are real.

But Don argues that addressing these issues is **one of the most important things designers can do**.

## The Takeaway

To work on major societal issues, you can:

- Join organizations already doing the work
- Build authority inside your company
- Move to mission-driven corporations like B Corps

Whatever path you choose:

- Think in systems
- Understand business realities
- Work with groups, not alone
- Focus on meaningful, long-term impact

Design is not just about products anymore. It’s about shaping systems that improve the world.
