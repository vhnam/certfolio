## Design Is No Longer Just About Products

Don Norman argues that the role of designers has expanded. We are no longer designing only objects, interfaces, or services — we are designing **complex socio-technical systems** that address issues like climate change, inequality, healthcare, and poverty.

This shift means traditional “design-as-craft” education is no longer enough.

Designers now need:

- Systems thinking
- Cross-disciplinary collaboration
- Business and economic literacy
- Technological fluency
- The ability to reframe problems

The skills required today are different — and so the way we teach design must change.

## New Technologies Require New Training

Modern tools such as AI, generative systems, AR/VR, and advanced engineering software are transforming design practice.

Designers are no longer just drawing or crafting forms. Instead, they are:

- Defining constraints
- Judging machine-generated outputs
- Reframing problems
- Deciding which direction is meaningful

This demands stronger **critical thinking and judgment skills**, not just technical execution.

## Design Schools Must Collaborate Across Disciplines

Don emphasizes that the best design education happens when schools partner with other disciplines:

- Engineering
- Business
- Economics
- Social sciences

He cites examples such as collaboration between the **Royal College of Art** and **Imperial College London**, where students learn both design and engineering.

He also highlights joint programs between:

- **MIT**
- **Rhode Island School of Design**

In these programs:

- Designers ensure human-centered thinking
- Engineers ensure technical feasibility
- Business students ensure economic viability

The result? Projects that could become real-world products immediately.

## Cross-Disciplinary Work Is Difficult — But Essential

Collaboration across disciplines is not easy.

- Engineers, designers, and business students speak different “languages.”
- They use the same words with different meanings.
- Leadership conflicts often arise.

However, when teams learn to work as equals, the outcome is far stronger than any discipline working alone.

Don argues that **real-world problems demand this kind of integration** — so education must reflect reality.

## Partner With Communities and Governments

Design education should not stay inside the classroom.

Don describes how his group at the University of California, San Diego works with:

- Local communities
- City governments
- Economic development organizations
- Cross-border collaborators in Tijuana, Mexico

He also mentions projects in London where design schools worked with city officials to redesign ambulance systems.

These real-world partnerships make education:

- More realistic
- More exciting
- More impactful

Students love it. Faculty often do too.

## There Is No Single Correct Curriculum

Don and Michael Meyer argue there is no universal model for design education. Each school must adapt based on:

- Its strengths
- Its faculty
- Its resources
- Its students’ interests

But one thing is clear:

> Design education must expand beyond traditional craft and embrace systems, collaboration, and real-world complexity.

## The Message for Designers

Even though the video addresses educators, the implication for designers is clear:

You must learn differently.

If your program or workplace does not expose you to:

- Business thinking
- Engineering
- Economics
- Psychology
- Public policy

Then seek those skills elsewhere.

The future designer:

- Thinks in systems
- Works across disciplines
- Partners with communities
- Focuses on real societal impact

That is how design evolves — and how it remains relevant in the 21st century.
