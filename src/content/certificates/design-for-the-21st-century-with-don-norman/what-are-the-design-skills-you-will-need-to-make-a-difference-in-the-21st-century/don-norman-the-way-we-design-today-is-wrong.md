## The Core Problem: Environmentally Destructive Design

### Designing “Crap”

- Environmental harm in production: Products require mining rare materials and large-scale manufacturing.
- Short lifespan: Many products (e.g., smartphones) are designed to last only 2–3 years.
- Disposal crisis: Products are not designed for disassembly or material recovery.
- Recycling failure: Composite materials (e.g., plastic-coated cartons) are difficult or impossible to separate.

### Example: Electronic Waste

- Resource extraction: Exotic materials mined globally.
- Manufacturing scale: Massive factories with large environmental footprints.
- End-of-life damage: E-waste dumped in large piles, sometimes burning and releasing toxic fumes.
- Design flaw: Products are not designed for repair, reuse, or material recovery.

## Historical Critique of Design

### Victor Papanek’s Argument

- Book: _Design for the Real World_ (1971).
- Provocative claim: Design can be socially harmful.
- Advertising critique: Advertisers persuade people to buy harmful products.
- Core issue: Designers create products that damage the environment in both production and disposal.

## Why Don’t Designers Change This?

### Designers Have No Power

- Limited authority: Designers rarely define what should be built.
- Late involvement: Called in after product decisions are already made.
- Narrow role: Asked to “make it pretty” rather than shape strategy.

### Where Designers Work

- Universities: Focused on teaching rather than shaping production decisions.
- Corporations: Must follow directives from management.
- Consultancies: Must satisfy client demands.

## The Real Problem: Flawed Design Education

### Craft Over Systems

- Craft training: Emphasis on making beautiful, desirable objects.
- Missing perspective:
  - Environmental impact
  - Business systems
  - Global economics
  - Trade and civilization
- Lack of systems thinking: Designers are not trained to understand the broader consequences of products.

### Lack of Authority

- Leadership gap: Designers are not trained to take strategic roles.
- Decision power: Authority is required to determine what should or should not be built.
- Moral responsibility: Some profitable products may be harmful to the planet.

## The Needed Shift: Humanity-Centered Design

### Broader Perspective

- Systems thinking: Understand economic, environmental, and societal systems.
- Strategic involvement: Participate in defining products, not just styling them.
- Ethical stance: Evaluate whether products should exist at all.

### Call to Action

1. Reform design education: Teach business, systems, and global impact.
2. Elevate designers: Prepare them for positions of authority.
3. Design responsibly: Refuse products that are profitable but harmful to the planet.
