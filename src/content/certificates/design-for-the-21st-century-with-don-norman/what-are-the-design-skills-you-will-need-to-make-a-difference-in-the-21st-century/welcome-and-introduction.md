## The Changing Nature of Design

### Increasing Scope and Complexity

- Expanding challenges: Design problems now operate at systemic and global levels.
- Outdated measurements: Traditional ways of designing and measuring success are insufficient.
- Organizational positioning: Designers must rethink their role and influence within companies.
- Interdisciplinary integration: Modern design requires combining multiple disciplines.

### From Products to Sociotechnical Systems

Quoted verbatim:

> “That's a new kind of design. It requires the skill of the designer. Not the drawing skill, but the skill to make judgments. A skill to reframe the problem. The skill to understand whether we're going in the right direction or not. A powerful skill that requires different training. And also, we're starting to look at different things. We’re no longer designing just devices or just products or just services. We are designing systems and we're starting to look at complex sociotechnical systems.”
> — Don Norman

#### Critical Designer Capabilities

- Judgment: Evaluating whether work is heading in the right direction.
- Problem reframing: Redefining challenges to uncover better solutions.
- Systems thinking: Designing interconnected sociotechnical systems.
- Strategic awareness: Understanding broader consequences of design decisions.

## Core Learning Areas

### Rethinking Design and Success

- Flawed methods: Today’s approaches to design and measurement need revision.
- Broader impact: Designers must address larger societal challenges.

### Increasing Influence and Career Growth

- Gaining power: Build influence to tackle complex problems.
- Moving up: Develop leadership capabilities within organizations.

### Failure and Learning

- Failure as learning: Treat setbacks as necessary steps toward improvement.

### Communication and Generalist Thinking

- Clear communication: Gain attention and alignment through strong messaging.
- Generalist strength: Leverage broad knowledge across disciplines.

### Skills for the 21st Century

- Critical competencies: Develop systemic, strategic, and interdisciplinary skills.
- Lone designer impact: Individuals can begin improving the world independently.
- Role of educators: Design education must evolve to support modern challenges.

## Learning Through Community

### Collaborative Learning

- Peer engagement: Use discussion forums for feedback and shared insights.
- Collective intelligence: Leverage the diverse knowledge of fellow learners.

## Strategic Path Forward

1. Understand new skills: Identify competencies required for modern design.
2. Chart a course: Plan how to address global and systemic problems.
3. Apply systems thinking: Move beyond isolated products toward meaningful impact.
