title: How You Can Get More Power and Influence as a Designer
---

## The Core Challenge: Why Designers Are Ignored

Many designers feel their ideas are neglected by management. Don Norman identifies that this is often due to a "language barrier" and a narrow focus:

- **The Wrong Approach:** Talking about design awards, past successes, or simply complaining about how hard a product is to use.
- **The Executive Perspective:** Leaders don't want to hear about problems; they want **solutions**. Pointing out a flaw without a business remedy is often seen as noise rather than a contribution.
- **The "Perfect Product" Fallacy:** Designers often create products that are technically or aesthetically perfect but fail to account for marketing, sales, or manufacturing realities.

## Human-Centered Design for Management

To influence a company, you must treat your leadership team as your primary "users."

- **Identify Your Real Users:** Your boss and senior executives are the people you are designing for when you pitch an idea.
- **Understand Their Motivations:** Management is driven by **viability**. A company cannot survive—no matter how good its mission—if it is losing money.
- **Speak the Language:** Use terms like **revenue, expenses, and margins**. Frame your design as a way to increase sales or improve efficiency.

## The Power of "Made-Up" Numbers and Spreadsheets

One of the most practical pieces of advice is to use spreadsheets to visualize success, even if the data isn't perfect.

- **Spreadsheets as Sketches:** Just as designers use sketches and personas to explore early ideas, executives use spreadsheets to think through business decisions.
- **Reasonable Assumptions:** Accuracy is less important than showing you understand the business logic. Propose a solution with realistic (even if estimated) projections of costs and profits.
- **Build Alliances:** Marketing teams often win arguments because they use spreadsheets. If you aren't comfortable with the numbers, partner with Marketing to build your case.

## Cross-Functional Collaboration: The Holistic Product

A product that fits user needs but doesn't sell is not a success. Designers must involve all parts of the company early in the process:

- **Engineering & Manufacturing:** To ensure the product is feasible to build.
- **Marketing & Sales:** To ensure people actually buy the product (e.g., the Sony "Better Sound" button example).
- **Service Teams:** To learn from recurring customer complaints.
- **Sustainability:** To plan for the full lifecycle, including recycling and environmental impact.

## Don Norman’s Vision: Think Big

Don Norman challenges designers to expand their perspective beyond the screen or the object to solve major societal issues.

> “Designers have to think about the company and about what is good for the company and then what is good for society and then what is good for humanity.” — Don Norman

### The Three Levels of Design Thinking:

1. **Company Level:** Ensure the business thrives and is profitable.
2. **Societal Level:** Address systemic issues and social impact.
3. **Humanity Level:** Protect the environment and ensure long-term global well-being.
