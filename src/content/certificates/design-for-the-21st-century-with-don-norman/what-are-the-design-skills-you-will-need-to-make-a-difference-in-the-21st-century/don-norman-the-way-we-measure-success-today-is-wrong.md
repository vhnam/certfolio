## The Crisis of Current Success Metrics

Our modern economic system is built on metrics that prioritize numerical growth over human well-being and environmental health.

### Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and Its Core Flaws

- **Definition:** GDP measures the market value of all final goods and services produced within a specific period.
- **The Ethical Gap:** GDP tracks economic activity regardless of whether that activity is beneficial or destructive.
- **Perverse Incentives:** Environmental destruction can actually _increase_ GDP if it generates spending (e.g., cleanup costs or healthcare). These "bads" are never subtracted from the total.

### Profit-Centered Measurement and Shareholder Primacy

- **Short-termism:** Companies driven by quarterly profits lose the incentive to create sustainable processes or reduce long-term waste.
- **Externalized Costs:** Businesses are often not held financialy accountable for the waste or "mess" they produce. As Don Norman notes, the burden of disposal and pollution is shifted from the corporation to society.

## The Root Cause: The Friedman Doctrine

The current global obsession with shareholder value can be traced back to the influential economist Milton Friedman.

- **The 1970 Mandate:** Friedman famously argued that "The Social Responsibility of Business is to Increase its Profits."
- **Allegiance to Capital:** This doctrine posits that a company owes responsibility only to its shareholders—not to its customers, employees, or the community.
- **The Moral Concern:** Critics argue this mindset is inherently immoral because it sacrifices long-term viability for immediate financial gain.

## The Necessity for New Measures

Recognizing the problem is common, but implementing global change remains the primary challenge.

- **Systemic Failure:** Economic structures currently reward harmful behavior because the "rules of the game" do not require companies to recycle or return what they build.
- **Global Synchronicity:** While alternative metrics exist in some regions, meaningful impact requires these changes to occur worldwide to prevent a race to the bottom.
- **Internalizing Costs:** Success must be redefined to include the mandatory internalizing of environmental and social impacts.

## The Role of Designers in Systemic Change

Design is no longer just about products; it is a way of thinking capable of addressing massive societal challenges.

### Design as Systems Thinking

Designers have the unique ability to reframe problems and question deep-seated assumptions about value. By applying design thinking to economic and social systems, they can help architect a more sustainable future.

### A Call to Action

1. **Question Metrics:** Critically evaluate how success is defined in every project.
2. **Advocate for Responsibility:** Support business models that take full accountability for their lifecycle impacts.
3. **Design for Policy:** Use design expertise to influence global systems and policy, moving beyond the "Friedman Doctrine."
