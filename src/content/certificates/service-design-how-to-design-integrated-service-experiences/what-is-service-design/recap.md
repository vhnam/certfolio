## Whole-System Perspective

Service design approaches experiences from a **whole system perspective**. Instead of focusing on a single interface or touchpoint, it considers how all elements of a service work together across channels to deliver a coherent experience.

## Frontstage and Backstage Actions

A service includes both **frontstage actions** (visible interactions with customers) and **backstage actions** (internal processes, systems, and operations). Effective service design aligns these elements so organizations can deliver smooth and reliable experiences.

## Relationship with UX, CX, and Customer Service

Service design overlaps with **customer experience (CX), customer service, and user experience (UX)**. It draws heavily from **Design Thinking and human-centered design**, using similar research and problem-solving methods to improve services.

## Visible and Invisible Services

Service design does not always involve designing a user interface or a physical product. Many service improvements occur **behind the scenes**, such as optimizing business processes or decision flows that indirectly improve the customer experience.

## Reducing Complexity and Addressing Pain Points

A major goal of service design is to **reduce complexity and eliminate customer pain points**. It also helps organizations develop strategies that enable services to be delivered smoothly without operational barriers.

## Key Differences from UX

Although service design shares many methods with UX research, it places stronger emphasis on:

- **Business objectives**
- **Stakeholder collaboration and co-creation**
- **Service staging and prototyping**
- **Omni-channel service blueprints**

## Business Value of Service Design

Service design helps organizations clarify **how services are delivered**, enabling better stakeholder collaboration, improved alignment with business goals, stronger user motivation, and reduced operational complexity.
