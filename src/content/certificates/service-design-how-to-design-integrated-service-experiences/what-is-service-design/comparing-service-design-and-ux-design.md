---
title: Comparing Service Design and UX Design
---

## Relationship Between UX and Service Design

### UX as the Foundation of Service Design

- UX Design: Focuses on the user's interaction with a product or interface.
- Service Design: Expands UX thinking to the **entire service ecosystem**, including processes, people, and systems.

Service design builds on **human-centered design and design thinking**, which are also core principles of UX.

Both disciplines share the same goal:

- Understanding user needs
- Solving real problems
- Creating meaningful experiences

## Shared Methods Between UX and Service Design

### Common Tools

Several tools used in UX are also used in service design:

- User research: Understanding user behaviors, needs, and pain points.
- Personas: Representing key user groups and their goals.
- Journey maps: Visualizing the steps users take when interacting with a service.
- Prototyping: Testing ideas quickly before building full solutions.

These methods help designers uncover **user expectations and service gaps**.

### Prototyping Approaches

Service design often expands prototyping beyond digital interfaces.

- Digital prototyping: Similar to UX UI prototypes.
- Service prototyping: Simulating real service interactions.
- Role-playing: Acting out service scenarios to understand customer experience.

Low-fidelity prototypes help teams **simulate and test service flows early**.

## Key Differences Between UX and Service Design

### Scope of Design

- UX Design: Focuses mainly on **digital interfaces and user interactions**.
- Service Design: Focuses on the **entire service journey**, including offline experiences.

Service design considers experiences such as:

- Transportation services
- Museums
- Healthcare systems
- Government services

### Service Blueprint

A core deliverable in service design.

- Service Blueprint: An expanded journey map that includes **frontstage and backstage processes**.

It shows:

- Customer actions
- Touchpoints
- Employee actions
- Internal systems
- Service artifacts

The blueprint ensures the service **operates smoothly across all layers**.

### Business and Operational Perspective

Service design looks deeper into the organizational system.

- Business model canvas: Defines the business logic behind the service.
- Value proposition: Explains the value delivered to customers.
- Back-end systems: Operations, support teams, and internal workflows.

Many UX designers focus on the **visible interface**, while service design examines **the entire system behind it**.

## Collaboration and Co-Creation

### Stakeholder Involvement

Service design heavily emphasizes collaboration.

- Cross-functional workshops
- Stakeholder participation
- Design thinking sessions

Workshops help align:

- Business goals
- User needs
- Operational constraints

### Co-Creation

- Co-creation: Designing services **together with stakeholders and sometimes customers**.

A service designer often acts as a **facilitator**, helping teams collaborate and develop solutions collectively.

## Core Tools Used in Service Design

Several UX techniques translate directly into service design:

- User research
- Design workshops
- Journey mapping
- Low-fidelity prototyping
- User testing

Service design simply adapts these tools to **simulate full service experiences instead of only interfaces**.

## Key Insight

Service design is **not a completely separate discipline from UX**.

Instead, it extends UX thinking by:

- Expanding the scope from interfaces to entire services
- Connecting customer experience with internal operations
- Integrating business strategy with design decisions

By adopting service design methods, designers gain a **system-level understanding of how experiences are delivered**.
