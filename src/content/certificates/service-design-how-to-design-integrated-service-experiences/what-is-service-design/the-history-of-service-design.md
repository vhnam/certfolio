title: "The History of Service Design"
---

## Transition from Products to Services

### Economic Shift

Over recent decades, industrialized economies shifted from **manufacturing products** to **delivering services**.

Key drivers:

- Growth of the **service economy**
- Rise of **digital technology**
- Expansion of **Software as a Service (SaaS)** and product-service systems

Examples:

- Smartphones acting as platforms for multiple services
- Software evolving from installed products to cloud-based services

### Impact on Innovation

This transition created a need for **new innovation processes focused on services**, leading to the development of **service design** as a distinct discipline.

## Origins of Service Design

### Academic Roots

Service design emerged from research and practice in:

- Operations management
- Marketing
- Design disciplines

Traditional perspectives:

- Operations: Focused on reducing manufacturing costs and improving efficiency.
- Marketing: Focused on understanding consumption behaviors to drive product sales.

These approaches did not translate well to services.

### Service-Dominant Logic

A major shift occurred when researchers proposed moving from **product-centered thinking** to **service provisioning**.

- Service provisioning: Delivering value through **performance and interactions**, not ownership of products.

## Development of Service Design Practice

### Interdisciplinary Collaboration

Service design emerged through collaboration between:

- Communication designers
- Product designers
- UX designers
- Service researchers
- Business organizations

These groups combined existing methods with **new systemic approaches**.

### Key Methods Developed

Two foundational service design tools include:

- Service blueprinting: Mapping frontstage interactions and backstage processes supporting a service.
- Customer journey mapping: Visualizing the sequence of user interactions with a service over time.

Both methods are now widely used in **UX design**.

## Expansion of Service Design Domains

Service design has been applied to many large-scale services, including:

- Banking and finance
- Healthcare
- Travel and hospitality
- Retail
- Government and public services

Research often focuses on:

- Frontline employees
- Customer interactions
- Backend operational processes

## Digital Transformation of Services

### Role of Information and Communication Technologies

Advances in **ICT (Information and Communication Technologies)** transformed service design.

Important technologies:

- Personal computers
- The Internet and World Wide Web
- Mobile phones and smartphones
- Cloud computing
- Social computing
- Internet of Things (IoT)

These technologies enabled the rise of **digital services**.

### Emergence of Self-Service Systems

Early digital services replaced frontline employees with **self-service interfaces**.

Examples:

- ATMs
- Ticket vending machines
- Airline check-in kiosks

These systems introduced new **usability challenges**, bringing service design closer to **Human-Computer Interaction (HCI)** and **UX design**.

### Online and Mobile Services

Later developments moved services to digital platforms:

- Websites
- Mobile apps
- Online support systems
- Chatbots and automated help systems

## Gig Economy and Service Innovation

Advances in mobile technology and data science enabled **gig-economy services**.

Examples:

- Food delivery platforms
- Ride-sharing services

These systems combine:

- Digital platforms
- Independent service providers
- Real-time data and logistics

Service designers must consider **technical infrastructure and economic models** when designing these services.

## Products vs Services

### Differences in Value Creation

- Product: Customers exchange money for **ownership of a physical object**.
- Service: Customers exchange money for **a performance or action**.

Example:

- Starbucks coffee: The **coffee is the product**.
- Starbucks store experience: The **service performed by employees**.

This relationship forms a **product-service system**, where products and services enhance each other.

## Growth of the Service Economy

Since the 1950s, services have increasingly dominated industrial economies.

Two important concepts explain this shift:

- Dematerialization: Reducing reliance on physical products.
- Liquification: Removing constraints of time and place through digital systems.

Example:

- Digital financial transactions replacing physical money exchanges.

These developments increase **speed, efficiency, and accessibility** of services.

## Service Innovation Through Unbundling and Rebundling

Service innovation often occurs by **reconfiguring existing services**.

Two key strategies:

- Unbundling: Separating services from traditional structures.
- Rebundling: Combining services into new integrated offerings.

Examples:

- Banks integrated inside grocery stores
- Smartphone-based payment systems replacing traditional cash registers

Digital platforms like **Square** transformed smartphones into **point-of-sale systems**.

## Product-Centric vs Service-Centric Models

### Traditional Product Model

Earlier software and hardware followed a **product ownership model**.

Example:

- Microsoft Office sold as boxed software in retail stores.

Customers:

- Purchased the product
- Owned physical materials
- Installed software locally

### Service-Based Model

Modern digital systems increasingly use **service-based models**.

Example:

- Google Docs delivered as a cloud service.

Key differences:

- Users access software online
- No ownership of the application
- Automatic updates
- Continuous access to the latest version

This model shifts value toward **ongoing service delivery** rather than product ownership.

## Value Co-Creation in Digital Services

Service platforms often create value through **co-creation between users and companies**.

Example:

- Users receive free software services.
- Companies collect usage data to improve services or generate revenue.

This relationship challenges traditional assumptions about the **user as a paying customer**.

## Disruptive Innovation in Services

Service innovations frequently follow the pattern of **disruptive innovation**.

Characteristics:

- Lower cost alternatives
- Fewer initial features
- Gradual improvement over time
- Attraction of new customer segments

Example:

- Google Docs disrupting traditional office software.

## Product-Service Systems

Modern systems combine **products and services into integrated ecosystems**.

Components of these systems include:

- Human actors
- Technology touchpoints
- Physical environments
- Digital infrastructure

### Innovation Approaches

Service innovation can occur through:

- Productization: Creating new products within a service ecosystem.
- Servitization: Adding services around existing products.

Examples:

- Amazon Alexa enabling access to Amazon services
- Apple iCloud enhancing Apple devices with cloud services
