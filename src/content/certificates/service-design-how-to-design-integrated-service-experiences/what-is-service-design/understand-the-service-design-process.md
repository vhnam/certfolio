## Service Design vs User-Centered Design

### Key Differences

- User-Centered Design (UCD): Focuses on designing **computational artifacts or products** based primarily on user needs.
- Service Design: Focuses on designing **entire service systems**, including customers, providers and organizational processes.

Service design outputs include:

- A service system
- A service concept
- A strategy for transitioning from the **current system to a preferred future system**

### Intersection of Needs

Service design solutions are developed at the intersection of:

- Customer needs
- Service provider goals
- Stakeholder initiatives

As a result, service design is often expressed through **models that explain how the service works and delivers value**.

## Overview of the Service Design Process

Service design typically follows a multi-stage process.

### Research and Discovery

1. User research: Conduct ethnographic field studies to understand users and their context.
2. Stakeholder interviews: Gather insights from stakeholders and subject-matter experts.
3. Pain point identification: Identify customer needs, frustrations and service gaps.

Common outputs:

- Personas
- Customer journey maps

### Strategy and Concept Development

After research, teams use strategic tools to define the service.

Tools commonly used:

- Business Model Canvas
- Value Proposition Canvas

These tools help teams:

- Align business goals with customer needs
- Define the service concept and value proposition

Stakeholder workshops are often used during this stage to ensure **cross-organizational alignment**.

### Service Blueprinting

After defining the service concept, teams map the full service system.

Key activities:

- Creating a service blueprint
- Mapping the service ecosystem
- Identifying interactions between customers and service providers

Service blueprinting helps teams understand:

- Customer touchpoints
- Internal processes
- Supporting systems

## Service Prototyping

Service designers create prototypes to test the proposed service before implementation.

### Types of Service Prototypes

Service prototypes may include:

- Digital prototypes (apps or websites)
- Physical environments
- Role-playing or acted-out service scenarios

These prototypes help simulate **real service interactions**.

### Goals of Prototyping

Prototyping allows teams to evaluate:

- How customers understand the service
- Whether the service fulfills user needs
- Whether the service creates value for both customers and providers

## Evaluation and Measurement

After prototyping and refinement, the service is launched and evaluated.

Common evaluation methods:

- Surveys
- Web analytics
- Exit interviews
- Diary studies
- Service safari (experiencing the service firsthand)

Continuous measurement ensures the service **improves over time**.

## Core Service Design Models

Three key models are commonly used to understand and design services.

### Stakeholder Model

- Stakeholder model: Visualizes the relationships among all stakeholders involved in the service system.

Purpose:

- Understand dependencies and interactions between stakeholders
- Identify influence and collaboration within the service ecosystem

### Service Blueprint

- Service blueprint: Maps the sequence of interactions customers experience while using a service.

The blueprint includes:

- Customer actions
- Frontstage interactions
- Backstage processes
- Supporting resources and infrastructure

Purpose:

- Reveal how different service components must coordinate to deliver the experience.

### Customer Journey Map

- Customer journey map: Visualizes the customer’s path through a service.

Focus areas include:

- Customer motivations
- Experiences during interactions
- Emotional responses across touchpoints

Purpose:

- Understand the user’s perspective and identify pain points.

## Role of Service Design Models

Service design models support team collaboration and decision-making.

They are used to:

- Align service design teams
- Explore scenarios and service concepts
- Evaluate the impact of changes to the system
- Understand relationships between products, services and stakeholders

These models help teams view the **service ecosystem holistically**.

## Prototyping and Iterative Improvement

To reduce risks, service design teams use **iterative prototyping**.

### Needs Validation

Initial concepts are tested against:

- Customer needs
- Service feasibility
- Value creation for both customers and providers

### Experience Prototypes

Experience prototypes simulate real service interactions.

Examples include:

- Role-playing service interactions
- Simulating environments where the service occurs
- Testing touchpoints and workflows

This method helps teams observe **social interactions and behaviors around the service**.

## Key Takeaways

- Service design expands traditional design methods to include **entire service systems**.
- The process includes **research, strategy development, blueprinting, prototyping and evaluation**.
- Key tools include **stakeholder models, service blueprints and customer journey maps**.
- Service prototypes test how services function in **real physical and social contexts**.
- Iterative refinement ensures services deliver value to **both customers and service providers**.
