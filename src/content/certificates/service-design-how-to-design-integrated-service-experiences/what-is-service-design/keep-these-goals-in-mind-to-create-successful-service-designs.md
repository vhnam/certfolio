## What Service Design Is

Service design focuses on **understanding all the components of a service and organizing them around user needs**. Instead of designing only a single interface or product, service designers examine the **entire system of interactions** that deliver value to users.

A service may include:

- Digital interfaces (apps, websites)
- Physical environments (stores, offices)
- Human interactions (customer service staff)
- Supporting materials (forms, brochures, programs)

The goal is to **design these elements together so they form a coherent experience**.

## Origins of Service Design

Service design was first discussed in the **early 1980s**, particularly through the work of **Lynn Shostack**. Her key contribution was highlighting the importance of looking beyond visible customer interactions and examining the **organizational systems behind service delivery**.

This perspective introduced the idea that services consist of both:

- Frontstage elements (what customers see)
- Backstage systems (internal processes and employees)

Understanding how these internal systems work together is essential for designing effective services.

## Service Design as an Interdisciplinary Practice

Service design developed from the combination of several disciplines:

- UX design
- Human-centered design
- Design thinking
- Business operations
- Organizational workflows

It also emerged alongside the growth of **omnichannel services**, where users interact with organizations across multiple channels.

Examples of service channels include:

- Websites and mobile apps
- Chatbots and online forms
- Phone support
- Physical service locations

These interactions must work together as **one unified service experience**.

## Digital, Physical, and Social Dimensions of Services

Modern services operate across three interconnected environments:

- Digital: Interfaces such as apps, websites, or chat systems.
- Physical: Real-world spaces where services occur.
- Social: Human interactions between customers and staff.

Service design integrates these dimensions into a **single, consistent experience**.

For example, a customer might:

1. Fill out a form online.
2. Contact support through chat.
3. Visit a physical location.
4. Receive help from a staff member.

Service designers ensure these interactions **work seamlessly together**.

## Goals of Service Design

The primary goal of service design is to **improve and transform service delivery experiences**.

This includes aligning three key elements:

- User needs and expectations
- Organizational resources and capabilities
- Desired business outcomes

Examples of business outcomes may include:

- Increasing conversions
- Improving task completion rates
- Encouraging service adoption
- Reducing operational friction

Service design helps organizations achieve these goals while maintaining **high-quality user experiences**.

## Systems Thinking in Service Design

Service design requires **systems thinking**, meaning designers must understand the broader network of interactions involved in delivering a service.

This includes:

- Business processes
- Organizational workflows
- Technology infrastructure
- Operational logistics
- Customer journeys over time

By examining the entire system, designers can **reduce complexity and identify opportunities for improvement**.

## Frontstage and Backstage Alignment

Effective service design requires balancing two perspectives:

- Frontstage: The visible experience customers interact with.
- Backstage: Internal systems and processes that enable the experience.

Designers must ensure both areas work together seamlessly.

Poorly designed backstage systems often result in:

- Broken service journeys
- Slow processes
- Inconsistent experiences

## Stakeholder Collaboration

Service design cannot be done by a single department. Instead, it requires collaboration across the organization.

Important participants include:

- Designers
- Product teams
- Operations staff
- Customer service teams
- Business stakeholders

Engaging stakeholders ensures that services are **practical, scalable, and aligned with organizational goals**.

## Total Experience Delivery

A key idea in service design is **Total Experience Delivery (TED)**.

This means organizations must:

- Understand all components of the service
- Coordinate them effectively
- Deliver the experience consistently

Designing a service is not only about **planning or mapping the experience**, but also about ensuring it is **actually implemented and delivered**.

## Framing the Total Problem

Another important concept is **Framing the Total Problem (FTP)**.

This involves:

- Understanding the full scope of a service challenge
- Considering both user experience and organizational systems
- Identifying how different parts of the service interact

By framing the entire problem, service designers can create **more holistic and sustainable solutions**.

## Key Takeaways

- Service design focuses on **designing entire service systems**, not just interfaces.
- It emerged in the **1980s** through research on how services are delivered.
- The discipline integrates **UX design, human-centered design, and business operations**.
- Services operate across **digital, physical, and social environments**.
- Effective service design aligns **user needs, business goals, and organizational resources**.
- Concepts such as **systems thinking, stakeholder collaboration, Total Experience Delivery, and Framing the Total Problem** guide successful service design practices.
