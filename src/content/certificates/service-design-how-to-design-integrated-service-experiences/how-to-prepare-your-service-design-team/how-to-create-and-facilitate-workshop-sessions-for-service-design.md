---

## Workshops Are Central to Service Design

- Workshops enable **co-creation and collaboration**
- Used across stages:
  - Research setup
  - Ideation and prototyping
  - Validation and co-design
- Purpose:
  - Align stakeholders
  - Define problems and solutions
  - Combine business, tech, and user perspectives

## Plan Workshops with Clear Structure

- Start with:
  - Clear **goal and overview**
  - Defined **expected outcomes**
- Key planning steps:
  - Invite the **right participants** (critical for success)
  - Decide **time and location**
  - Work **backwards from outcomes → activities**
- Avoid:
  - Poor participant fit (wrong mindset for the stage)
  - Over-inviting → slows progress

## Facilitation Best Practices

### Provide Clear Instructions

- Give instructions **directly on materials**, not just slides
- Repeat and clarify tasks when needed
- Keep sessions tightly structured

### Manage Time Carefully

- Track time for each activity
- Maintain momentum throughout the workshop

### Use Warm-ups and Energy Management

- Use **icebreakers or improv games**
- Especially useful:
  - After lunch
  - In long sessions
- Keep participants:
  - Active
  - Focused
  - Energized

## Use Better Collaboration Techniques

### Prefer Brainwriting over Brainstorming

- Brainwriting:
  - Reduces bias
  - Produces more concrete ideas
- Traditional brainstorming:
  - Can lead to noise without substance

### Encourage Visual Thinking

- Use:
  - Sketching
  - Diagrams
  - Journey maps
- Benefits:
  - Makes progress visible
  - Improves shared understanding
- Important:
  - Reassure participants → no need to be “good at drawing”

### Use Tangible Artifacts

- Examples:
  - Worksheets
  - Empathy maps
  - Printed templates
- Helps:
  - Guide activities
  - Increase engagement

## Make Workshops Interactive

- Avoid passive formats (e.g., long presentations)
- Instead:
  - Use sticky notes
  - Group activities
  - Movement and hands-on tasks
- Goal:
  - Keep participants **actively contributing**

## Workshops as Learning and Alignment Tools

- Workshops are not just for execution
- Also used for:
  - **Clarity-making** (understanding service design vs UX, Agile, etc.)
  - **Action learning** (real problems, safe experimentation)
- Help teams:
  - Understand roles and overlaps
  - Build shared language

## Co-Design with Stakeholders

- Include:
  - Business stakeholders
  - Technical teams
  - Domain experts
  - Users
- Use techniques:
  - Card sorting
  - Dot voting
  - Collaborative exercises
- Key idea:
  - Designers are **facilitators**, not sole creators

## Balance Expertise

- Effective service design combines:
  - **Horizontal skills** → design process & facilitation
  - **Vertical knowledge** → domain expertise
- Collaboration across both is essential

## Drive Engagement and Ownership

- Make workshops:
  - Interactive
  - Hands-on
  - Outcome-driven
- When participants:
  - Contribute ideas
  - Create outputs
    → They feel **ownership**, leading to better adoption

## Key Takeaways

- Workshops are a **core tool** in service design
- Success depends on:
  - Clear planning
  - Right participants
  - Strong facilitation
- Focus on:
  - Interaction over presentation
  - Co-creation over instruction
- The ultimate goal:
  - Align people
  - Generate actionable outputs
  - Build shared ownership of solutions
