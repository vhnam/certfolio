## Transcript Core Concepts

### AI as an Amplifier, Not a Thinker

- AI: Amplifies the quality of your input (clarity or confusion).
- Output quality: Directly depends on how well the problem is framed.
- Limitation: Does not understand real-world context or user reality.

### Risks of Using AI

- Hallucination: Generates plausible but incorrect information.
- Bias: Reinforces patterns from training data.
- Overconfidence: Sounds convincing even when wrong.

### Understanding Bias in AI Systems

Bias in AI operates across three levels:

- **Pre-existing bias**:  
  Originates from society and historical data. AI inherits existing inequalities and cultural assumptions.

- **Technical bias**:  
  Introduced during system design (data selection, labeling, model decisions).

- **Emergent bias**:  
  Appears after deployment as users interact with the system in real-world contexts.

→ Designers must continuously evaluate bias, not just at build time but throughout the product lifecycle.

### Core Principle

- AI: Generates options.
- Designer: Makes decisions.
- Role: Maintain control over judgment and direction.

## The Designer's Role Has Shifted: From Creator to Director

### Role Transformation

- Creator (before): Produces artifacts (UI, flows, ideas).
- Director (now): Orchestrates AI to produce meaningful outputs.

### Key Responsibilities

- Frame the situation: Define what matters.
- Provide evidence: Supply research, constraints, data.
- Specify deliverables: Define usable outputs.
- Refine and judge: Evaluate against user and business needs.

### Mental Model

- AI: Junior designer (fast but lacks context).
- Designer: Strategic decision-maker.

## The AI Workflow Framework

### Purpose

- Apply AI consistently across all design stages.
- Maintain flexibility while ensuring useful outputs.

### Design Thinking Stages

- Empathize
- Define
- Ideate
- Prototype
- Test
- Deliver

### Framework Components

1. Goal: What needs to be solved or created.
2. Design Context: Constraints and situational truths.
3. Inputs: Research, data, assumptions.
4. Desired Output: Decision-ready deliverable.
5. Quality Checks: Validation criteria.
6. Refinement: Iteration and judgment process.

### Key Insight

- If these elements are unclear → AI will amplify confusion.

## How to Write Prompts That Actually Work

### Prompt = Structured Thinking

- Not about wording.
- About translating the framework into instructions.

### Core Elements of a Strong Prompt

- Context: Situation and constraints.
- Inputs: Evidence and data.
- Output: Clear deliverable definition.
- Refinement: Criteria for iteration.

### Prompt as a Design Brief

- Functions like a mini brief for AI.
- Enables decision-ready outputs instead of vague responses.

### Common Mistake

- Asking generic questions instead of defining problem space.

## Stay in Control with These Four Quality Checks

### Purpose

- Prevent “plausible but wrong” outputs from influencing decisions.

### 1. Traceability

- Ask: Where did this come from?
- Require: Sources, assumptions, links to inputs.
- Rule: Unverified = hypothesis, not fact.

### 2. Context Completeness

- Ask: What is missing?
- Add: Constraints, user reality, exclusions.
- Rule: AI only knows what you include.

### 3. Data vs Interpretation

- Ask: What is fact vs opinion?
- Ensure: Clear labeling of observations vs assumptions.
- Rule: Avoid decisions based on speculation.

### 4. Bias and Blind Spots

- Ask: Who is excluded?
- Check: Edge cases, alternative perspectives.
- Rule: Actively counter bias from training data.

## Six Ways to Use AI Across the Design Process

![Five stages in the design thinking process: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test.](../../../../assets/images/ai-for-designers/become-an-ai-powered-designer-research-and-ideation/td-design-thinking-non-linear-process.avif)

### 1. Empathize (Research)

- Use: Summarization, clustering, organizing notes.
- Ask: Themes with supporting evidence and gaps.
- Avoid: Treating summaries as truth.

### 2. Define

- Use: Problem statements, hypotheses, success metrics.
- Ask: Multiple framings with validation criteria.
- Avoid: Locking onto the first framing.

### 3. Ideate

- Use: Generate multiple ideas and variations.
- Ask: Options with pros, cons, and risks.
- Avoid: Choosing based on polish instead of relevance.

### 4. Prototype

- Use: UI copy, states, microcopy, variations.
- Ask: Outputs optimized for clarity, trust, accessibility.
- Avoid: Ignoring tone, brand, accessibility.

### 5. Test

- Use: Heuristic evaluation, risk analysis.
- Ask: Usability risks and quick validation methods.
- Avoid: Using AI as a substitute for real users.

### 6. Deliver

- Use: Documentation, release notes, decision logs.
- Ask: What changed, why, risks, and metrics.
- Avoid: Sharing sensitive data with unapproved tools.

## The Take Away

### Universal Workflow

- Always define:
  - Goal
  - Context
  - Inputs
  - Output
  - Quality checks
  - Refinement

### Prompting Principle

- Prompt = structured decision-making, not clever phrasing.

### Validation is Mandatory

- Check:
  - Source traceability
  - Missing context
  - Bias (pre-existing, technical, emergent)
  - Data vs interpretation

### Role Shift

- From: Producing outputs.
- To: Directing systems and making decisions.

### Final Insight

- AI: Accelerates execution and exploration.
- Designer: Owns judgment, context, and outcomes.
