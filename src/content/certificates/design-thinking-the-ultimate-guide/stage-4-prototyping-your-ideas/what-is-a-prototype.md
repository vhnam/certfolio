---
title: What is a Prototype?
---

## Definition and Purpose

### What is a Prototype

- Prototype: A simple model of a proposed solution used to test and explore ideas quickly and cheaply.
- Forms:
  - Sketches (paper wireframes)
  - Interactive digital mockups
  - Physical models

### Core Role

- Tangibility: Turns abstract ideas into concrete artifacts.
- Execution: Moves from thinking → doing.
- Communication: Enables discussion, testing, and refinement.

Quoted verbatim:

> “Ideas are easy. It's the execution of ideas that really separates the sheep from the goats.”

## Role of Prototypes in the Design Process

### Key Benefits

- Explore ideas:
  - Compare alternatives early in the process.
- Test and improve:
  - Identify usability issues before full development.
- Flesh out ideas:
  - Reveal gaps, missing features, and dependencies.
- Communicate with stakeholders:
  - Make ideas understandable and gain buy-in.

### Efficiency Advantage

- Fail fast:
  - Reduce time and cost by testing early.
- Avoid over-investment:
  - Prevent committing to weak ideas too soon.

Quoted verbatim:

> “They slow us down to speed us up... we avoid costly mistakes... and sticking with a weak idea for too long.”

## Fidelity in Prototyping

### Definition

- Fidelity: The level of detail and realism in a prototype.

## Low-Fidelity Prototypes

### Characteristics

- Simple, quick, and inexpensive
- Focus on structure and functionality
- Not visually detailed

### Common Types

- Sketches
- Paper prototypes
- Wireframes
- Lego models
- Wizard of Oz prototypes

### When to Use

- Early-stage exploration (divergent phase)
- Rapid idea generation and comparison
- Tight timelines and low cost
- Co-creation with non-designers
- Testing costly product ideas early

### Pros

- Fast and cheap to create
- Easy to modify or discard
- Encourages openness to feedback
- Focus on core functionality
- Inclusive for stakeholders

### Cons

- Low realism → limited feedback quality
- Cannot test visual details
- Harder for users to understand/interact

## High-Fidelity Prototypes

### Characteristics

- Detailed and realistic
- Close to final product in look and behavior

### Common Types

- Interactive digital mockups
- 3D-printed models
- Coded prototypes

### When to Use

- Later-stage refinement (convergent phase)
- Usability and visual testing
- Stakeholder presentations
- Handoff to development teams

### Pros

- Realistic user feedback
- Test fine details (UI, animation, microcopy)
- Strong stakeholder engagement

### Cons

- Time and resource intensive
- Risk of attachment to design
- Feedback may focus on superficial aspects
- Users may hesitate to criticize

## Choosing the Right Prototype

### Decision Process

1. Define idea: Identify key components.
2. Define questions: What do you want to learn?
3. Match fidelity:
   - Low-fidelity → broad questions
   - High-fidelity → detailed questions
4. Act: Build and test quickly.

## Prototyping Guidelines

### Best Practices

1. Start building: Learn through making.
2. Limit time: Prioritize testing over polishing.
3. Focus questions: Each prototype answers a core question.
4. Keep user-centered:
   - Test against real user needs and behaviors.

## Summary

### Key Takeaways

- Prototypes:
  - Explore, test, refine, and communicate ideas.
- Low-fidelity:
  - Best for early exploration.
- High-fidelity:
  - Best for detailed validation.
- Iteration:
  - Move from rough → refined through continuous testing.
