## Overview of Low-Fidelity Prototypes

### Purpose and Usage

- Low-fidelity prototypes:
  - Quick, inexpensive tools to test ideas
- Best used:
  - Early (divergent) stages of design
- Focus:
  - Broad concepts, not fine details
- Key advantage:
  - Easy to discard → reduces attachment to weak ideas

## Sketches

### Definition

- Sketches: Quick, rough drawings to visualize ideas.

### Pros and Cons

- Pros:
  - Fast and cheap
  - Easy to create anywhere
  - Disposable → encourages experimentation
- Cons:
  - Lack detail and clarity
  - Hard for outsiders to understand
  - Not ideal for narrowing down final ideas

### When to Use

- Early ideation and exploration
- Communicating rough ideas with team
- Mapping systems, journeys, and relationships

### Best Practices

- Sketch ideas immediately (bias toward action)
- Keep details minimal
- Use diagrams for complex systems
- Involve team members in sketching sessions

## Paper Prototypes

### Definition

- Paper prototypes:
  - Physical representations of interfaces using paper elements

### Pros and Cons

- Pros:
  - Cheap and easy to modify
  - Can simulate interactions manually
  - Focus on concept, not visuals
  - Encourages honest user feedback
- Cons:
  - Structural changes can be time-consuming
  - Limited for familiar UI patterns
  - Requires in-person testing
  - Still requires user imagination

### When to Use

- Exploring novel solutions
- Comparing different interface approaches
- Early-stage usability testing

### Best Practices

- Keep prototypes neat and legible
- Simulate interactions during testing
- Run dry tests before real sessions
- Align team on testing flow beforehand

## Lego Prototypes

### Definition

- Lego prototypes:
  - Physical models built using Lego bricks

### Pros and Cons

- Pros:
  - Quick to build and modify
  - Encourages experimentation
  - Useful for physical products
- Cons:
  - Not suitable for digital interfaces
  - Higher cost than paper methods

### When to Use

- Simulating user journeys
- Modeling complex systems with multiple actors
- Testing physical product size and weight

### Best Practices

- Use for storytelling and empathy
- Involve non-designers
- Simulate real-world scale and weight

## Digital Wireframes

### Definition

- Wireframes:
  - Simple digital layouts focusing on structure and functionality

### Pros and Cons

- Pros:
  - Easy to update
  - Focus on layout and functionality
  - Show relationships between screens
- Cons:
  - Hard for users to interpret without guidance
  - Placeholder content may distort feedback

### When to Use

- Later than sketches (mid-stage)
- Designing layout, flows, and information architecture
- Refining selected ideas

### Best Practices

- Focus on structure, not visuals
- Use grayscale only
- Minimize placeholder content
- Limit layout variations for consistency

## Wizard of Oz Prototypes

### Definition

- Wizard of Oz:
  - Fake functionality simulated by a human (“wizard”)

### Pros and Cons

- Pros:
  - Test complex features without building them
  - Explore future technologies
  - Gather realistic user feedback
- Cons:
  - Requires preparation and coordination
  - Needs trained “wizard”
  - Risk of inconsistent behavior

### When to Use

- Later stages of design
- Complex systems or interactions
- Voice interfaces, AI, or hard-to-build features

### Best Practices

- Define clear testing questions
- Use simple tools to simulate interactions
- Train the “wizard” with clear rules
- Note limitations (does not test system reliability)

## Summary

### Choosing the Right Method

- Sketches:
  - Early exploration and idea generation
- Paper prototypes:
  - Testing novel interaction concepts
- Lego prototypes:
  - Physical systems and empathy building
- Wireframes:
  - Structure, layout, and flows
- Wizard of Oz:
  - Complex or future functionality testing

### Key Principle

- Low-fidelity prototyping:
  - Enables fast learning, rapid iteration, and low-cost experimentation
