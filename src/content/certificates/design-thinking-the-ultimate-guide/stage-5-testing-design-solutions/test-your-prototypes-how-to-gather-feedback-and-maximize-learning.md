## Why Gathering Feedback Matters

Gathering feedback is a crucial step in design thinking and human-centered design. Once you have built prototypes, testing them with users helps you validate ideas, uncover problems, and improve your designs. Being purposeful about how you collect feedback saves time and resources while maximizing what you learn from each test session.

## Six Best Practice Tips for Gathering Feedback

### 1. **Think of How to Solicit Feedback**  
   Test several versions of your prototype to encourage honest comparisons. Use the “I Like, I Wish, What If” method to help participants give constructive criticism in a positive way.

### 2. **Test Your Prototypes on the Right People**  
   In early stages, testing with teammates is fine for quick feedback. Later, test with a representative sample of real users. Also consider extreme users and stakeholders (manufacturers, retailers, etc.) to gain broader insights. For international products, test across different regions and cultures.

### 3. **Ask the Right Questions**  
   Define clear questions you want to answer with each prototype (e.g., usability, desirability). Stay open-minded during sessions, as unexpected insights often emerge. Evaluate feedback after each round and adjust future questions accordingly.

### 4. **Be Neutral When Presenting Ideas**  
   Avoid selling or defending your prototype. Present it objectively so participants feel comfortable giving honest (including negative) feedback. Remember, this is a rehearsal — use criticism to improve the design.

### 5. **Adapt While You Test**  
   Stay flexible. If something distracts participants, remove it. You can adjust the script if needed, but keep changes reasonable so you can still compare results across sessions.

### 6. **Let Participants Contribute Ideas**  
   Encourage users to suggest improvements. Turn their questions back to them (e.g., “What do you think would be the best way?”). Even if you don’t use every idea, it reveals important user concerns.

## Three Methods to Organize and Maximize Feedback

### 1. Feedback Capture Grid

![Illustration of a feedback capture grid. It's a 2 by 2 grid with Likes, Criticisms, Questions, and Ideas.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-5-testing-design-solutions/feedback-capture-grid.avif)

Divide a page into four quadrants:

- **Likes** — Positive feedback
- **Criticisms** — Negative feedback
- **Questions** — Doubts or new questions raised
- **Ideas** — Suggestions for improvement

Use it during or after sessions to structure notes systematically.

### 2. I Like, I Wish, What If

![Illustration of three sections: I like, I wish, and What if.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-5-testing-design-solutions/i-like-i-wish-what-if.avif)

A simple, positive framework for feedback:

- **I Like…** — What works well
- **I Wish…** — What could be improved
- **What If…** — New ideas or possibilities

This method helps participants give honest criticism constructively.

### 3. Sharing Inspiring Stories

After testing, have the team share memorable or inspiring stories from the sessions. Capture them on Post-its, look for patterns, and turn insights into actionable next steps.

## The Takeaway

Effective feedback gathering requires planning, neutrality, and flexibility. Use the six tips to solicit better input and the three methods (Feedback Capture Grid, I Like/I Wish/What If, and Sharing Inspiring Stories) to organize insights. Always turn feedback into action by iterating on your prototypes.

Build → Gather Feedback → Iterate. Repeat this cycle to move toward a solution that truly meets user needs.
