## Overview of Usability Testing

Usability testing is a core activity in design thinking and human-centered design. It helps identify major usability problems early so you can refine prototypes and launch products that are easy and intuitive to use. Testing should happen frequently throughout the project. The lesson covers six common types of usability tests, their pros and cons, and when to use each one. Remember: you are testing the design, not the user.

## Six Main Types of Usability Tests

### 1. Heuristic Evaluations

Experts (usually 3–5) review the design against established usability principles (heuristics). This is often done early, before testing with real users.

**Pros**:

- Can be done at any stage, even on low-fidelity prototypes
- Relatively quick and catches obvious issues

**Cons**:

- Less effective than testing with real users
- May identify problems that don’t actually affect users
- Not suitable for understanding whether the solution addresses real user problems

**Best used**:

- Early in the process
- For novel interfaces
- As a quick check before user testing

### 2. Guerrilla (Quick & Dirty / Hallway) Usability Tests

Informal tests conducted with anyone available — colleagues, friends, or strangers in a café.

**Pros**:

- Very fast and cheap
- Better than no testing at all

**Cons**:

- Results can be unreliable because participants may not match your target audience
- Feedback may be overly positive or shallow
- Not suitable for products requiring specialized knowledge

**Best used**:

- For quick checks on a tight budget or timeline
- Always combine with more formal testing

### 3. Lab Usability Tests

Structured sessions in a controlled environment where participants perform tasks while being observed and recorded.

**Pros**:

- Provides rich, high-quality qualitative insights
- Allows probing questions to understand user thinking

**Cons**:

- Expensive and time-consuming (recruitment, moderation, facilities)
- Participants may behave differently due to the lab setting (Hawthorne effect)
- Setup may not reflect real-world usage

**Best used**:

- When you need detailed insights on more advanced prototypes

### 4. Contextual Usability Tests

Similar to lab tests but conducted in the participant’s natural environment (home, office, or wherever they would normally use the product).

**Pros**:

- More realistic behavior since users are in their familiar setting and using their own devices

**Cons**:

- More expensive and logistically challenging (travel required)
- Prototype must work across different devices and setups

**Best used**:

- When context of use significantly affects how people interact with the product
- For mobile or location-specific tools

### 5. Unmoderated Remote Usability Tests

Participants complete tasks remotely on their own, usually through online platforms that record sessions.

**Pros**:

- Cheaper and faster than moderated tests
- Can reach a larger and more geographically diverse sample
- Users test in their natural environment

**Cons**:

- Less detailed insights (limited ability to ask follow-up questions)
- Not suitable for complex tasks or deep exploration of user thinking

**Best used**:

- For validating findings with a larger audience
- When reaching users in different locations or time zones

### 6. Moderated Remote Usability Tests

Conducted remotely with a moderator guiding the session via screen-sharing or specialized tools.

**Pros**:

- Combines rich insights of moderated testing with the convenience of remote sessions
- Cheaper and more flexible than in-person lab tests

**Cons**:

- Technical issues can disrupt sessions
- Moderator cannot see the participant’s physical environment

**Best used**:

- When you need moderated depth but have budget or scheduling constraints

## Key Takeaways

Different usability tests serve different purposes and project stages. A good approach is to combine methods:

- Use heuristic evaluations and guerrilla tests early for quick wins.
- Move to lab, contextual, or moderated remote tests for deeper insights on more mature prototypes.
- Use unmoderated remote tests to validate with larger samples.

The goal is to test early and often, learn from real user behavior, and iteratively improve your design until it is easy, effective, and delightful to use.
