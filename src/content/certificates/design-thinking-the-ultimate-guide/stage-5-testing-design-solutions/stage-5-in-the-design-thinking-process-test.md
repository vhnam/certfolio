## The Role of Testing in Design Thinking

Testing is the fifth stage of the design thinking process. It is essential for validating ideas, uncovering usability issues, and improving prototypes based on real user feedback. Without testing, the iterative nature of design thinking cannot succeed. User feedback is invaluable — it reveals what works, what doesn’t, and how to create solutions that are truly desirable, feasible, and viable.

Testing should happen **throughout the entire design thinking process**, not only at the end. It works hand-in-hand with prototyping: you create prototypes (starting low-fidelity and progressing to higher-fidelity) and test them repeatedly with real users. Insights from testing often feed back into earlier stages — helping you better empathize with users, refine the problem statement, generate new ideas, or improve the prototype.

![Illustration of the 5-Stage design process showing how it is not linear.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-5-testing-design-solutions/5-stages-in-design.avif)

The five stages of design thinking (Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test) are not strictly linear. They are flexible “modes” that can overlap or loop as needed to maximize learning.

## 5 Guidelines for Conducting Effective Tests

### 1. **Show, don’t tell**

Let users experience the prototype naturally. Avoid over-explaining how it works. Introduce yourself neutrally (never say you are the designer), explain the session length and expectations, and answer any questions before starting. Observe their genuine reactions.

### 2. **Ask participants to think aloud**

Encourage users to verbalize their thoughts, expectations, and feelings as they interact with the prototype. Remind them to speak out loud if they go quiet. This “think-aloud” approach reveals their mental model and uncovers confusion or delight.

### 3. **Observe neutrally**

Watch how participants use the prototype without interrupting or correcting them. Mistakes and struggles provide valuable learning opportunities. Remember: you are testing the prototype, not the user.

### 4. **Ask follow-up questions**

Probe deeper with questions such as “What do you mean by…?”, “How did that make you feel?”, “What did you expect would happen?”, and especially “Why?”. Clarify assumptions to gain richer insights.

### 5. **Embrace negative feedback**

Negative comments are opportunities to improve. They help uncover problems you and your team may have missed. Accept criticism constructively — it leads to better, more user-centered solutions.

## The End Goal: Desirable, Feasible, and Viable Solutions

![Venn diagram of Feasibility, Viability, and Desirability with the goal at the intersection of the three circles.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-5-testing-design-solutions/venn-diagram-fearsibility-desirability-viability.avif)

The ultimate aim of design thinking is to create solutions that succeed at the intersection of three criteria:

- **Desirability** — Appeals to people’s needs, emotions, and behaviors (human-centered).
- **Feasibility** — Technically possible with available or realistic technology.
- **Viability** — Sustainable as a business or long-term initiative (can continue without constant external funding).

When a solution meets all three, it has the potential to create lasting positive impact.

## The Takeaway

Testing is a continuous, iterative activity that drives learning and improvement across the entire design thinking process. Follow the five guidelines — show don’t tell, encourage thinking aloud, observe carefully, ask thoughtful follow-ups, and welcome negative feedback — to gather honest and actionable insights.

The design thinking process is fluid. Insights from testing often loop back to earlier stages. The strongest outcomes occur when your final solution is desirable to users, feasible to build, and viable for the long term.
