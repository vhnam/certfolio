## Purpose of User Interviews

### Why User Interviews Matter

- User Interviews: A qualitative research method used to understand users’ experiences, behaviors, motivations, and needs.
- Baseline Understanding: Interviews help designers ground their assumptions in real user perspectives.
- Human Connection: They allow designers to directly connect with users and understand their context.
- Efficient Research Tool: They are relatively cheap, quick, and produce a large amount of insight.

![Three types of note taking - descriptive, inferential, and evaluative.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-1-how-to-build-empathy-with-the-people-you-design-for/notetaking-types.avif)

User interviews can be used throughout the product lifecycle:

- Early discovery: When you know little about the problem space.
- Idea generation: To explore potential solutions.
- Concept testing: To validate design ideas.
- Pre-launch: To refine product decisions.
- Post-launch: To understand real usage and improve the product.

### Diversity in Participants

- Diverse Participants: Include different user types and profiles.
- Broader Perspectives: Diversity helps uncover needs designers might not initially consider.

## Planning User Interviews

### Identify Research Goals

Before conducting interviews, define what you want to learn.

1. Define the research question: Clarify the problem the team wants to answer.
2. Align the team: Discuss goals with stakeholders, product managers, developers, and clients.
3. Identify learning objectives: Determine the key topics the interviews should explore.

- Research Question: The main problem the research aims to answer.
- Learning Objectives: Specific insights the team wants to gain from interviews.

## Creating a Discussion Guide

### What is a Discussion Guide

- Discussion Guide: A structured list of prompts used to guide an interview.
- Purpose: Ensures the interviewer covers all important topics.

A good discussion guide is short and simple.

- Can fit on an index card.
- Serves as a checklist during interviews.

### The Funnel Structure

A discussion guide follows a **funnel approach**:

1. Context Setting
2. Focus Questions
3. Probe Questions
4. Closing

#### Context Setting

Purpose: Establish the interview context and build rapport.

Typical elements include:

- Introduce yourself
- Explain the purpose of the research
- Set expectations for the conversation
- Ask about the participant’s background

Example opening question:

- “Can you tell me a little about who you are and your experience in this area?”

#### Focus Questions

Focus questions cover the **main topics** of the interview.

- Focus Questions: High-level questions addressing the key research topics.

Examples:

- How users perform a task
- What tools they use
- Their current challenges

#### Probe Questions

Probe questions dig deeper when answers are unclear or incomplete.

- Probe Questions: Follow-up prompts used to gather deeper insights.

Examples:

- “Why did you choose that option?”
- “What happened next?”
- “How did that make you feel?”

#### Closing the Interview

The interview ends with a short wrap-up.

Typical closing actions:

- Thank the participant
- Ask for referrals to other potential participants
- Ask permission for future follow-ups

## Getting Rich Insights

### Encourage Storytelling

The most valuable interview responses come from **stories**.

- Stories: Detailed accounts of real experiences rather than opinions or hypotheticals.

Example prompt:

- “Tell me about the last time you bought a musical instrument.”

Why stories matter:

- They reveal real behavior.
- They include context, emotions, and decision-making processes.
- They provide richer insights than abstract answers.

## Analyzing Interview Findings

### Look for Patterns

After conducting interviews, analyze the data to find **recurring themes**.

- Patterns: Repeated behaviors, problems, or motivations appearing across multiple interviews.

Patterns help teams:

- Draw conclusions
- Create personas
- Develop user journeys
- Build workflows and scenarios

### Recognizing Saturation

You know you have conducted enough interviews when:

- New interviews repeat the same insights.
- You can predict participants’ responses.

This point is called **research saturation**.

- Saturation: When additional interviews produce little new information.

## Handling Surprises and Outliers

Not all findings will match common patterns.

- Outliers: Unusual responses that differ from the majority.
- Surprises: Unexpected behaviors or insights discovered during interviews.

Do not discard these insights.

Instead:

- Document them.
- Place them in a “parking lot” or research backlog.
- Use them as potential directions for future research.

These unusual findings can reveal:

- New opportunities
- Hidden user needs
- Future research questions
