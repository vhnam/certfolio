## The Role of Empathy in Design

### Definition of Design Empathy

- Empathy: The ability to understand another person’s context, emotions, goals, and motivations.
- Design empathy: Using insights from people’s real-world experiences to guide design decisions.

Empathy helps designers **inform and inspire decisions in the early stages of the design process**. By understanding users deeply, design teams can create solutions that address real needs and lead to meaningful innovation.

### Evolution of Design Focus

Design has evolved over time toward broader user experiences:

- Product design: Focused on creating physical tools or artifacts.
- Service design: Focused on delivering useful services around products.
- Experience design: Focused on the overall experience users have with systems, communities, and services.

Example in the biking industry:

- Past: Physical maps helped cyclists plan routes.
- Recent past: Route-planning services automated this process.
- Present: Online cycling communities allow users to share experiences and routes.

Designers must now understand **not only what users do but also how they feel during the experience**.

## The Importance of Subjective Information

### Objective vs. Subjective Understanding

To design meaningful experiences, designers must gather two types of insights:

- Objective information: What users do to accomplish tasks.
- Subjective information: What users feel, aim for, and experience while performing those tasks.

Subjective information reveals:

- Emotional drivers
- Motivations
- Personal goals
- Meaning behind actions

These insights help designers identify **new opportunities for innovation**.

### Embedding in the User’s Context

To collect subjective insights, designers should immerse themselves in the user’s environment.

Three key approaches:

1. Look at what people do.
2. Ask people to participate.
3. Try things yourself.

Using these approaches together helps designers build **both cognitive and emotional empathy**.

## The Four Steps to Developing Empathy

![Illustration of various ways to collect data and then analyze it.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-1-how-to-build-empathy-with-the-people-you-design-for/four-steps-to-developing-empathy.avif)

According to design research frameworks, empathy development follows four stages.

### Step 1: Discovery

- Enter the user’s world.
- Observe their environment and daily routines.
- Develop curiosity about their experiences.

Example: Visiting a food court workspace to understand employee workflows.

### Step 2: Immersion

- Participate in the user’s environment.
- Collect qualitative data through observation and interaction.
- Document experiences using notes, photos, or videos.

This step allows designers to see situations **from the user’s perspective**.

### Step 3: Connection

- Relate the user’s experiences to your own.
- Interpret emotional responses and motivations.
- Identify meaningful patterns within the collected data.

This stage helps transform observations into **empathic insights**.

### Step 4: Detachment

- Step back into the role of a designer.
- Analyze collected insights objectively.
- Use findings to generate design ideas.

Idea generation should occur **after understanding users**, not before.

### Simplified Research Activities

These four steps can also be summarized as:

- Collect: Gathering qualitative insights (immersion and connection).
- Analyze: Interpreting insights to generate ideas (detachment).

## Methods for Collecting Empathy Data

### Method 1: Observe What People Do

Designers observe users interacting with products in their natural environment.

Common observation techniques:

- Note-taking
- Sketching maps or workflows
- Photography
- Video recordings

Examples:

- Mapping nurses’ movements in a hospital ward to improve workflows.
- Recording a wheelchair user’s perspective inside a building to improve accessibility.

### Method 2: Ask People to Participate

Users actively contribute data about their experiences.

Common techniques include:

- Cultural probes: Activities that capture daily experiences and emotions.
- Participatory observation: Designers working alongside users.

Examples:

- Users documenting exercise routines through photos and diaries.
- Nursing home residents giving “thank you” cards to reveal meaningful relationships.

These methods help uncover **personal perspectives and motivations**.

### Method 3: Try the Experience Yourself

Designers directly experience users’ situations.

Examples:

- Working temporarily in a job role to understand daily challenges.
- Using simulation tools to experience physical limitations.

Example activities:

- Cleaning office buildings to understand cleaners’ experiences.
- Wearing a pregnancy simulation suit to understand mobility challenges.

This approach often generates **strong emotional insights**, though it is time-intensive.

## Combining Cognitive and Emotional Empathy

The three research methods support different types of empathy:

- Observation: More objective and cognitive insights.
- Participation: Balanced cognitive and emotional insights.
- Personal experience: More emotional and subjective insights.

Using multiple methods together helps designers develop **a deeper understanding of user experiences**.

## Practical Considerations in Empathy Research

Empathic research often requires significant time investment.

Instead of focusing on large statistical samples, designers prioritize:

- Contextual understanding
- Purposeful observation
- Insightful qualitative data

Example scenario:

A project focused on improving the integration of immigrant children in schools might involve:

- Observing classroom interactions.
- Participating in teaching activities.
- Interviewing students and teachers.

Although the sample size may be small, the insights gained can still be **highly valuable for design solutions**.

## Key Takeaways

- Empathy is essential for designing meaningful user experiences.
- Designers must balance subjective insights with objective analysis.
- The best insights come from embedding in the user’s context.
- Three main empathy-building approaches exist: observation, participation, and personal experience.
- The empathy process involves discovery, immersion, connection, and detachment.
- Empathic research focuses on **insights and understanding rather than statistical certainty**.
