## The Role of Empathy in Design Thinking

![Showing the paths of the design process as they can be circular.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-1-how-to-build-empathy-with-the-people-you-design-for/design-thinking-a-non-linear-process.avif)

### Why Empathy Matters

- Empathy: The ability to understand users’ needs, behaviors, emotions, and motivations.
- The **Empathize stage** is usually the starting point of the design thinking process.
- Designers must deeply understand users before defining problems or generating solutions.

Empathy helps designers discover:

- What users **need and want**
- How users **think, feel, and behave**
- Why users **act in certain ways in real-world situations**

Understanding these factors ensures that design solutions are **user-centered and meaningful**.

## Key Empathy Techniques

### 1. Assume a Beginner’s Mindset

- Beginner’s mindset: Observing users without relying on prior assumptions or expertise.
- Designers should approach research with **curiosity and openness**.

Key practices:

- Avoid judging user behavior.
- Question everything, even if the answer seems obvious.
- Listen carefully to what users say.

This mindset helps designers uncover **unexpected insights about user experiences**.

### 2. Photo- and Video-Based User Observations

Observing users in their natural environment can reveal important behavioral patterns.

Methods include:

- Taking photos or videos of users interacting with products.
- Recording contextual details such as environment and collaboration.

Benefits:

- Captures real user behaviors.
- Reveals emotional responses and interaction difficulties.
- Provides evidence that can be analyzed later.

These recordings help teams revisit observations and extract deeper insights.

### 3. Personal Photo and Video Journals

Instead of observing users directly, designers may ask users to **document their own experiences**.

Users can:

- Take photos of activities related to the problem.
- Record short videos of their interactions.
- Capture moments when difficulties occur.

Advantages:

- Shows authentic user contexts.
- Minimizes researcher interference.
- Provides real-life stories and experiences.

These materials help designers maintain a **human-centered perspective**.

### 4. The What–How–Why Method

This method helps designers interpret observations by asking three questions:

- **What?** – What exactly happened during the interaction.
- **How?** – How the user performed the action or task.
- **Why?** – Why the user behaved that way (motivations or emotions).

By analyzing behaviors this way, designers can move from **observable actions to deeper insights** about user motivations.

### 5. Conduct Empathy Interviews

One-on-one interviews are a powerful way to understand users.

Key characteristics:

- Conversations are usually semi-structured.
- Questions focus on users’ needs, experiences, and goals.

Best practices include:

- Preparing interview themes in advance.
- Asking **open-ended questions**.
- Encouraging users to share stories and emotions.

Interviews help designers gain **personal and detailed insights into user experiences**.

### 6. The 5 Whys Method

![The 5 Whys Method](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-1-how-to-build-empathy-with-the-people-you-design-for/5-whys-method.avif)

The **5 Whys** technique helps uncover root causes behind user behavior.

Process:

1. Ask a user about a behavior or experience.
2. Follow up by repeatedly asking **“Why?”**
3. Continue until the underlying motivation or cause is revealed.

This approach helps designers move beyond surface-level explanations and discover **deeper problems**.

### 7. Engage with Extreme Users

Extreme users are people whose behaviors or circumstances differ significantly from the majority.

Examples:

- Users with unusual constraints.
- People who rely heavily on a product or rarely use it.

Studying extreme users can:

- Highlight hidden problems.
- Reveal unmet needs.
- Inspire innovative solutions.

Insights from extreme users often **apply to mainstream users as well**.

### 8. Use Analogies for Inspiration

Analogies help designers compare problems across different domains.

Example:

- Hospital emergency operations compared to **race car pit stops**.

Benefits:

- Encourages creative thinking.
- Helps teams discover new solutions from other industries.
- Broadens the perspective on user challenges.

Analogies can spark **unexpected and innovative ideas**.

### 9. Bodystorming

Bodystorming involves **physically acting out user scenarios**.

Design teams recreate environments and simulate user experiences.

Examples:

- Simulating visual impairments using special glasses.
- Acting out real-life user tasks.

Benefits:

- Builds strong empathy through physical experience.
- Reveals challenges that might not appear during observation.
- Encourages creative problem solving.

### 10. Story Share-and-Capture

Teams should regularly share insights gathered during empathy research.

Activities include:

- Sharing stories from interviews and observations.
- Capturing key insights on notes or boards.
- Discussing patterns and important findings.

Benefits:

- Ensures the entire team understands the user perspective.
- Combines insights from different researchers.
- Inspires ideas for later design stages.

## Key Takeaways

- Empathy is essential for understanding users in design thinking.
- Designers must observe, interview, and engage directly with users to uncover meaningful insights.
- Techniques such as **observations, interviews, bodystorming, and extreme user analysis** help reveal user needs and motivations.
- Sharing stories and insights within the team strengthens the design process.

By applying these techniques, designers can better understand users and create **solutions that genuinely meet their needs**.
