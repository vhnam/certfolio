## Personas Overview

### Definition

- Persona: A **fictional character created from research data** that represents a type of user and describes their behaviors, needs, motivations, and goals.

Personas help designers understand **who they are designing for** and ensure the design process remains **user-centered**.

Important characteristics:

- Personas are **not real people**
- They are **based on real research data**
- They represent **patterns discovered across multiple users**

### Purpose of Personas

Personas help design teams:

- Understand user needs and motivations
- Communicate user insights to stakeholders
- Focus design decisions on real user behavior
- Evaluate design ideas from the user’s perspective

Using personas encourages designers to ask questions such as:

- How would this user react to a new feature?
- What goals does this user want to achieve?
- What needs or frustrations does this user have?

Personas were popularized by **Alan Cooper**, who introduced them in his book _The Inmates Are Running the Asylum_ (1999).

## Personas in Design Thinking

Personas are typically created during the **Define phase** of Design Thinking.

Process context:

1. Empathize: Collect research about users.
2. Define: Synthesize research insights and create personas.
3. Ideate: Use personas to guide idea generation.

Personas help guide ideation techniques such as:

- Brainstorming
- Worst Possible Idea
- SCAMPER

They ensure ideas remain **focused on real user needs**.

## Four Perspectives on Personas

According to Lene Nielsen, personas can be approached from **four different perspectives**.

### Goal-Directed Personas

![Illustration of a Goal Directed Persona tying the persona, goal, and scenario together.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-2-how-to-define-the-problem/goal-directed-persona.avif)

- Focus on **what users want to accomplish** with a product.

Key question:

- What does the user want to achieve?

Goal-directed personas analyze:

- User goals
- Preferred workflows
- Tasks users want to complete

This approach is based on the work of **Alan Cooper**.

### Role-Based Personas

- Focus on **user behavior and responsibilities**.

These personas are strongly **data-driven**, using both:

- Qualitative data
- Quantitative data

Role-based personas analyze:

- User job roles
- Context of product use
- Business objectives related to the role
- People impacted by the user’s work

Advocates of this perspective include:

- Jonathan Grudin
- John Pruitt
- Tamara Adlin

### Engaging Personas

Engaging personas focus on making personas **feel realistic and relatable**.

> “The engaging perspective is rooted in the ability of stories to produce involvement and insight.”
> – Lene Nielsen

These personas include:

- User emotions
- Personal background
- Psychological characteristics
- Stories about user behavior

The goal is to help designers **connect emotionally with users** and avoid treating them as stereotypes.

### Proto Personas

- Proto Personas: Personas created from **team assumptions rather than research data**.

Characteristics:

- Based on team experience and assumptions
- Created quickly during early product development
- Useful for forming an **initial hypothesis about users**

Limitations:

- May be inaccurate
- Should not replace research-based personas

Proto personas are often used as **early sketches of potential users** until real research is conducted.

## Key Takeaways

- Personas: Fictional representations of user types based on research data.
- Help designers understand **user needs, behaviors, goals, and motivations**.
- Created during the **Define phase** after synthesizing research insights.
- Used to guide **design decisions and ideation**.
- Four major persona perspectives:
  - Goal-Directed Personas
  - Role-Based Personas
  - Engaging Personas
  - Proto Personas
- Well-designed personas help teams create **human-centered products and services**.
