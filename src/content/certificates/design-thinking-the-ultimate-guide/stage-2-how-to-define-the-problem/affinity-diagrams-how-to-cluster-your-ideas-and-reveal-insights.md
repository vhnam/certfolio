## Affinity Diagrams Overview

### Definition

- Affinity Diagram: A method used to **organize large amounts of data into groups or themes based on relationships**.

Affinity diagrams help designers make sense of complex and messy information gathered during research or ideation.

Common types of information used in affinity diagrams include:

- Research findings
- User opinions
- Observations
- Quotes
- Ideas from brainstorming
- User needs
- Design issues

This technique is widely used in **Design Thinking**, especially in the:

- Define phase
- Ideate phase

### Purpose

Affinity diagrams help teams:

- Organize large volumes of information
- Discover patterns and relationships
- Synthesize research insights
- Identify key user needs and pain points

> “Organizing is what you do before you do something, so that when you do it, it is not all mixed up.”
> — A. A. Milne

## How Affinity Diagrams Work

Affinity diagramming helps transform **unstructured data** into **organized clusters of insights**. It allows teams to move from scattered information (e.g., many Post-it notes) to structured themes and patterns.

This process encourages:

- Team collaboration
- Discussion of insights
- Shared understanding of the problem space

## How to Create an Affinity Diagram

Affinity diagrams are typically created by a **design team**, especially those involved in research or ideation.

### Part 1: Group Similar Data

1. **Write data on notes:**
   Record each observation, quote, idea, or insight on **separate Post-it notes or cards**.
2. **Display the data:**
   Place the notes on a **wall, whiteboard, or table** so they can easily be moved.
3. **Start a group:**
   Choose a note randomly and make it the **first item in a group**.
4. **Compare notes:**
   Take another note and ask whether it is **similar or different** from the first.
5. **Cluster related items:**
   Continue grouping similar notes together and create new groups when necessary.

Notes:

- Some notes may remain **alone initially**.
- Groups can be **changed and reorganized at any time**.

### Part 2: Discuss and Clarify Clusters

![Illustration of clustering in an affinity diagram.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-2-how-to-define-the-problem/affinity-diagram-clustering.avif)

1. **Review clusters with the team:**
   Discuss the meaning of each group.
2. **Reorganize if necessary:**
   Move notes between groups if they fit better elsewhere.
3. **Name the clusters:**
   Give each group a **label that represents its main theme**.
4. **Connect related groups (optional):**
   Link clusters or create **larger super-groups** if themes are related.

By the end of this process, the data becomes **structured and easier to interpret**.

## Using Affinity Diagrams in Design

The organized insights generated from affinity diagrams can support several design activities:

- Creating empathy maps
- Building user personas
- Defining problem statements
- Supporting ideation sessions

Affinity diagrams help teams move from **raw research data to meaningful design insights**.

## Key Takeaways

- Affinity Diagram: A technique used to **cluster and organize large amounts of information**.
- Helps designers identify **patterns, themes, and relationships** in research data.
- Commonly used in **Define and Ideate phases** of Design Thinking.
- The process involves:
  1. Writing data on notes
  2. Grouping similar items
  3. Discussing clusters
  4. Naming themes
- The organized insights can then be used to create:
  - Empathy maps
  - Personas
  - Problem statements
  - Foundations for ideation
