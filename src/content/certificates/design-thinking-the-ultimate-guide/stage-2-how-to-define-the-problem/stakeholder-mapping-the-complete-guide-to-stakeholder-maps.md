## What Is a Stakeholder Map?

A **stakeholder map** is a visual representation of the individuals, groups, or organizations involved in or affected by a design project. These stakeholders may influence decisions, contribute to the process, or experience the outcomes of the project.

Stakeholder maps help design teams clearly understand the broader system surrounding their challenge. By identifying all relevant stakeholders, teams can ensure they involve the right people, consider different perspectives, and manage relationships effectively throughout the design process.

## Who Are Stakeholders?

![Illustration of various user persona groups that are stakeholders.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-2-how-to-define-the-problem/stakeholders.avif)

**Stakeholders are people or groups who can affect or be affected by a design project.** They may exist both inside and outside an organization.

Examples of stakeholders include:

- Organizational leaders or managers
- Team members and collaborators
- Target users and customers
- External partners or suppliers
- Members of the public who may experience indirect effects

Because stakeholders have different levels of influence and interest in a project, understanding their roles is essential for guiding communication and collaboration.

## Why Stakeholder Maps Are Useful

Stakeholder maps provide a structured way to analyze the ecosystem around a design challenge. They help teams:

- Identify the key individuals and groups involved in the project
- Understand where influence and decision-making power lie
- Ensure no important stakeholders are overlooked
- Gain insight into different stakeholder perspectives and relationships

This understanding allows designers to plan engagement strategies and anticipate potential challenges or support.

## Step 1: Identify Stakeholders

The first step in creating a stakeholder map is to **brainstorm all possible stakeholders**.

A common method is to conduct a short team brainstorming session where participants write down stakeholders on separate notes. The team then shares and groups similar stakeholders together.

During this step, it is important to think broadly and include anyone who might be affected directly or indirectly by the project.

## Step 2: Prioritize Stakeholders

![Illustration of a Influence versus Interest Chart with four quadrants: Keep Satisfied, Manage Closely, Monitor, Keep Informed.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-2-how-to-define-the-problem/influence-vs-interest-chart.avif)

Once stakeholders have been identified, they should be **prioritized according to their level of influence and interest** in the project.

This is often done using an **Influence–Interest matrix**, which divides stakeholders into four groups:

- **Manage Closely** – High influence and high interest. These stakeholders should be actively engaged and involved.
- **Keep Satisfied** – High influence but lower interest. They should remain satisfied without excessive communication.
- **Keep Informed** – High interest but lower influence. They may provide useful insights and should be regularly informed.
- **Monitor** – Low influence and low interest. These stakeholders require minimal attention but should still be observed.

This prioritization helps teams allocate their time and communication efforts more effectively.

## Step 3: Engage and Communicate with Stakeholders

After identifying and prioritizing stakeholders, the next step is to **engage them through communication and collaboration**.

Design teams should gather insights from stakeholders by asking questions such as:

- How will the project affect you?
- What motivates your interest in this project?
- What information would you like to receive?
- What is the best way to communicate with you?
- Who influences your opinions, and whom do you influence?

These conversations help teams understand stakeholder expectations and uncover additional insights that may shape the design process.

If stakeholders express concerns or resistance, teams should also plan how to address these challenges, gain support, or manage potential opposition.

## The Takeaway

Stakeholder maps help designers **identify, prioritize, and understand the people involved in or affected by a design project**. By mapping stakeholders and analyzing their influence and interest, teams can develop more effective collaboration and communication strategies.

A well-developed stakeholder map ensures that important voices are included in the design process and increases the likelihood of gaining support and alignment throughout the project.
