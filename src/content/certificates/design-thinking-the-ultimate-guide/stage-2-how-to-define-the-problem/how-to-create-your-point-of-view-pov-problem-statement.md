---

## What a Point of View (POV) Is

A **Point of View (POV)** is a clear problem statement that defines the design challenge in a human-centered way. It explains **who the user is, what their needs are, and why those needs matter**. The POV is created during the **Define phase** of design thinking and guides the rest of the design process.

A strong POV helps teams focus on real user problems rather than jumping directly to solutions.

## Why the POV Is Important

The POV plays a key role in design thinking because it:

- Provides a clear focus for the design challenge
- Guides ideation and innovation
- Helps communicate the problem to stakeholders and team members
- Ensures the design process remains centered on user needs

Without a clear POV, teams may struggle to define the problem and generate meaningful solutions.

## Characteristics of a Good POV

A well-written POV should include several important qualities.

### User-Centered

The problem statement must focus on **specific users and their needs**, not on technology, business goals, or product features.

### Balanced Scope

A POV should be **broad enough** to encourage creativity but **narrow enough** to remain manageable and solvable.

### Action-Oriented

Good POV statements are actionable and often **start with a verb**, such as “create,” “define,” or “improve.”

### Inspirational

A strong POV should inspire teams to generate innovative ideas and motivate them to solve the problem.

## Steps to Define a POV

### Step 1: Define the User

Identify the target user you want to design for. This can be done using tools like **personas or empathy maps** based on research such as interviews and observations.

### Step 2: Identify User Needs

![Illustration of a Point of View Template.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-2-how-to-define-the-problem/pov-template.avif)

Determine the most important needs of the user. These needs should usually be expressed as **verbs**, describing actions or goals.

### Step 3: Identify Insights

Insights provide context about why the need exists. They help reveal deeper motivations or patterns discovered during research.

### Step 4: Write the POV Statement

![Illustration of a Point of View Madlib.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-2-how-to-define-the-problem/pov-madlib.avif)

Combine the user, need, and insight into a simple structure known as a **POV Madlib**:

```
[User . . . (description)] needs [Need . . . (verb)] because [Insight . . . (compelling statement)]
```

Example:
“A city resident needs access to shared transportation because owning a private car is expensive and environmentally harmful.”

## How to Evaluate a Good POV

A strong POV should:

- Provide a clear focus
- Inspire creative thinking
- Guide the ideation process
- Help evaluate possible design solutions
- Capture attention and communicate the challenge effectively

If a POV meets these criteria, it can successfully guide the design process.

## Well-Framed vs. Restrictive Challenges

Traditional problem statements often suggest a solution immediately, which can limit creativity.

For example:

- **Restrictive:** “Design a poster to increase event sign-ups.”
- **Well-framed:** “Why are people not signing up for the event?”

Design thinking encourages **explorative challenges** that focus on understanding user needs rather than applying quick fixes.

## Iterative Nature of the POV

The POV is not fixed. As new insights emerge during research, testing, or prototyping, the problem statement should be **revisited and refined**.

Design thinking is an iterative process, meaning teams may return to earlier stages to improve their understanding of the problem.

## Key Takeaway

A Point of View (POV) helps define the design challenge by clearly describing the **user, their needs, and the insights behind those needs**. A strong POV is user-centered, actionable, and inspiring. By framing the problem correctly, design teams can generate better ideas and create more effective solutions.
