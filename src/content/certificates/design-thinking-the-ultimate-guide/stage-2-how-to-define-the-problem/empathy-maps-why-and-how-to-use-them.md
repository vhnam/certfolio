## What an Empathy Map Is

An empathy map is a visualization tool used to organize and summarize insights gathered from user research. It helps design teams better understand users by capturing their experiences, behaviors, thoughts, and emotions.

Empathy maps are often used during the **Define stage** of the design thinking process. They help transform research findings into a clear understanding of user needs, which can then guide problem statements and design solutions.

## Why Empathy Maps Are Important

### Understanding Users

Empathy maps help teams interpret research data and gain deeper insight into user needs. They allow designers to:

- Remove bias from research interpretations
- Organize and categorize research data
- Add depth to user personas
- Understand the reasons behind user behaviors and motivations
- Identify gaps in research knowledge

By looking at users from multiple perspectives, teams gain a holistic view of the user’s experiences and problems.

### Communicating Research Insights

Empathy maps also make it easier to share research findings with others. They help:

- Involve stakeholders and team members in the design process
- Create shared understanding of user needs
- Present research insights clearly and visually
- Support better decision-making during product development

## The Four Quadrants of an Empathy Map

Most empathy maps are divided into four quadrants that describe different aspects of user behavior and experience.

### Says

This section contains direct quotes from users gathered during interviews or research sessions. It reflects what users openly express about their experiences.

### Does

This quadrant records the actions users take and how they behave when interacting with a product or service.

### Thinks

The “Thinks” section represents what users are thinking during their experience. Ideally, this information comes directly from research questions, but sometimes it is inferred from user behavior.

### Feels

This quadrant focuses on the emotional state of the user. It captures feelings such as frustration, excitement, worry, or satisfaction that influence user behavior.

## Additional Sections: Pains and Gains

![Illustration of the pieces of an empathy map.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-2-how-to-define-the-problem/empathy-map-expanded.avif)

Some empathy maps also include two extra sections:

- **Pains:** The problems, frustrations, fears, or obstacles users experience.
- **Gains:** The goals users want to achieve and the outcomes they desire.

These insights help designers clearly identify opportunities to improve the user experience.

## How to Create an Empathy Map

### Define Scope and Purpose

Start by deciding what user group or persona the empathy map represents and what insights the team hopes to gain.

### Conduct User Research

Collect data through methods such as interviews, observations, or usability testing to understand user behavior and motivations.

### Fill the Empathy Map

Organize research findings into the four quadrants using notes or sticky notes. Begin with objective data (Says and Does), then move to more interpretive insights (Thinks and Feels).

### Analyze the Data

Group related information into clusters and identify patterns, repeated themes, contradictions, or missing insights.

### Identify User Needs

Use the patterns discovered in the empathy map to identify core user needs. These insights help define a user-centered problem statement and guide the design process.

## Using Maslow’s Hierarchy to Understand Needs

![Illustration of Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs.](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/stage-2-how-to-define-the-problem/maslows-hierachy-of-needs.avif)

Designers can analyze user needs through the framework of **Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs**, which organizes human needs into levels such as physiological needs, safety, belonging, esteem, and self-actualization.

Understanding which level of need users are trying to satisfy helps designers create solutions that address meaningful problems.

## What Empathy Maps Are Not

### Not a Customer Journey Map

Customer journey maps visualize the entire experience across multiple touchpoints, while empathy maps focus on understanding a user’s thoughts, actions, and emotions.

### Not the Same as Personas

Personas describe user characteristics and demographics, while empathy maps explore deeper aspects such as behaviors, motivations, and emotional experiences.

## Key Takeaway

Empathy maps help transform raw user research into clear insights about user behavior, motivations, and emotions. By organizing this information visually, teams gain a deeper understanding of users and can define user-centered problems that guide the design process and lead to better products.
