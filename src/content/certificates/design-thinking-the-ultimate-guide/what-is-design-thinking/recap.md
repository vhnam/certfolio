## Overview

Lesson 1 introduces the **context and key concepts of design thinking**. The goal is to build a foundational understanding of the methodology, including where it originated, how the process works, and how it can be applied to innovation and problem-solving.

## The Context of Design Thinking

![The Context of Design Thinking](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/what-is-design-thinking/design-thinking-key-wakeaways.avif)

This lesson explains why design thinking has become an important approach to innovation.

Key ideas include:

- Design thinking is powerful because it challenges assumptions and can transform innovation processes.
- The concept of design thinking dates back to the 1960s, emerging from architecture, engineering and industrial design.
- The design thinking process consists of five iterative, non-linear stages: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype and Test.
- Organizations can adopt design thinking through an innovation mindset, cross-functional teams and a creative work environment.

## The Design Thinking Process

The design thinking process consists of **five stages**:

- **Empathize** – Understand users and their needs.
- **Define** – Clearly articulate the problem.
- **Ideate** – Generate ideas and possible solutions.
- **Prototype** – Build early versions of solutions.
- **Test** – Evaluate solutions with users.

These stages are **iterative and non-linear**, meaning teams often move back and forth between them as they learn more about the problem and possible solutions.

## Applying Design Thinking in Organizations

Organizations can adopt design thinking by creating an environment that supports innovation, including:

- An **innovation mindset**
- **Cross-functional collaboration**
- A **creative working environment**

These elements help teams apply design thinking effectively to real-world challenges.

## Learning Through Discussion

Learners were encouraged to **share their initial thoughts about design thinking with peers**. This activity helps broaden perspectives and can spark ideas about how the methodology might be applied to personal or professional projects.
