## What is Design Thinking

### Definition

- Design Thinking: An **iterative, non-linear problem-solving approach** focused on understanding users, challenging assumptions, redefining problems, and creating innovative solutions through prototyping and testing.

### Key Characteristics

- **Human-centered:** Focuses deeply on the people who use a product or service.
- **Iterative:** Ideas are continuously refined through testing and feedback.
- **Non-linear:** Stages can occur in parallel or repeat in any order.
- **Experimental:** Encourages prototyping, testing, and learning from failures.
- **Problem reframing:** Looks beyond symptoms to discover root causes.

### Core Principles

- Understand users and their needs.
- Question assumptions and problem statements.
- Explore many possible solutions before selecting one.
- Use experimentation and prototypes to learn quickly.

## The Five Phases of Design Thinking

Design thinking is commonly represented through five stages proposed by the **Stanford d.school**.

1. **Empathize:** Research and understand users, their behaviors, needs, and motivations.
2. **Define:** Clearly articulate the core problem based on insights from research.
3. **Ideate:** Generate a wide range of creative ideas and potential solutions.
4. **Prototype:** Build simple, tangible versions of ideas to explore solutions.
5. **Test:** Evaluate prototypes with users to gather feedback and refine solutions.

Characteristics of the phases:

- Can happen **in parallel**.
- Can be **repeated or revisited** at any point.
- Aim to improve understanding of both **problem and solution**.

## Human-Centered Design

### Definition

- Human-centered design: An approach that prioritizes **people, their needs, and their experiences** when designing solutions.

### Key Concepts

- Focus on real people rather than abstract data.
- Understand emotional, behavioral, and contextual factors.
- Involve users directly in the design process.

### Community-Driven Design

- Community-driven design: A form of human-centered design where **affected communities actively participate in creating solutions**.

Principles:

- People experiencing the problem should help shape the solution.
- Designers act as facilitators and collaborators.
- Local knowledge and creativity are essential.

## Thinking Beyond Traditional Problem-Solving

### Outside-the-Box Thinking

People using design thinking:

- Develop new ways of thinking beyond conventional methods.
- Analyze how users interact with products and services.
- Challenge assumptions and test their validity.

### Root Cause Exploration

Design thinking avoids solving superficial symptoms.

Methods include:

- Investigating underlying causes.
- Asking deeper questions such as **“Why does this problem exist?”**
- Using techniques like the **Five Whys**.

Example insight:

- Solving symptoms often causes problems to reappear.
- Identifying root causes leads to sustainable solutions.

## Design Thinking for Teams and Organizations

### Cross-Functional Collaboration

Design thinking encourages multidisciplinary teamwork.

Participants may include:

- Designers
- Engineers
- Business strategists
- Researchers
- Product managers
- Stakeholders from different departments

Benefits:

- Diverse perspectives improve solutions.
- Complex problems are better understood.
- Teams align around user needs.

### Organizational Impact

Design thinking can be applied across an organization:

- Product development
- Service design
- Business innovation
- Strategic decision-making

It enables innovation by integrating:

- Human desirability
- Technical feasibility
- Business viability

## Design Thinking as Art and Science

Design thinking combines two complementary approaches.

### Creative Exploration (Art)

- Intuition
- Creativity
- Pattern recognition
- Storytelling
- Emotional insight

### Analytical Research (Science)

- User research
- Data analysis
- Experimentation
- Prototype testing
- Evidence-based decision-making

The combination helps reveal **new insights and innovative strategies**.

## Challenging Mental Models

### Cognitive Constraints

Humans naturally develop habitual thinking patterns based on:

- Repeated activities
- Expertise in a specific field
- Familiar problem-solving methods

These patterns can limit creativity.

### Example Insight

A classic story illustrates this:

- A truck becomes stuck under a low bridge.
- Experts suggest dismantling the truck or bridge.
- A child proposes **deflating the tires**.

Lesson:

- Simple solutions may be overlooked due to rigid thinking patterns.

## The Role of Stories in Design Thinking

Stories help designers:

- Understand real human experiences.
- Imagine possible solutions.
- Identify design opportunities.

Practical use:

- Observe real-life situations.
- Document good and bad design examples.
- Use photos, notes, and stories to analyze experiences.

## Key Takeaways

- Design thinking is a **non-linear, iterative process** for solving complex problems.
- The process consists of **five stages**: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test.
- It focuses on **deep understanding of users and their needs**.
- It encourages **experimentation, creativity, and questioning assumptions**.
- It integrates **human-centered insight with analytical research**.
- It is **not limited to designers**—anyone in an organization can apply it.
- Its goal is to generate **innovative solutions that improve products, services, and systems**.
