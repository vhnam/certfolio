## Overview

### Definition

- Design Thinking: A human-centered methodology used to solve complex and ill-defined problems.

Key characteristics:

- Focus on **understanding human needs**
- Encourages **creative ideation**
- Uses **experimentation and testing**
- Works through **iteration and learning**

Design thinking is **non-linear and iterative**, meaning stages can repeat, overlap, or occur in parallel.

The framework used in this lesson comes from the **Hasso Plattner Institute of Design at Stanford**, which popularized a widely used **five-stage model**.

## The Five Stages of Design Thinking

### Empathize

![Empathize](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/what-is-design-thinking/empathize.avif)

Purpose:

- Understand users and their real needs.

Activities:

- User research
- Observations
- Interviews
- Immersion in the user’s environment

Goal:

- Develop a deep understanding of users’ **experiences, motivations, and problems**.

### Define

![Define](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/what-is-design-thinking/define.avif)

Purpose:

- Synthesize research findings into a clear problem statement.

Activities:

- Analyze insights gathered during the Empathize stage
- Identify key patterns and user needs
- Frame the problem in a **human-centered way**

Example:

- Instead of business-focused statements, define problems based on **user needs and experiences**.

Goal:

- Create a clear **problem statement** that guides ideation.

### Ideate

![Ideate](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/what-is-design-thinking/ideate.avif)

Purpose:

- Generate a wide range of possible solutions.

Activities:

- Brainstorming
- Brainwriting
- Worst Possible Idea
- SCAMPER and other ideation techniques

Approach:

- Encourage **divergent thinking**
- Explore multiple perspectives

Goal:

- Produce many potential solutions before narrowing down promising ideas.

### Prototype

![Prototype](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/what-is-design-thinking/prototype.avif)

Purpose:

- Build simple versions of potential solutions.

Activities:

- Create low-cost models
- Develop wireframes or rough concepts
- Test ideas internally or with small groups

Goal:

- Explore and validate potential solutions quickly.

Prototypes help reveal:

- Design limitations
- User reactions
- Areas for improvement.

### Test

![Test](../../../../assets/images/design-thinking-the-ultimate-guide/what-is-design-thinking/test.avif)

Purpose:

- Evaluate prototypes with real users.

Activities:

- Usability testing
- User feedback sessions
- Observing user behavior

Goal:

- Gain deeper insight into how users **think, behave, and interact with the solution**.

Testing often reveals new insights that may require revisiting earlier stages.

## The Non-Linear Nature of Design Thinking

Design thinking does **not follow a strict sequence**.

Key characteristics:

- Stages may happen simultaneously.
- Teams can revisit earlier stages.
- New insights can redefine the problem.

Example cycles:

- Testing may reveal new insights → return to **Ideate** or **Prototype**.
- User feedback may change the **problem definition**.

This creates a **continuous learning loop**.

## Benefits of the Iterative Process

- Encourages constant learning
- Expands the solution space
- Improves understanding of users
- Leads to more innovative solutions

Knowledge gained in later stages can **inform and refine earlier stages**.

## Key Takeaways

- Design thinking is a **human-centered, iterative problem-solving approach**.
- The five core stages are:

1. Empathize
2. Define
3. Ideate
4. Prototype
5. Test

- The process is **flexible and non-linear**, allowing teams to repeat stages and refine solutions continuously.
- The ultimate goal is to **deeply understand users and create innovative solutions that meet real human needs**.
