## Overview

Many leading organizations and institutions such as **IDEO, IBM and the Stanford d.school** actively teach and apply design thinking. While their frameworks may differ slightly, they all share the same core methodology and mindset. Learning from their videos, tools and resources can help practitioners understand how design thinking works in real-world contexts and apply it effectively in their own work.

### Design Thinking vs. Engineering Thinking

[![Want to Make Better Decisions? Know the Difference between Engineering and Design Thinking](https://img.youtube.com/vi/q7LRxKHdao8/0.jpg)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=q7LRxKHdao8)

In this video, Stanford lecturer Dave Evans explains:

- The difference between **tame and wicked problems**
- Different problem-solving approaches (analytical vs design thinking)
- How **prototyping** helps explore possibilities

He emphasizes that design thinking is an **innovation methodology**, not just a design skill.

#### Dave Evans’ Types of Problems

##### Tame Problems

**Definition:**
Problems that have a **clear definition and known solution**, and can usually be solved using established methods or rules.

**Characteristics**

- The problem is **well-defined**.
- A **correct solution already exists**.
- The solution can be **repeated or optimized**.
- Usually solved with **engineering or analytical methods**.

**Example**

- Calculating the load capacity of a bridge.
- Implementing a known algorithm to sort data.

##### Analytical Problems

**Definition:**
Problems that start with a **clear premise or model** and can be solved through **logical analysis, mathematics, or optimization**.

**Characteristics**

- Based on **data, formulas, or logical reasoning**.
- Solutions come from **analysis and deduction**.
- Often solved by **scientific or mathematical approaches**.
- Forecasting revenue using statistical models.
- Determining the shortest path in a network.

##### Wicked Problems

**Definition:**
Complex problems with **no single correct answer**, where the problem itself is difficult to define and solutions evolve over time.

**Characteristics**

- **Uncertain or changing context**
- **Multiple stakeholders and perspectives**
- **No clear right or wrong solution**
- Require **experimentation, iteration, and empathy**

**Example**

- Designing better healthcare systems.
- Improving urban transportation.
- Reducing climate change impacts.

## Useful Design Thinking Methods and Tools

### Human-Centered Design Methods (IDEO.org)

Link: https://www.designkit.org/methods.html

IDEO.org provides a large collection of design thinking methods organized into three phases:

- **Inspiration** – understanding users and context
- **Ideation** – generating and refining ideas
- **Implementation** – turning ideas into real solutions

These tools help designers keep **people at the center of the design process**.

### Design Thinking Artifacts (Stanford d.school)

Link: https://dschool.stanford.edu/innovate/tools

The Stanford d.school offers practical materials to support design thinking activities, such as:

- Visual aids
- Content cards
- Workshop tools
- Collaboration resources

These artifacts help teams **facilitate workshops and apply design thinking in real environments**.

## The Take Away

There are many resources available to help people learn and practice design thinking, including videos, toolkits and practical methods. These materials make it easier for beginners and professionals alike to **understand the mindset, tools and processes of design thinking**, and apply them to solve complex problems.
