## Origins of Design Thinking

### Early Emergence

- Design Thinking: A methodology that evolved from attempts to understand how designers solve complex problems.
- Timeframe: Early ideas emerged during the **1950s and 1960s**.
- Influences:
  - Architecture
  - Engineering
  - Industrial design
  - Cognitive science
  - Business innovation

Major historical drivers:

- Rapid technological change
- Global societal shifts after **World War II**
- Increasing need for structured approaches to solving complex problems

## The 1960s: Design Becomes Scientific

### Design Science Movement

Researchers attempted to make design more systematic by applying scientific methods.

Key ideas:

- Analyze design processes objectively.
- Study how designers think and solve problems.
- Develop structured methodologies for innovation.

Key contributor:

- Buckminster Fuller: Advocated a **“design science revolution”** combining science, technology, and rational thinking to solve global problems.

## Wicked Problems

### Definition

- Wicked Problems: Complex, multidimensional problems that are difficult to define and solve.

Characteristics:

- No clear problem definition
- No single correct solution
- Solutions often create new problems
- Involve many interconnected factors

Key contributor:

- Horst Rittel: Coined the term **“wicked problem”** in the 1960s.

Examples:

- Urban planning
- Environmental issues
- Social inequality
- Public health challenges

Design thinking emerged partly as a response to these complex problems.

## The 1970s: Design as a Way of Thinking

### Herbert Simon’s Contributions

Herbert A. Simon introduced the idea of **design as a way of thinking** in:

- _The Sciences of the Artificial_ (1969)

Key concepts introduced:

- Prototyping
- Iterative experimentation
- Observing system behavior to understand problems

Simon emphasized that:

- Building and testing solutions helps understand complex systems.

### Visual Thinking

Robert H. McKim expanded the concept in:

- _Experiences in Visual Thinking_ (1973)

Key ideas:

- Problem-solving involves both analytical and creative thinking.
- Combining **left-brain and right-brain thinking** improves innovation.
- Visualization aids understanding and ideation.

## The 1980s: Designerly Ways of Knowing

### Nigel Cross

Nigel Cross studied how designers approach problem-solving.

Key insight:

- Designers use **“designerly ways of knowing”**, a unique cognitive approach distinct from scientific reasoning.

### Bryan Lawson’s Experiment

Lawson compared how **scientists and designers solve problems**.

Results:

- Scientists:
  - Analyze problems systematically.
  - Search for the optimal solution.
- Designers:
  - Generate many possible solutions quickly.
  - Test and eliminate ineffective solutions.

Conclusion:

- Designers are **solution-focused**, not purely problem-focused.

Concept introduced:

- Satisficing: Producing a satisfactory solution rather than seeking the theoretically optimal one.

## 1987: Architectural Perspectives

### Peter Rowe

Peter Rowe’s book _Design Thinking_ explored:

- How architects approach creative problem-solving.
- The cognitive processes behind design decisions.

Contribution:

- Provided a structured description of how designers explore and invent solutions.

## The 1990s: Design Thinking Goes Mainstream

### IDEO

IDEO played a major role in popularizing design thinking.

Key contributions:

- Simplified terminology
- Practical toolkits
- Human-centered design methods
- Accessible innovation frameworks

IDEO helped make design thinking usable by **non-designers and businesses**.

### Richard Buchanan

Richard Buchanan expanded the theory of design thinking.

Key idea:

- Design thinking integrates knowledge from **multiple specialized disciplines**.

Purpose:

- Solve complex modern problems through a **holistic approach**.

## 2004: Stanford d.school

### Founding of the d.school

David Kelley founded the **Hasso Plattner Institute of Design at Stanford (d.school)**.

Goals:

- Teach design thinking methodologies
- Promote human-centered innovation
- Encourage multidisciplinary collaboration

Impact:

- Helped spread design thinking globally through education.

## Modern Design Thinking

### Current Adoption

Design thinking is now widely used in:

- Technology companies
- Business organizations
- Universities
- Government and public innovation

Key characteristics of modern design thinking:

- Human-centered design
- Multidisciplinary collaboration
- Iterative experimentation
- Rapid prototyping

### Wicked Problems Today

Modern thinkers often refer to wicked problems as:

- Complex socio-technical systems

Characteristics:

- Difficult to define
- Difficult to solve completely
- Require continuous improvement

Goal:

- Improve systems and people's lives progressively rather than achieve a perfect solution.

## Key Takeaways

- Design thinking emerged gradually from research in **architecture, engineering, and cognitive science**.
- The concept developed significantly between the **1950s and 1980s**.
- Major contributors include:
  - Herbert Simon
  - Horst Rittel
  - Nigel Cross
  - Bryan Lawson
  - Peter Rowe
- Organizations such as **IDEO** and institutions like **Stanford’s d.school** helped popularize design thinking globally.
- Today, design thinking is widely used to tackle **complex human-centered challenges and wicked problems**.
