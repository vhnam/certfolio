---

## Understanding MVP in UX

### Definition of MVP

- Minimum Viable Product (MVP): A lean product version with enough features to release quickly and validate ideas.
- Minimal Usable Product (MUP): A variation emphasizing **usability**, ensuring the product is functional for users.
- Minimal Desirable Product: Focuses on **user needs and desirability**, ensuring the product is valuable and appealing.

Key insight:

- MVP should not only be **viable**, but also **usable and desirable** for users.

## Strategic vs Tactical MVP

### Strategic Perspective

- MVP should be **planned carefully**, not randomly assembled.
- Product decisions must balance three factors:
  - UX
  - Technical feasibility
  - Business goals

Strategic MVP ensures:

- The product delivers meaningful value.
- The user experience remains coherent.
- Future features can expand logically.

### Tactical Implementation

During development planning (e.g., sprint planning or release discussions):

- Teams decide what features to include in a release.
- UX must help guide which elements are essential to maintain usability.

Risk:

- Removing too many UI elements can break the user experience.

Example metaphor:

- Jenga: Removing pieces from the interface like pulling blocks from a tower can cause the whole system to collapse.

## Risks of Poor MVP Decisions

### Feature Removal Without Planning

Common scenario:

- Teams remove interface components and say:
  - “We'll add it later”
  - “Phase 2”
  - “Next release”

Problems:

- Those features often **never return**.
- The product becomes **incomplete or confusing**.

### Incomplete User Experiences

Early startup culture sometimes assumed:

- A product labeled **“beta”** could be incomplete.

However:

- Even early releases must **feel complete enough for users**.

Requirement:

- Users must still be able to **successfully complete tasks**.

## Designing a Meaningful MVP

### Deliver Complete Task Experiences

When releasing an MVP:

- Each release should support **complete user tasks**.
- Features should not be partially implemented in ways that prevent task completion.

Good approach:

1. Identify the core task the product supports.
2. Ensure the MVP allows users to complete that task.
3. Expand features later in logical increments.

### Add Features in Logical Phases

Future features should:

- Extend existing capabilities.
- Support new user tasks.
- Maintain coherence with the existing experience.

Avoid:

- Adding disconnected micro-features that fragment the experience.

## Role of UX in MVP Decisions

### UX as an Advocate for Product Integrity

UX professionals (especially UX leaders) should:

- Advocate for **usable and coherent product releases**.
- Ensure MVP decisions **do not break the experience**.
- Guide teams when cutting or phasing features.

UX responsibility:

- Maintain a balance between **speed and usability**.

### UX in the Product Decision Triad

Effective product decisions involve three disciplines:

- UX: User needs, usability, desirability.
- Technology: Technical feasibility.
- Business: Market and business viability.

These elements must work together.

Key principle:

- UX is **not a finishing step** after technical and business decisions.

Instead:

- UX is a **core partner** in shaping the product.

### Avoiding Technical Bias

Technical bias occurs when:

- Product decisions are driven primarily by engineering perspectives.
- UX and user-centered design are undervalued.

Consequences:

- Poor user experience
- Reduced product value
- Missed business opportunities

Solution:

- Treat UX as an **equal partner** alongside business and engineering.

## Key Takeaways

- MVP should also consider **usability and desirability**, not only viability.
- Cutting features randomly can **damage the user experience**.
- MVP releases must allow users to **complete meaningful tasks**.
- UX should influence MVP decisions **from the beginning**, not at the end.
- Successful products balance **UX, technology, and business priorities**.
