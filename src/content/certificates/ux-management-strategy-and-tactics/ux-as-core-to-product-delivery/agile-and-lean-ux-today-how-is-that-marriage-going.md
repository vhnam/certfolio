## Agile UX Collaboration

### Relationship Between Agile and UX

- Agile + UX: Should work closely together to deliver **user-centered products quickly**.
- Common problem: In many organizations, agile development and UX operate separately.
- Goal: Fully integrate UX into agile workflows so that **design, development, and user needs stay aligned**.

### Why Integration Matters

- UX provides **real user insights** that guide product decisions.
- Agile focuses on **rapid delivery of working software**.
- When combined effectively, they support **customer-centered development**, which is the core intention of agile.

## Good Agile UX Practices

### Early Research (Cycle Zero)

- Cycle Zero: Research phase conducted **before the first sprint**.
- Field studies: Observing and studying users in real contexts.
- Purpose: Ensure user stories and product requirements are **based on real behavior rather than assumptions**.

### Just-in-Time UX Support

UX designers collaborate directly with development teams during sprints.

Typical workflow:

1. Developers encounter a design challenge.
2. UX joins a quick discussion (call, sketch, whiteboard).
3. A rapid solution is created.
4. Development continues without blocking progress.

Benefits:

- Fast problem-solving
- Continuous collaboration
- Maintaining usability without slowing development

### Validating Product Requirements

UX research helps ensure product requirements are realistic.

Key practices:

- Scenario-driven design: Designing based on real user scenarios.
- Outside-in design: Starting from **user needs**, not internal assumptions.

Result:

- User stories reflect **actual user behavior**.

### Supporting Product Owners and MVP Decisions

UX teams help product owners make better decisions about **Minimum Viable Products (MVPs)**.

UX contributions:

- Share user research insights
- Identify the most valuable features
- Help prioritize features based on user needs

### Collaborative Design (Design Studios)

- Design Studio: A collaborative design workshop where team members sketch ideas together.
- Participants may include developers, support staff, managers, and designers.

Purpose:

- Encourage cross-disciplinary creativity
- Share perspectives across roles
- Generate better design solutions

Examples:

- A customer support employee produced effective wireframes.
- A business development manager created strong mobile design sketches.

Insight:

- Valuable design ideas can come from **any role in the team**.

### Continuous User Testing

User testing should occur frequently during development.

Typical cadence:

- Weekly testing in some teams
- Every two weeks in others
- At least once a month

Goals:

- Bring real user feedback into sprint planning
- Keep development aligned with user needs
- Maintain an **outside-in design perspective**

### Summary of Good Agile UX

Good agile UX practices include:

- Field research conducted during **Cycle Zero**
- UX providing **just-in-time solutions** for developers
- Product requirements **validated through user research**
- UX helping product owners **stay grounded in MVP decisions**
- **Collaborative design workshops** (design studios)
- **Frequent usability testing** during development

## Signs of Bad Agile UX

### Skipping Research and Testing

Common problems:

- Teams claim they **do not have time for usability testing**.
- Teams do not understand the value of **field studies**.

Consequences:

- Products are built without validating usability.
- User needs are ignored.

### UX Excluded from Agile Processes

UX teams are sometimes excluded from key agile activities:

- Stand-up meetings
- Backlog grooming
- Sprint planning
- Retrospectives

Problem:

- UX becomes disconnected from development decisions.

Good practice:

- UX does not need to attend every meeting, but **should participate regularly and influence decisions**.

### Engineering-Dominated Priorities

In some teams, discussions focus mainly on:

- Data models
- Technical architecture
- Development constraints

Risk:

- User needs become secondary.

UX teams help:

- Reframe priorities around **what users actually need**.

### UX Not Treated as a Priority

Indicators:

- UX considered optional
- Product decisions driven only by engineering concerns
- User experience not influencing development priorities

### Quality Sacrificed for Speed

Misinterpretation of agile:

- Agile becomes **“build fast without UX.”**

Result:

- Usability issues
- Poor user experience
- Lower product quality

### Summary of Bad Agile UX

Bad agile UX is characterized by:

- No time allocated for **user testing**
- Lack of understanding of **field research**
- UX excluded from **stand-ups, backlog discussions, and sprints**
- UX not prioritized in decision-making
- Development priorities based on **engineering needs instead of user needs**
- **Product quality compromised**

## Agile Principles and Customer-Centered Development

### Agile’s Core Intent

The original goal of agile is to:

- Deliver working software frequently
- Stay close to customers
- Avoid siloed work between teams

This naturally aligns with UX principles.

### UX Role in Agile Teams

UX helps agile teams:

- Understand real user needs
- Validate product decisions
- Prioritize meaningful features
- Maintain usability and product quality

Key takeaway:

- Agile and UX are naturally aligned because both aim to deliver **valuable, user-centered products quickly**.
