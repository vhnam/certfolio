## The Shift in Product Management

### From Waterfall to Agile

Product management has evolved significantly with the shift from **waterfall development** to **agile development**.

Traditional waterfall development:

- Long planning cycles
- Heavy upfront decision-making
- Product managers defining requirements before development begins

Example timeline evolution:

- Intel-style development: Long planning cycles (around one year)
- HP iterative cycles: About six months
- Modern agile: Iterations as short as **one week**

Impact of agile:

- Products are built through **continuous iteration**
- Product decisions happen **collaboratively with the team**

### Team-Based Product Creation

In modern agile environments:

- Product managers no longer dictate every feature.
- The product is created **collaboratively by the team**.

Team members involved include:

- Product managers
- UX designers
- Developers

Key change:

- UX is no longer a separate service used occasionally.
- UX is integrated into **daily product development work**.

## Evolving Role of UX in Product Development

### UX as a Core Process Component

Historically, UX often functioned as a **service bureau**:

- Product managers defined requirements.
- UX teams were asked to review or polish the interface.

Typical example of the old model:

- UX teams providing simple validation like confirming UI elements.

Modern agile teams treat UX differently:

- UX contributes to the **ongoing design of the product**
- UX decisions happen **continuously during development**

Example workflow:

- New feature proposed
- UX evaluates how it fits into the existing experience
- Team collaborates to integrate the feature smoothly

## Product Manager Attitudes Toward UX

### Two Types of Product Managers

Organizations often have two general types of product managers.

#### Protective Product Managers

These managers:

- Guard product requirements closely
- See UX as disruptive
- Fear that usability insights may derail the roadmap

Motivation:

- Desire to maintain control over the product direction.

#### Collaborative Product Managers

These managers:

- Value UX insights
- See user research as **validation for product decisions**
- Work closely with UX teams

Benefits:

- Stronger product validation
- Better design decisions
- More effective collaboration

## The Importance of UX Empowerment

### UX as an Afterthought

In some organizations, UX is treated as a **final step in the process**.

Characteristics of this approach:

- UX asked only for superficial feedback
- Designers restricted to small interface changes
- UX teams discouraged from exploring broader design improvements

Example:

- UX asked to confirm whether a button color is correct.

Consequences:

- UX teams cannot contribute meaningful insights
- Innovation and design quality suffer.

### UX Informing Business Processes

High-performing teams empower UX to influence:

- Product design
- Business processes
- Feature priorities

Requirements for success:

- UX teams must understand the **purpose and goals of the product**
- Product managers must clearly communicate product objectives.

Responsibility balance:

- Product managers explain the product vision.
- UX teams translate that vision into usable experiences.

## Daily UX Contribution in Agile Teams

Agile development enables UX to contribute **continuously**.

UX activities occur:

- Daily during product development
- Throughout feature design
- During iterative product improvements

This continuous involvement ensures that:

- UX informs both **business decisions and design implementation**.

## The Culture of Validation

### UX and User Empathy

UX teams bring a **culture of validation** to product development.

Core contributions include:

- Understanding user perspectives
- Conducting research with real users
- Validating product ideas with evidence.

UX research helps teams:

- Avoid assumptions about users
- Understand real user goals.

### Risks of Assumption-Based Product Management

Product managers sometimes believe they understand users because:

- They use similar products
- They have market experience
- They have previously studied users

However, this confidence can lead to incorrect assumptions.

Example analogy:

- Driving a car does not mean you know how to design one.

Users and product managers experience products differently.

## Understanding User Goals

### Designing for User Goals

Effective UX focuses on identifying:

- What users want to achieve
- The simplest way to achieve those goals

Example:

Netflix users want to **watch movies easily**.

The delivery method may change:

- Physical DVDs by mail
- Streaming over the Internet

However, the **user goal remains the same**.

Design implication:

- Focus on enabling the user’s goal.
- Delivery mechanisms can evolve.

## Characteristics of High-Performing UX Teams

High-performing agile teams combine UX insights with continuous product development.

These teams stay informed through:

- Direct interaction with users
- Competitive analysis
- Persona development

UX insights help teams understand:

- User goals
- User behaviors
- Market expectations

Result:

- Better-informed product decisions
- Continuous improvement of the user experience.
