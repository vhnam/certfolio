## Quantitative vs Qualitative Measurement

### Balancing Metrics

- Quantitative metrics: Hard numbers such as conversion rates or KPI targets.
- Qualitative metrics: Indicators of real value such as:
  - User retention (stickiness)
  - User satisfaction and emotion
  - Long-term engagement
- Key insight: Quantitative success alone can be misleading if it ignores user quality and experience.

### Misleading Metrics Example

- Artificial growth: Incentives (e.g., discounts) can inflate user numbers.
- Problem:
  - Users may not return (low retention)
  - High acquisition cost with low long-term value
- Lesson:
  - Short-term gains ≠ sustainable success

## Designing for Long-Term Engagement

### Building User Habits

- Strategy:
  - Encourage repeated use over time
  - Gradually build trust and familiarity
- Example approach:
  - Smaller incentives spread across multiple interactions
  - Reinforces behavior and habit formation

### Measuring True Value

- Focus on:
  - Frequency of use
  - Consistency of positive experiences
  - Integration into user routines

## Business Viability & UX Decisions

### Unsustainable UX Models

- Example:
  - Services that delight users but are economically unscalable
- Problem:
  - UX decisions ignore operational and business realities
  - Can harm providers or system sustainability

### End-to-End Experience Awareness

- UX must consider:
  - Users
  - Business
  - Service providers (e.g., delivery workers)
- Key concept:
  - Last-mile usability: The full journey, including delivery and execution

## Advanced UX Research & Measurement

### Beyond Usability Testing

- Usability testing:
  - Short-term, task-based evaluation
- Limitations:
  - Misses long-term behavior and emotional impact

### Longitudinal Research Methods

- Diary study: Observing user behavior over time (e.g., days or weeks)
- Benefits:
  - Captures real-world usage
  - Reveals habits and emotional patterns

### Measuring Emotion

- Emotional metrics:
  - User frustration, joy, satisfaction
- Methods:
  - Biofeedback (e.g., heart rate, sweat response)
  - Behavioral signals (tone, repetition, reactions)
- Insight:
  - Emotion is measurable and critical to UX success

## Learning from User Behavior

### Observing Real Usage

- Methods:
  - Watching user sessions
  - Reading feedback and forums
  - Analyzing complaints and frustrations
- Key insight:
  - Negative reactions provide high-value insights

### Behavioral Data Sources

- Search queries:
  - Reveal user intent and mental models
- Forums & feedback:
  - Provide large-scale, real-world insights
- Application:
  - Improve information architecture
  - Create relevant content (FAQs, features)

### Virtual Ethnography

- Definition: Studying user behavior through digital footprints (e.g., reviews, comments).
- Use cases:
  - Identifying unmet needs
  - Discovering product opportunities

## UX in Agile Teams

### Shared Responsibility for UX

- High-performing teams:
  - Product owner
  - UX researcher
  - Developers
- All team members:
  - Engage with user feedback
  - Observe user behavior

### Indirect User Contact

- Not always direct interaction:
  - Watching recordings
  - Reading forums
  - Analyzing feedback
- Goal:
  - Build shared understanding of users across the team

### Consequences of Ignoring Users

- Risks:
  - Poor design decisions
  - Misaligned product architecture
  - Reduced product success
- Principle:
  - Continuous exposure to user feedback is essential for good UX decisions
