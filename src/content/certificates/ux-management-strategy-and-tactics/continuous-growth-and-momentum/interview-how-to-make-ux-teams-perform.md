## What Good UX Performance Looks Like

### Continuous UX Cycle

- UX operates as a **continuous loop**:
  - Research → Design → Validation → Iteration
- Key indicator:
  - **All decisions are research-based and validated**
- Outcome:
  - Reduced guesswork
  - Higher confidence in decisions

### UX Recognition & Enablement

- UX teams are:
  - Trusted
  - Empowered to do their work
- Signs of maturity:
  - UX is not blocked or sidelined
  - Teams are allowed to apply proper methods

### Strategic Placement

- UX exists at **senior management level**
- Indicators:
  - UX leaders in org chart
  - Influence at strategic decision level

## From “Good Enough” to Excellent UX

### The Danger of “Good Enough”

- Many companies deliver:
  - Acceptable but flawed experiences
- Root causes:
  - Lack of deep research
  - Incomplete journey understanding
  - Weak process

### Real-World Insight

- Companies may:
  - Have UX teams
  - Still fail due to:
    - Poor visibility
    - Inconsistent research
    - Lack of global perspective

### Key Gap

- Difference between:
  - **Doing UX**
  - **Doing UX thoroughly and excellently**

## Common Failure Patterns in UX

### Politics & Misuse of Data

- Risks:
  - Cherry-picking research insights
  - Manipulating user feedback to support agendas
- Impact:
  - Misleading decisions
  - Loss of integrity in UX process

### “Resume-Driven Design”

- Designers protect their work:
  - Avoid testing
  - Resist feedback
- Motivation:
  - Portfolio building
  - Ego
- Result:
  - Poor product outcomes

### Lack of Experience & Process

- Overconfidence without depth:
  - Shallow understanding of UX methods
- Problem:
  - Using wrong methods for wrong problems
- Insight:
  - UX maturity depends on **method selection and application**

## Importance of UX Process Maturity

### Right Method, Right Problem

- Examples:
  - Card sorting ≠ solution for all problems
  - Diary studies underused but valuable
- Key principle:
  - Match **method to context**

### Avoiding Mediocrity

- Even UX teams can:
  - Produce low-quality work
- Cause:
  - Weak or inconsistent processes

## Organizational Foundations for UX Excellence

### Executive-Level UX Leadership

- Critical requirement:
  - UX represented at top level (e.g., executive role)
- Benefits:
  - Influence strategy
  - Gain organizational respect
- Reality:
  - Only ~20% of organizations achieve this

### Organizational Placement Matters

- Risks:
  - UX under dev/product → biased decisions
  - UX under QA → severe limitation
- Ideal:
  - Independent but **collaborative and distributed**

### Distributed UX Model

- UX should be:
  - Embedded across teams
  - Present in all departments
- Avoid:
  - Isolated UX teams

## Enabling UX Teams to Succeed

### Strong Support System

- Requirements:
  - Leadership support
  - UX culture
  - Open access to users

### Funding & Resources

- Essential:
  - Budget for research
- Without funding:
  - No real user research
  - Fake personas and assumptions

### Training & Growth

- Invest in:
  - Continuous learning
  - Team development
- Risk:
  - “Team of one” stagnation

## Measuring UX Performance

### Continuous Measurement

- Track:
  - UX effectiveness
  - Outcomes and improvements
- Use:
  - Data to refine and adjust

### ROI as Feedback Loop

- Measure:
  - What works / doesn’t work
- Benefits:
  - Justify investment
  - Unlock more funding

## Key Principles

### Research-Driven Decisions

- All UX decisions should be:
  - Evidence-based
  - Validated

### Process Over Output

- Strong UX = strong process
- Weak process = inconsistent results

### Culture Over Tools

- UX success depends on:
  - Organizational mindset
  - Not just methods or deliverables

### Excellence Requires Structure

- High-performing UX teams need:
  - Leadership
  - Funding
  - Process
  - Measurement
