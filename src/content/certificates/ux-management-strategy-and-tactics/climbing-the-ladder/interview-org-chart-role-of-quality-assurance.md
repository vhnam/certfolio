---
title: "Interview: Org Chart – Role of Quality Assurance"
---

In many organizations, UX is mistakenly associated with quality assurance (QA) because both relate to improving product quality. However, UX and QA serve **fundamentally different purposes**. While QA focuses on verifying that software functions correctly, UX focuses on ensuring that the product meets **user needs, expectations, and behaviors**.

## The Difference Between UX and QA

Although both UX and QA contribute to product quality, they operate at different levels.

**Quality Assurance (QA)** focuses on:

- Detecting software bugs
- Verifying functionality
- Ensuring features work as intended
- Testing code and system stability

**User Experience (UX)** focuses on:

- Understanding user needs and expectations
- Designing intuitive interfaces
- Evaluating usability and interaction behavior
- Ensuring the product solves real user problems

In short, QA asks **“Does the product work correctly?”**, while UX asks **“Is this the right product and interface for users?”**

## Why UX Should Not Be Managed by QA

Some organizations place UX within the QA department because both relate to “quality.” However, this structure often limits the impact of UX.

When UX is embedded in QA:

- UX designers may be treated as **bug checkers rather than design experts**
- UX insights may be introduced **too late in the development process**
- UX teams lose influence over **product direction and design decisions**

This reduces UX to a validation step rather than allowing it to shape product strategy.

## UX Designers Play an Ongoing Role During Development

Instead of waiting until QA testing begins, UX designers should be involved **continuously during development**.

Effective practices include:

- Frequent check-ins between designers and developers
- Quick UI walkthroughs during development
- Early feedback on layout, interaction behavior, and micro-interactions

These reviews often take only a few minutes but help catch issues early, when developers can easily fix them.

Examples of UX checks include:

- Alignment and spacing of interface elements
- Hover states and interaction behaviors
- Timing of animations or delays
- Consistency with design prototypes

Catching these details early prevents costly corrections later in the development cycle.

## Usability Testing vs. Software Testing

Another source of confusion comes from the difference between **usability testing** and **software testing**.

**Software testing (QA)**

- Tests functionality and system reliability
- Uses scripted test cases
- Ensures the system works as specified

**Usability testing (UX)**

- Observes real users interacting with the product
- Evaluates usability, expectations, and understanding
- Identifies behavioral problems in the interface

Even experienced QA testers cannot fully substitute for real users because their familiarity with the system makes them **“power users”** rather than representative users.

## The Importance of Testing with Real Users

UX research focuses on observing **actual users performing real tasks**. This provides insights that internal teams cannot replicate.

Real user testing reveals:

- Misaligned expectations
- Confusing workflows
- Hidden usability problems
- Opportunities to improve the overall experience

Testing with real users ensures the product is not only functional but also **usable and meaningful for its intended audience**.

## UX and QA Serve Complementary Roles

Although UX and QA should remain separate disciplines, they both contribute to product success.

Their relationship can be summarized as:

- **UX** ensures the product is designed correctly for users.
- **QA** ensures the product works correctly from a technical perspective.

When both disciplines collaborate while maintaining their distinct roles, organizations can deliver products that are **both functional and user-centered**.
