---
title: Interview – Qualitative Research Discussion
---

Qualitative research plays a central role in developing UX maturity because it helps organizations understand users deeply. Through interviews, field studies, and observation, teams gain insight into real user behavior, which builds empathy and informs better design decisions. Despite its value, many organizations struggle to adopt qualitative research due to misconceptions about its cost, relevance, and timing.

## What Qualitative Research Is

Qualitative research focuses on **understanding meaning, behavior, and context**. It helps organizations learn:

- How people actually perform tasks
- How users perceive products and services
- How individuals collaborate and solve problems in real situations

Its main purpose is to **build empathy and insight**. Rather than producing statistical conclusions, qualitative research explains **why users behave the way they do**.

While quantitative research measures patterns through numbers, qualitative research investigates the **human factors behind those numbers**.

## The Relationship Between Qualitative and Quantitative Research

Many organizations assume quantitative research is more legitimate because it produces measurable results. However, both approaches serve different purposes.

- **Quantitative research** helps measure patterns and scale findings.
- **Qualitative research** helps explain the reasons behind those patterns.

Effective UX research often combines the two approaches. Quantitative data identifies trends, while qualitative research explores the **underlying motivations and behaviors** that produce those trends.

## Why Organizations Often Resist Qualitative Research

Organizations frequently hesitate to adopt qualitative research for several reasons.

### Perception of Time and Cost

Managers often associate research with:

- Long timelines
- High costs
- Delays in product development

Because of this perception, research can appear to slow down innovation rather than support it.

### Fear of Disrupting Product Plans

Some stakeholders worry that research findings might:

- Challenge existing ideas
- Reveal flaws in the product roadmap

This uncertainty can make teams reluctant to engage in early user research.

### Belief That Users Cannot Inform Innovation

Another common belief is that users cannot provide useful insight for products that do not yet exist. Teams may assume that feedback is only useful once a tangible prototype or product is available.

However, UX research shows that observing **current behaviors, tools, and workflows** provides crucial insight for designing innovative solutions.

## The Importance of Field Studies

Field studies are particularly valuable because they allow researchers to observe users in their **real environments**.

By immersing themselves in users’ contexts, UX teams can understand:

- Existing workflows and habits
- Workarounds and improvised solutions
- Collaboration patterns
- Cultural and organizational influences

These observations provide the **foundation for designing meaningful improvements**, especially when introducing disruptive or innovative products.

## Shifting from Inside-Out to Outside-In Thinking

Organizations often approach product design from an **inside-out perspective**, focusing on internal ideas and assumptions.

Qualitative research encourages an **outside-in approach**, where teams begin with a deep understanding of users’ lives and contexts before designing solutions.

This shift helps organizations:

- Challenge internal assumptions
- Design solutions that align with real user needs
- Create innovations that fit naturally into users’ environments

## Starting Small with Qualitative Research

Organizations new to qualitative research do not need to launch large studies immediately.

A practical starting approach includes:

- Interviewing **three or four users**
- Spending **one or two hours with each participant**
- Conducting the research over **a single day or two**

Even small studies can reveal powerful insights that demonstrate the value of research.

Starting small helps organizations see that qualitative research does **not have to be expensive or time-consuming**.

## Making Research Inclusive

Qualitative research becomes even more impactful when it involves multiple stakeholders.

Inviting team members to participate in research sessions—such as:

- Product managers
- Developers
- Designers

can create powerful **“aha” moments**.

However, participants should be prepared to observe rather than dominate the conversation. The goal of research is to **listen and learn from users**, not to judge or instruct them.

When stakeholders experience user research directly, they often gain a deeper appreciation of user needs and become stronger advocates for UX practices.

## Building Empathy Through User Contact

Direct interaction with users is one of the most effective ways to build empathy within an organization.

When team members meet users and observe their challenges firsthand, they often realize:

- The gap between internal assumptions and real user behavior
- The complexity of users’ environments
- The importance of designing solutions grounded in real contexts

These experiences can transform how teams approach product development and strengthen the organization’s commitment to user-centered design.
