---
title: The 5 Tactical Keys to Scaling Your Org
---

Improving UX maturity requires deliberate organizational change. To help organizations move toward stronger UX capability, five practical tactics can be applied. These tactics help teams progress from early UX awareness to stages where UX becomes embedded in strategy and organizational culture.

## Assess Your UX Maturity

The first step is understanding **where the organization currently stands on the UX maturity spectrum**.

Typical maturity stages include:

1. **Interested** – UX is recognized as important but receives little funding or structural support.
2. **Invested** – UX becomes more important and formalized programs begin to appear.
3. **Committed** – UX becomes critical and executives actively support it.
4. **Engaged** – UX is integrated into core business strategy.
5. **Embedded** – UX becomes part of the organization’s DNA and is no longer treated as a separate activity.

However, assessment should not only come from the UX team. It is equally important to understand **how the rest of the organization perceives UX maturity**. Different teams may have different views of UX’s importance and effectiveness.

A second perspective is the **UX maturity lifecycle**, which shows how organizations evolve over time:

- **Kick-off** – UX resources or consultants are introduced.
- **Skunkworks** – UX work happens ad hoc with limited resources.
- **Align** – UX activities begin to gain validation and positioning.
- **Peak** – UX teams consistently deliver measurable ROI.
- **Culture** – UX becomes systematic and widely adopted.
- **User-Centered Enterprise** – UX maturity is fully achieved and supported by metrics and continuous improvement.

Organizations may also encounter common traps along the way, such as:

- **“Wheel Spinning” trap** – isolated UX efforts, poor results, and lack of funding.
- **“Slash and Burn” trap** – over-stretched teams, poorly managed resources, and UX practices misaligned with Agile development.

The goal is to move organizations toward **committed and engaged stages**, where UX becomes a reliable contributor to product success.

## Identify Bright Spots

Rather than focusing only on problems, organizations should look for **areas where UX-related practices are already working well**.

Key questions include:

- What is already working in the organization?
- What aspects of UX does the organization already understand or support?
- How can UX build upon these strengths?
- Which roles outside UX might already contribute to customer-centric thinking?

Examples of bright spots include:

- Teams already using **wireframes or prototypes** in product development.
- Sales teams collecting **customer feedback during client conversations**.
- Developers who quickly build **proof-of-concept prototypes**.
- Marketing teams that regularly gather **customer insights**.

These existing practices can become **entry points for expanding UX activities**, such as adding usability testing or field studies to an established design workflow.

Building on what already works is often more effective than introducing completely new processes.

## Develop a UX Roadmap

Improving UX maturity requires **a clear roadmap for organizational change**.

A typical UX program may evolve through several phases:

### Phase 1 – Process Beta (≈ 3–5 months)

Initial UX practices begin to take shape.

Activities often include:

- Training teams in **User-Centered Design (UCD)**
- Coaching teams on **Agile UX integration**
- Running a **pilot project** to demonstrate value

### Phase 2 – Staffed UX Effort (≈ 6–24 months)

UX begins to scale within the organization.

Typical developments include:

- Ongoing UX skill development
- Hiring senior UX specialists
- Launching multiple UX projects
- Starting regular user testing

### Phase 3 – UX Program (≈ 23–48 months)

UX becomes a structured organizational capability.

Key characteristics:

- A formal UX program
- Regular user testing and field studies
- Dedicated UX leadership and management

Over time, this roadmap helps transform UX from isolated activities into **an integrated organizational capability**.

## Identify UX Sponsors

UX initiatives require **strong internal advocates**.

Successful UX transformation typically involves sponsors at three levels:

### Executive Level

Senior leaders must:

- Champion UX strategically
- Defend UX initiatives when resources are challenged
- Advocate for UX across the organization

### Middle Management

Middle managers are crucial because they:

- Translate strategy into day-to-day execution
- Integrate UX into team workflows
- Provide practical support for UX teams

However, effective support must go beyond **“lip service passion.”** Managers must demonstrate real commitment through decisions and actions.

### Grassroots Level

Support at the operational level is equally important.

Examples include:

- Developers who collaborate closely with UX designers
- Engineers who build quick prototypes or proof-of-concepts
- Product managers who incorporate user insights into product decisions

Together, these levels create a **network of internal champions** that protects and strengthens UX efforts.

## Get Comfortable with Qualitative Research Quickly

At the heart of UX is **understanding user behavior**. Most UX insights come from **qualitative research methods**.

Key principles include:

- UX research focuses on **how and why users behave**, not just numerical metrics.
- The rules for **qualitative and quantitative research differ**.
- Research quality is not determined solely by large sample sizes.

Typical research sample sizes include:

- **Field studies:** approximately 15–40 participants
- **Usability testing:** typically 5–15 participants
- **Segmented testing:** about 5 users per segment

Qualitative insights can also be converted into quantitative metrics.

Example:

- If 8 out of 10 users fail a task, the usability failure rate becomes **80%**.

## Ground UX Deliverables in Real Research

Many UX artifacts should be rooted in field research.

### Personas

Personas should be built from **real field study insights**, not internal assumptions. Using verbatim user observations and narratives helps create more accurate representations of users.

### User Stories

User stories should also be grounded in **real scenarios discovered during research**.

Instead of writing generic stories such as:

> “As a user, I want to perform task X…”

UX teams should base them on **observed behaviors and real user contexts**.

## Start Research with Credibility

If an organization is new to UX research, it may be beneficial to involve **experienced researchers or consultants** in early research initiatives.

External expertise can help:

- Establish correct research methodologies
- Build organizational trust in UX findings
- Train internal teams for long-term capability

This ensures the organization begins its UX journey with **credible and reliable insights**.
