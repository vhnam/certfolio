## Planning UX Strategy

### Defining the Direction of UX

- UX Roadmapping: The process of understanding the current UX state of an organization and planning how UX should evolve over time.
- Planning, Scaling, Growing: The three key stages for developing and expanding UX strategy across the organization.

UX strategy must operate at multiple organizational layers:

- Organization level: Company-wide UX vision and leadership support.
- Line of business level: UX integrated into business units or product divisions.
- Team level: UX practices embedded within product teams.
- Product/UI level: The user interface where UX strategy becomes visible.

Different layers may have different UX maturity levels.

### Key Planning Questions

When defining a UX roadmap, organizations should ask:

- Where do we need to get to?
  - Organization-wide
  - Line of business
  - Team level

- What is driving the need for UX?
- What business objectives does UX support?
- What outcomes do we expect UX to deliver?

UX initiatives should always be grounded in business objectives rather than purely design improvements.

### Learning From Existing UX Efforts

Organizations should examine previous UX initiatives to understand:

- What worked well
- What failed
- What techniques were used
- What business outcomes were achieved

A common problem occurs when UX work is limited to UI-level improvements performed by consultants or contractors. Strategic UX should influence processes and decision-making across the organization—not just interface design.

## Scaling UX Across the Organization

### Leveraging Existing Resources

Scaling UX begins by identifying resources already present in the organization.

Hidden UX contributors may include:

- Developers creating wireframes
- Designers applying Lean UX practices
- Product managers performing informal research
- Team members experimenting with usability practices

These individuals can become internal UX advocates and help expand UX practices.

### Identifying Required UX Skills

Organizations must evaluate skill gaps within their teams.

Important UX capabilities include:

- Wireframing
- Field studies
- User testing
- Customer research
- UX leadership
- UX management

Managers themselves may also require training to effectively lead UX teams.

Because UX management is still a relatively young discipline, resources and training programs remain limited compared to other management fields.

### Choosing UX Techniques That Fit the Product

UX methods should be selected based on organizational context.

Considerations include:

- Product type
- Industry
- User needs
- Development process
- Business objectives

Examples:

- Lean UX may not fit every product environment.
- Service design may not be appropriate for certain B2B products.

Organizations should adopt UX techniques strategically rather than copying popular methods blindly.

## Avoiding Tactical UX

### Strategic UX vs Tactical UX

A common failure pattern is focusing only on tactical UX.

Tactical UX includes:

- Small UI improvements
- Minor usability fixes
- Isolated team-level UX activities

Strategic UX requires broader impact:

- Across teams
- Across departments
- Across the entire organization

UX management must ensure UX influences decision-making beyond interface design.

## Measuring UX Impact

### Demonstrating UX Value

UX strategy must demonstrate measurable outcomes.

Effective ways to show UX value include:

- ROI case studies
- Improved user performance metrics
- Reduced support costs
- Increased conversion or retention

UX improvements must be connected to business performance to gain long-term organizational support.

## Identifying Barriers to UX Growth

Organizations should identify factors blocking UX progress.

Common barriers include:

- Lack of time
- Rigid development processes
- Organizational resistance
- Misunderstanding of UX roles
- Developers acting as informal UX owners
- Cultural differences within distributed teams

Example issues include developers refusing to change features despite usability findings or teams misunderstanding the role of UX professionals.

Distributed teams may require:

- Stronger communication channels
- More face-to-face interactions
- Cultural awareness within the organization

## Evaluating UX Process Effectiveness

Organizations should regularly evaluate whether their UX practices are:

- Repeatable: UX processes can be consistently executed.
- Scalable: UX practices can expand across teams and products.
- Valuable: UX activities produce measurable business value.

UX changes that are not tied to business goals provide limited strategic impact.

## UX Maturity Scorecard

### Assessing UX Maturity

A UX maturity scorecard is a practical tool for evaluating the current UX capability of an organization.

Typical evaluation attributes include:

- Conducting regular usability activities
- Team understanding of usability and UX
- Willingness to apply usability findings
- Conducting customer research
- Readiness for process change
- Leveraging usability best practices in design

Each attribute can be scored (for example, from **1 to 5**) to measure maturity.

### Example UX Maturity Assessment

Example scorecard results:

- Conducts regular usability activities: **1**
  - No consistent usability testing; past efforts were sporadic.

- Team understanding of usability and UX: **2**
  - Lack of consensus about UX deliverables.

- Willingness to apply usability findings: **3**
  - Teams show interest but are unclear about UX value.

- Conducts customer research: **1**
  - Research is infrequent and mostly market-driven.

- Readiness for process change: **5**
  - Organization recently adopted Agile and is open to change.

- Leverages usability best practices in design: **1**
  - UI style guides and UX practices are not trusted or widely used.

Overall maturity score example:

- Total UX maturity: **43%**

### Purpose of the Scorecard

The goal of UX maturity scoring is **not to criticize teams**, but to reveal improvement opportunities.

Scorecards should be used to:

- Identify weak areas in UX processes
- Prioritize improvements
- Guide UX strategy development
- Support long-term UX roadmap planning

The real value of maturity assessments lies in highlighting where organizations need support, resources, and process improvements rather than assigning judgment.
