## Starting a UX Team in an Organization

### Common Hiring Approach

When organizations begin building a UX team, they often start by hiring:

- A junior UX designer
- A recent graduate
- A bootcamp-trained UX professional

This approach feels less risky to organizations that are new to UX.

However, this strategy can create problems because junior designers may lack:

- Experience working in large organizations
- Confidence navigating cross-functional teams
- Political skills needed to advocate for UX

Without an established design culture, junior designers can struggle to influence decision-making.

## Challenges of Being the First UX Hire

### Difficulties for Junior Designers

Junior UX professionals entering an organization as the only UX resource may face several challenges:

- Lack of mentorship or guidance
- Limited support network
- Pressure to handle strategic and tactical work simultaneously

Many candidates prefer organizations with established UX teams where they can learn from experienced professionals.

### Challenges for Senior Designers

Even experienced UX professionals can face difficulties when joining an organization with no UX structure.

Common challenges include:

- Being expected to immediately produce designs
- Lack of time to conduct proper user research
- No existing UX infrastructure or assets

Examples of missing assets include:

- Design systems
- Pattern libraries
- Usability testing frameworks
- UX documentation

Without these resources, building a mature UX practice becomes difficult.

## The “Team of One” Problem

### Pros and Cons of a Solo UX Role

A “UX team of one” can be effective if the individual is highly skilled and experienced.

Possible advantages include:

- Direct ownership of UX decisions
- Ability to influence strategy

However, major disadvantages include:

- Lack of support and collaboration
- Heavy workload across strategic and tactical tasks
- Difficulty building UX systems alone

Scaling UX requires eventually expanding beyond a single UX professional.

## Setting Direction Through User Research

### Starting with Field Research

A key recommendation for building UX maturity is to conduct a **field study early in the process**.

- Field study: Observing and interviewing users in their real work environment.

Benefits include:

- Understanding how users interact with systems
- Identifying broader design problems
- Establishing a strategic direction for UX work

Instead of fixing small interface issues, field research helps teams understand the **bigger picture of user behavior and system design**.

### Avoiding “Low-Hanging Fruit” UX

Organizations often focus on quick UX improvements such as:

- Visual design tweaks
- Small interface changes
- Cosmetic UI fixes

While these changes may appear efficient, they often ignore deeper usability issues.

Strategic research helps teams address root problems rather than temporary fixes.

## Conducting Agile User Research

### Fast and Practical Field Studies

Field research does not need to be slow or expensive.

Effective studies can be conducted:

- Within a few days
- With a small number of participants
- Through contextual observation and interviews

This approach provides valuable insights quickly while fitting within agile development timelines.

### Understanding User Context

UX research should explore:

- Users’ working environments
- Tools they use
- Workflows and collaboration patterns
- Other systems they interact with

Understanding the full context of use leads to better product decisions.

## Using External UX Consultants

### Supporting a Team of One

External UX consulting agencies can support organizations during early UX adoption.

Consultants can help with:

- Conducting initial field research
- Developing UX strategy
- Introducing UX methods
- Training internal teams

This support is especially valuable for organizations with a single UX practitioner.

### Building Organizational Credibility

Consultants can also provide:

- Credibility for UX initiatives
- External validation of UX practices
- Influence for internal UX advocates

This can help organizations accept and adopt UX methods more effectively.

## Hiring Strategy for Scaling UX Teams

### Starting With a UX Director

Instead of hiring junior designers first, organizations may benefit from hiring a **UX director-level leader**.

Advantages include:

- Strong strategic leadership
- Ability to influence stakeholders
- Experience navigating organizational politics

A senior leader can establish the foundation for a mature UX practice.

### Hybrid Leadership Role

An effective UX director should be both:

- A manager responsible for building the team
- An individual contributor capable of performing UX work

Early responsibilities may include:

- Conducting research
- Creating wireframes
- Prototyping
- Hiring and mentoring new team members

This hybrid role allows the organization to build UX capabilities while establishing leadership for future team growth.
